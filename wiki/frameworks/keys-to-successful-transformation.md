# Keys to Successful Transformation — Cagan's Ten

> Cagan and [[lea-hickman|Lea Hickman]] studied the small subset of companies that actually transformed successfully — and distilled the ten things that made the difference. The list appears in Ch 48 of *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* as the book's conclusion. This framework page is the wiki's canonical enumeration + coaching artifact — the checklist against which to sequence a transformation and score readiness.

## When to use it
- **Diagnostically** — pair with [[transformation-readiness]] (readiness *before* starting) and [[model-maturity]] (current state) to identify the specific keys currently missing.
- **Prescriptively** — as the sequencing outline for a transformation program: which keys need to be in place before which others become effective.
- **When executives ask "what does successful transformation actually require?"** — Cagan's Ch 48 is his short-form answer. This page is that answer in one place, cross-linked to the depth pages.
- **When coaching an ambivalent leader** — Keys #1 (CEO) and #10 (Corporate Courage) explicitly name what most transformations lack from the top.

## The premise
- Successful transformations are **rare**. Cagan's line elsewhere: *"the overwhelming majority of companies globally that have attempted to transform have failed."* The ten keys are what SVPG found across the ones that succeeded.
- *"Everything we have described is meant to increase your chances of success, but it's also true that not everything is equally critical."* Ch 48 is Cagan's ranked essential list.

## The ten keys

### 1. The Role of the CEO
- **Extremely difficult to transform without the active support of the CEO.** The rest of the list makes this obvious.
- **The naming anti-pattern:** designating "some leader responsible for digital transformation" is *not* what's meant. This *"common mistake makes it all too easy for the rest of the company to continue business as usual."*
- **Scope beyond product.** *"It's not unusual to find that you also need fundamental changes to finance, human resources, sales, marketing, customer success, business operations, and more, and occasionally the CEO will need to get involved."* The CEO occasionally needs to be the one who intervenes.
- **The CEO's role:** *"chief evangelist of the product model."* See also [[transformation-politics|internal-evangelism]] · [[product-leadership]].

### 2. The Role of Technology
- **The mindset shift.** *"A successful transformation changes the role of technology from a necessary cost to the core enabler of the business."*
- **What it touches:** how technology is funded, how teams are staffed, whether technology is considered a core competency or something outsourced.
- Corresponds to the wiki's existing **cost-center vs profit-center** framing (on [[product-leadership]] and [[transformation]]). See also [[overcoming-objections|Ch 44 CIO objections]] where this shift is worked at the CIO's own level.

### 3. Strong Product Leaders
- Everything depends on them once senior leadership support is secured.
- **These are the leaders of product management, product design, and engineering.** *"The importance of this can't be stressed enough. These are the people who are responsible and accountable for everything that follows."*
- **If the leaders aren't experienced in the product model, product coaches are the answer** (aligned with [[product-coaching]]'s coaching-supply prescription).
- Deep reference: [[product-leadership]].

### 4. True Product Managers
- Empowered teams depend on competent PMs. *"For most companies that are trying to transform, even if they currently have people with this title, this is a new competency."*
- The title *"can be very misleading"* — feature-team PMs and true-team PMs have very different demands.
- Experienced product leaders determine who has PM potential vs who's better suited to another role — via coaching + training.
- **Two lines worth remembering when coaching senior leaders:**
  - *"Each product manager is a potential future leader of the company."*
  - *"Product leaders will be judged based on their weakest product manager."* (Also appears in [[transformation-politics]]; now also grounded here.)
- Deep reference: [[product-manager]] · [[pm-competency]] · [[coaching-the-pm]].

### 5. Professional Product Designers
- The product model *"puts users and customers much closer to the product teams and to the company"* — designers craft the customer experience.
- *"This is why we elevate design from a supporting role to a central role, alongside product managers and engineers."*
- Deep reference: [[product-designer]] · [[the-product-team-trio]].

### 6. Empowered Engineers
- *"Empowered engineers form the engine that fuels consistent innovation. Much of effective transformation is all about enabling and encouraging truly empowered engineers."*
- **The outsourcing test:** *"Just in case it's not blatantly obvious, you won't have empowered engineers if you outsource your engineers."*
- **The stance:** *"No one is suggesting that you put your engineers on a pedestal, but you need to get them out of the proverbial basement and put them front and center on your product teams and in coming up with solutions to the hardest problems you face."*
- Deep reference: [[empowered-engineers]] · [[engineers]] · [[missionaries-vs-mercenaries]].

### 7. Insights-Based Product Strategy
- **Purpose of product strategy:** *"determine the most important problems that need to be solved for the company to achieve its objectives."*
- **The starting-state problem:** *"Most companies that have yet to transform have never created a product strategy before. That's because their strategy was simply to serve as many of the business stakeholders as they could. And, of course, that's not a strategy at all."*
- **Effective strategy is based on both quantitative and qualitative insights.** Owned by product leaders — *"yet another reason why it's so important to have experienced and strong product leaders."*
- Deep reference: [[product-strategy]] · [[product-strategy-quality]] · [[focus]] · [[powered-by-insights]].

### 8. Stakeholder Collaboration
- **The core cultural shift:** *"transformation involves moving from a model where the technology teams exist to serve the business to one where they exist to serve the customers, in ways that work for the business… moving from a subservient model to a collaborative model."*
- **Most stakeholders are frustrated with the old way** and willing to try — but some will take the loss of control personally.
- **The sequencing rule:** *"It's essential that product leaders do not try to push this change until and unless product teams have competent product managers who are ready to step up and do what they need to."* Then senior leadership may need to actively support stakeholders through the change.
- *"It's important not to sugarcoat this, as the change is profound."*
- Deep reference: [[stakeholder-relationship-health]] · [[cross-functional-partnering]] · [[transformation-politics]] · [[overcoming-objections]].

### 9. Continuous Evangelization of Outcomes
- Product leaders evangelize the product vision, product strategy, the move to outcomes, and the broader transformation.
- **What each is for:** vision inspires and motivates the whole org; strategy makes transparent *"the rationale and the evidence for what will get done and what won't."*
- **Ongoing learning:** product teams learn every week; share those learnings honestly and openly so the broader company understands where innovations come from and *"what it means to truly deliver business results versus just shipping features and projects."*
- Deep reference: [[product-vision]] · [[strategic-context]] · [[transformation-politics|internal-evangelism dimension]] · [[the-point-is-outcomes]].

### 10. Corporate Courage
- *"Every case of successful transformation we know of required true courage from executives and other senior leaders."*
- **The leap:** *"Moving to a fundamentally different model — even when the current model is broken — requires a real leap of faith, and it takes courage to make the leap."*
- **The recognition gap:** senior leaders who transform *"don't get the recognition for this courage they deserve (although the stock market has rewarded them very well)."*
- **The counter-observation:** *"We know many more senior leaders who don't have the courage and aren't willing to do what it takes to help their company not just survive, but thrive."*
- The wiki treats this as its own concept — see [[corporate-courage]].

## The dependency structure (wiki synthesis)
The ten keys are ordered by Cagan but also form a rough dependency graph. Reading the list in Ch 48:
- **Key #1 (CEO) is a prerequisite for everything else.** Without it, the "digital transformation VP" anti-pattern kills the effort.
- **Key #2 (Role of Technology) is a mindset gate.** Without the cost-center → profit-center shift, funding + staffing + core-competency questions can't be answered correctly.
- **Key #3 (Strong Product Leaders) unlocks Keys #4–7.** Product leaders are the ones who develop the PMs (#4), staff the designers (#5), coach the tech leads for empowerment (#6), and author the strategy (#7).
- **Key #7 (Insights-Based Strategy) unlocks Key #8.** Without a strategy, there's nothing to *collaborate* with stakeholders on; the relationship stays subservient.
- **Key #8 (Stakeholder Collaboration) requires Key #4 (True PMs) in place.** Cagan's explicit sequencing note above.
- **Key #9 (Evangelization) is the ongoing operating discipline** once the earlier keys are in place.
- **Key #10 (Corporate Courage) sits alongside Key #1** — it's the leadership disposition without which the CEO commitment can be nominal only.

## Inputs & outputs
**Input.** The current-state map — usually from [[model-maturity]] (whole-org rollup) and [[transformation-readiness]] (preconditions for starting). The book's own diagnostic instrument is [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] **Ch 29 (Transformation Assessment)** — a self-conducted assessment in ~a few days by looking at *how the org actually builds, solves, and decides*. See [[transformation-readiness]] and [[model-maturity]] where the Ch 29 instrument is captured.

**Output.** A prioritized list of which of the ten keys are strong, weak, or missing — plus the interventions that would move the weakest ones. The natural next artifacts are: a strategic-context document ([[strategic-context]]), a pilot-team plan ([[pilot-teams]]), and a monthly-outcomes drumbeat plan ([[transformation-politics|internal-evangelism]]).

## Pitfalls
- **Treating this list as a checklist rather than a sequence.** Marking keys "done" without recognizing which unlock others is the surest way to declare victory prematurely (the [[model-maturity|Level-2 checkbox transformation]] failure).
- **Skipping Key #10 (Corporate Courage) as "soft."** Cagan is explicit: it's the difference between the leaders who transform and those who don't. Naming it explicitly in coaching lets a leader recognize whether *they* have it.
- **Confusing Key #1 (CEO active support) with executive sponsorship theater.** Sponsorship = a name on a document. Active support = the CEO occasionally being the one to intervene when other functions push back.
- **Reading Key #7 (Insights-Based Strategy) as "we have OKRs."** Strategy is problem selection informed by insights; OKRs are the tracking tool. See [[okrs]] for the SVPG framing that resolves this.
- **Treating Key #3 (Strong Product Leaders) as "hire senior PMs."** Product *leadership* is the layer above the trio — the CPO / CTO / head of design and the levels below them. Coaching them, or bringing in a [[types-of-product-coaching|product leadership coach]], is often the highest-leverage move.

## Example — the "But What Can I Do?" appendix (Ch 48)
Ch 48 closes with Cagan's guidance for the individual contributor or single product leader who reads the book first, in an org that isn't yet ready to transform:

1. Talk to the rest of your product team; if you're alone, your first job is to convince the team of the benefits.
2. Focus on upskilling yourselves to the new product model competencies — *"at an absolute minimum, learning these new skills will only help your careers."*
3. Ask your manager if the company would be willing to let your team serve as a **pilot** for the new way of working for the next quarter or two — starting with changing how you build and how you solve problems (**not** how you decide — echoing [[transformation-politics|Cagan's "Decide last" sequencing]]).
4. *"If the experiment goes well, hopefully it will spread. If it doesn't, you have contained the risk to just your team."*

This is [[pilot-teams|the pilot pattern]] at the bottom-up scale.

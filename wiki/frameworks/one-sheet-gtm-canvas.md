# The One-Sheet Product Go-to-Market Canvas

> The lightweight one-page artifact that aligns product + marketing + sales + customer-success on a product's GTM. Lauchengco's practical framework from [[loved|LOVED]] Ch 19. *"Think guardrails that clarify purpose and priorities and keep things on track."*

## Why it matters
Ch 19 opens with **Bandwidth**, a highly technical voice/messaging platform. The product team was solving a thorny problem: the top 10 accounts weren't growing as fast as the next 50 — and those 50 had **vastly different needs** than the top 10. This was causing friction around how to prioritize development and GTM.

**John Bell** (product leader) recognized that PMM was lean and *"tended to focus more on tactics and tools"* — GTM misalignments were more visible to the product team (with deeper category + product expertise) than to PMM. So the product team took the lead: gathered sales, marketing, PMM, and BD for a three-hour working session, each coming ready with market insights.

*"Three hours later, they emerged with a product go-to-market canvas now owned by product marketing. They felt more aligned, inspired, and confident in their go-to-market strategies and key activities. The product team also gained a more market-oriented framing for what they should prioritize and why."*

## The metaphor — Ch 19
*"A product go-to-market is like a puzzle. A product go-to-market canvas is like giving everyone the picture of what that puzzle should look like before anyone starts building it. And like puzzle building, it goes better and faster with the border pieces in-place first. They create reference points that make filling in the rest much easier."*

## What the canvas does (Ch 19)
Ch 19's four claims for the canvas — the coaching pitch:
- **Quickly show opportunities or misalignment** — for both product and marketing.
- **Keep everyone focused**, even when improvising or reacting to something new.
- **See the most important activities at-a-glance** and communicate them internally.
- **Understand the why behind actions and stay aligned at the strategic level**, not just ticking off tactics.

**What it's *not*:** *"It's intentionally a lightweight canvas, not meant to be a comprehensive plan."*

## The canvas structure (Figure 19.1)
The canvas has four **horizontal swim lanes** across a time axis (quarters):

1. **Customer / Outside Environment** — customer + market realities that shape everything.
2. **Product Milestones** — anticipated releases, integrations, new platforms, feature segments.
3. **Marketing Strategies** — the guardrails for activity (the "why" behind everything).
4. **Key Activities** — the important tactics others depend on or plan around.

*"It gives teams the equivalent of puzzle borders."*

## Who runs it — Ch 19
- **Owned + driven by product marketing.**
- **The canvas is a living document.**
- **Attended by product, marketing, sales, customer success, and BD** — all bring insights.

## The 6-step process (Ch 19)
Ch 19's step-by-step recipe:

### The setup — a ~3-hour meeting
- **Hour 1:** what's working · what's not · market realities for customers · what must improve for the business to grow. Each function shares learnings from their domain. Start to see gaps the PGTM must address.
- **Hours 2–3:** the rest of the framework. Optionally include a **SWOT** exercise (see below).

### Step 1 — Customer + outside environment (top swim lane)
Everyone contributes customer + market knowledge. **Do this first: grounds everything from customers' perspective, not the company's.** Add competitive announcements, conference events, major ecosystem releases (iOS updates). *"You don't need to list everything — just key elements that might affect what people think, do, or see."*

### Step 2 — Product milestones (next swim lane)
With agile, detail may be limited — but for major initiatives (integrations · new platforms · features for particular segments) you probably have direction. **Capture whatever is known.**

### Step 3 — Marketing strategies (the guardrails)
Business goals = company's goals (assumed). Strategies are the guardrails for activity. *"You should be able to draw a direct line between your strategies and how they move the needle on business goals."* Clear strategies help executors understand the bigger picture.

### Step 4 — Key activities supporting strategies
*"This is not everything on a team's checklist — it's only important activities others may be dependent on or want to plan around."* Examples: quarterly sales training, new sales playbook, launching new pricing, naming key features, building a demo across a product suite. *"This canvas is as much a communication tool as it is a planning one."*

### Step 5 — Work from the outside edges in
**Start with how you want the year to end and work backwards.** *"Do you want to have more partners by the end of the year? What will they need to see when, and what's a necessary precondition for anything you want to do?"*

The tendency Ch 19 warns against: *"most start with the most immediate quarter or two, fill in all the things they think they need to do, then the canvas peters out in the outbound quarters. This is especially true for early-stage startups; there isn't much value planning past two quarters."*

For earlier-stage companies, *"just bucketing 'second half' as a parking lot for ideas works fine, but don't skip discussing it."*

### Step 6 — Revise
Initial meeting produces direction + cross-functional alignment. Use time outside the meeting to think through details + fill gaps. *"Don't try to fill everything in. You want to have some capacity to add things you haven't anticipated and keep the canvas responsive. I recommend bringing the teams together to revise thinking once a quarter."*

## Leverage your strengths — SWOT as a gut check (Ch 19)
Ch 19 recommends **SWOT** (Strengths / Weaknesses / Opportunities / Threats) as a fast gut check:
- **Are your strategies leveraging your opportunities?**
- **Are they avoiding walking into the teeth of a competitor?**
- **Do they leverage your strengths without requiring you to fix all your weaknesses?**

Example: if a weakness is a small sales force relative to competition, prioritize enabling partnerships or product-led growth.

## Make it customer-first (Table 19.1)
Ch 19's most-quoted discipline: *"What often separates a really good product go-to-market canvas from others is how well you're mapping actions to your customer's world."*

Table 19.1 contrasts company-first vs customer-first:

| Scenario | Company-first | Customer-first |
|---|---|---|
| Major new product release ready in March | Launch when product is done, which turns out to be Spring Break | Work with product to find a launch date clear of major holiday breaks and in time for an industry conference |
| New customer vertical campaign in December | Sales worries about missing number, launches in December. New vertical is busy with year-end planning + holidays. | Focus on a year-end campaign with pricing incentive. Launch the vertical campaign with the new year, when the vertical has capacity |

**The framing:** *"Anchoring PGTM from the customer's vantage point is about optimizing based on what customers want to hear and when they have the capacity to hear it. Sometimes who does the talking matters as much as what's being said. If a sales rep tells a customer about a coming product upgrade it lands differently than if a customer success manager says the same thing."*

## The Bandwidth session output (Ch 19)
Ch 19 walks through the first-hour outputs from the Bandwidth working session:

- No strong enough brand / clear market position.
- Fastest-growth customer segment was one sales knew little about.
- Externally facing digital assets didn't speak to high-growth segments or the varied customer journeys across product lines.
- Sales front-line experiences weren't getting back to product or PMM.
- No learning from customers lost.
- Not clear on ongoing value they could provide customers after the initial sale.

*"This is as far as the initial meeting got and was all that was required. The first meeting is about capturing ideas and needs. The customer-first polishing and idea refinement happens in smaller, subsequent meetings."*

## What it's not — a complete marketing plan
Ch 19's boundary: *"One thing it's not is a complete marketing plan."* The canvas sets the strategic frame; marketing teams then own their detailed plans. Ch 20 (Understanding in Action) walks through real marketing plans to show how the canvas' strategic thinking gets carried through — *"you'll notice (not surprisingly) that the plans get better as company stage matures."*

## When to use it
- **Product go-to-market planning** at any stage.
- **Cross-functional alignment reset** when GTM misalignment is showing up.
- **Quarterly PMM–PM–marketing–sales working sessions.**
- **Kickoff for a new product** heading to market.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Bandwidth's original state — PMM in tactics/tools mode without strategic frame.** The canvas is the fix.
- **Start-here-now planning.** Focus only on the immediate quarter; canvas peters out. Fix: start with year-end + work backward.
- **Company-first anchoring.** Optimize around what's easy for the company; miss customer capacity to hear. Fix: Table 19.1 discipline.
- **Complete marketing plan in one document.** The canvas is a lightweight one-page guardrail, not the plan.
- **PMM owns it in name only.** Without ownership + quarterly refresh, the canvas becomes shelf art.
- **No SWOT check.** Strategies that walk into the teeth of a competitor or require fixing weaknesses first.

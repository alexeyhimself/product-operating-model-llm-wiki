# Continuous Interviewing

> [[teresa-torres|Torres]]'s **keystone habit** for continuous discovery: **at least one customer interview per week, by the trio itself, on a target-market user, using story-based (not direct-question) interviewing.** Root source: *[[continuous-discovery-habits|Continuous Discovery Habits]]* Ch 5 (technique) and Ch 14 (why it's the keystone habit — Duhigg).

## When to use it
- **Every week.** The whole point is that it's continuous. If the team is only interviewing "when we need to," this framework isn't in play yet.
- **Feeds the opportunity space of the [[opportunity-solution-tree|OST]].** Interviews are the primary input.
- **Complements — does not replace — [[the-four-big-risks|value testing]] and [[assumption-testing|assumption tests]].** Interviews surface opportunities and reveal actual behavior; value tests reveal what customers *will do* with a specific solution. Both are needed.

## Why "keystone"
[[continuous-discovery-habits|CDH]] Ch 14: Duhigg's *Power of Habit* defines a **keystone habit** as one that, once adopted, drives the adoption of others. Torres's coaching observation: teams that build a weekly-interview habit also *"remember to doubt what they know and to test their assumptions"* — they naturally start rapid prototyping, running assumption tests, connecting research to decisions. If a team is picking one discovery habit to install first, this is the one.

## The definition matters
The [[continuous-discovery-habits|CDH]] Ch 1 operational definition of continuous discovery has four clauses, and interviewing lives inside all four:
- **Weekly touchpoints with customers** — cadence, not campaigns.
- **By the team building the product** — the trio does the interviews. Not outsourced to a design agency, a research team, or the account manager. The trio needs the empathy transfer that only comes from being in the room.
- **Small research activities** — 20–30 minute conversations, not two-hour deep-dives. Sustainable at a weekly cadence.
- **In pursuit of a desired outcome** — you're not interviewing "in general"; you're interviewing to inform the current outcome.

Break any clause and the whole discipline breaks.

## The core technique — story-based interviewing (Ch 5)

**Direct questions produce ideal-self answers, not real behavior.** Torres's workshop demo: ask "what factors do you consider when buying a pair of jeans?" and everyone answers *fit*. Ask "tell me about the last time you bought a pair of jeans" and they describe a brand-loyalty + on-sale + convenient-shipping purchase — actual behavior, not aspirational behavior. Grounded in: decades of investigative-interviewing research; Michael Gazzaniga's **left-brain interpreter** studies (participants fabricate reasons whenever the real reason isn't accessible); Kahneman's *"confidence is a feeling"* — coherent story ≠ true story.

**Ask for a specific recent story, then excavate it.** The pattern:
1. Set the expectation upfront: *"I'd like to hear the full story with as many details as possible. When you're done I'll ask about anything you left out."* Overrides the conversational-norm 50/50 back-and-forth.
2. Anchor to a specific instance: *"Tell me about the last time you [watched TV / bought jeans / applied for a loan]."*
3. Use **temporal prompts** to guide the arc: *"start at the beginning — what happened first?" · "what happened next?" · "what happened before that?"*
4. Use **story-element prompts**: *"where were you? · who was with you? · what challenges did you encounter? · how did you overcome them? · did anyone help?"*
5. **Redirect generalizations back to the specific.** When the participant slides into *"I usually…"* or *"in general…"*, gently guide back: *"in this specific instance, did you face that challenge?"*
6. **Draw the story as they tell it.** Node-and-link representation of the customer's arc. The drawing is the artifact — you use it to align the trio, feed the [[opportunity-solution-tree|OST]], and structure the opportunity space (Ch 6).

## The interview snapshot
[[continuous-discovery-habits|CDH]] Ch 5 introduces the **interview snapshot** — a one-page synthesis of each interview capturing: who was interviewed (recruitment criteria for variation); the story-drawing; the needs, pain points, and desires (candidate opportunities); notable quotes; open questions for next week. Snapshots let the tree evolve without re-listening to hours of recordings.

## Distinguishing research questions from interview questions
Most teams generate too many **research questions** ("what do our customers care about?") and try to answer them all in one interview, ending with 10-page discussion guides. Torres's stance: if you interview weekly, you don't need to ask everything now. In any given interview:
- The **primary research question** is *what needs, pain points, and desires matter most to this customer?*
- Only *after* those surface do you dive into the specifics that are most relevant to your current outcome.
- Let the participant set the direction. What matters to *them* trumps what you need to learn.

## Recruiting
- **Target market only.** Torres's warning: interviewing whoever is convenient is why the wiki's *"passive-candidates"* story flopped (Ch 5) — the wrong participant tells you a coherent-but-wrong story.
- **Recruit for variation.** Different demographics, contexts, product usage. Don't over-index on your most vocal customers.
- **Build recruiting into the product.** Torres's Ch 14 stance for teams whose companies say "you can't talk to customers": start with someone who is *like* a customer; use each conversation to get introduced to another. Iterate from there.

## Cadence
- **Minimum:** 1 interview per week per trio (some SVPG canon says [[product-discovery-techniques|2–3 qualitative sessions per week]]; treat 1/week as the floor).
- **The trio itself.** Rotating who drives is fine (Ch 4 individual-first pattern). PM present at value-oriented interviews is non-negotiable (matches Cagan's *"PM must be at every qualitative value test"* — [[product-discovery-techniques]] §5c).
- **Sustainable, not heroic.** Small research activities. The moment interviews become a two-week project every quarter, the habit breaks.

## What continuous interviewing is *not*
- **Not** a demand test or a value test. Interviews reveal *actual behavior* around opportunities; value tests reveal what customers do with a *specific* solution. Don't try to prove value in an interview.
- **Not** *"what feature do you want?"* Torres opens Ch 5 with the Jobs quote — Apple didn't ask customers to design visual voicemail. The point of the interview is to uncover *unmet needs*, not solicit specs.
- **Not** market research surveys. Different tool, different purpose.
- **Not** contract research handed off to an agency. The trio must be in the room.

## Anti-patterns
- **Direct questions.** *"What matters most to you?"* → ideal-self answers.
- **Long discussion guides.** Signals infrequent interviews; produces shallow answers.
- **Interviews without an outcome.** Research for research's sake; findings never touch a decision.
- **The trio outsourcing the empathy.** The research report arrives; the team reads a summary; no one has heard a customer describe the pain in their own words. Empathy transfer requires presence.
- **One customer, one conclusion.** Torres — Simply Business team (Ch 12) — heard *"late payments"* in interview after interview and prioritized it — but assumption tests showed customers didn't want a third party to help. Interviews surface candidates; the OST and assumption tests validate.
- **Skipping the story-drawing.** The drawing is what lets the team compare stories across interviews to find distinct branches (Ch 6).

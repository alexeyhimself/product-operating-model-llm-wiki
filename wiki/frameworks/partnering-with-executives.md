# Partnering with Executives — the Six-Element Trust Model

> Cagan's Ch 26 of *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* structures the product-team ↔ executive relationship around **six named elements**: **Decisions · Outcomes · Disagreements · Promises · Surprises · Trust**. This framework page is the coaching artifact — a checklist product leaders can walk with executives to establish the mutual commitments the model depends on. Sits within [[cross-functional-partnering|the Part VI partnership hub]] and pairs with [[transformation-politics|the political frame]].

## When to use it
- At the start of a transformation, when the product leader is establishing working relationships with the executive team.
- When a specific interaction (a decision, a broken promise, a post-build surprise) reveals the trust model isn't shared.
- During executive onboarding when a new executive joins.
- As a self-assessment for the product leader — *am I holding up my end of each of the six?*
- As part of [[overcoming-objections|CEO/Board objections coaching]] (Ch 38 objections resolve here).

## The two premises the framework rests on
Cagan states these before the six elements:
1. **Empowerment ≠ backing off.** *"Many product leaders and product teams would like to think this is as easy as saying to the executives 'Please just back off and give the product teams space to do their work.' But this ignores the reality that executives have very real needs when it comes to running the company responsibly and effectively."*
2. **Frequency stays high; *nature* changes.** *"Rather than reducing the level of interaction, in the product model, product teams need frequent, high-quality engagement."*

## The six elements

### 1. Decisions
- **Executive commitment:** push decisions down to the product leader or team best positioned to make them (usually those working directly with the technology, users, and customers).
- **Executive commitment:** share the strategic context — business strategy, financial parameters, regulatory developments, industry trends, strategic partnerships. Teams *"can make good decisions only when they are provided the necessary strategic context."*
- **Team commitment:** share openly and honestly the data and reasoning behind decisions.

### 2. Outcomes
- **Team commitment:** *"what matters is results"*; accept accountability for outcomes over output whenever possible.
- **Executive commitment:** give the team a *problem to solve* (not a solution to build); provide *as many degrees of freedom as possible*.
- **Team commitment:** take responsibility for a solution that simultaneously works for customers, business, and technology.

### 3. Disagreements
- **The premise:** teams have access to enabling technology + actual customers; executives have the broadest data + best wider business context. Differences will occur.
- **The stance change:** in the old model, contrarian/counterintuitive ideas aren't explored; in the product model, these *often power real innovation*.
- **Team commitment:** when it matters, run tests quickly and responsibly to collect evidence — or, where necessary, proof.
- **Executive commitment:** allow (and encourage) alternative approaches when the team believes it's necessary to succeed.
- **Team commitment (loop):** for a proposed high-risk approach, run product discovery tests responsibly and share findings.

### 4. Promises
- **Team commitment:** when an executive needs a specific date for a specific deliverable, treat it as a [[high-integrity-commitments|high-integrity commitment]].
- **Team commitment:** honor the organizational damage of broken promises; only the team responsible for delivery makes a promise; no promise is asked or given without knowing what is involved.
- **Executive commitment:** *"high-integrity commitments being the exception and not the rule"*; the time + effort in making and delivering on HICs is significant.
- **Team commitment (once made):** *"do everything within their power to deliver on it."*

### 5. Surprises
- **The premise:** surprises are sometimes unavoidable; teams must try hard to minimize them.
- **Team commitment (pre-build):** if there's anything even potentially sensitive, the PM recognizes it and **shows and discusses the prototype with the impacted executive before building.** Cagan's frame of *"one of the worst forms of waste"* — a quality, scalable implementation, discovered post-build to be unacceptable.
- **Executive commitment (pre-build):** if you review a prototype during discovery, raise concerns then — not after production. Cagan explicitly names *"very wasteful and frustrating"* the pattern of nodding during discovery and objecting after ship.
- **Both:** when a serious problem does surface after build, hold a **postmortem** to see how to avoid the waste going forward.

### 6. Trust
- **The mutual understanding.** Empowerment depends on trust; teams strive to earn it. Product leaders + teams trust that executives are leading the company in a positive direction.
- **Not perfection.** *"Neither expects the other to be perfect, but both product leaders and product teams each recognize that they depend on the other and commit to best efforts to help each other succeed."*

## Inputs & outputs
**Inputs.** A product leader + an executive (or executive team) willing to have a structured conversation about the working model; ideally paired with the strategic-context inputs the org already has (or must author — see [[strategic-context]]).

**Outputs.** Shared mutual commitments on each of the six elements. The best form is *written* — a short document each side signs off on — because Cagan's Ch 26 is fundamentally a **written narrative** in structure. A useful pairing: an [[strategic-context|explicit strategic-context document]] on the executive side + a **weekly / monthly outcomes drumbeat** on the team side ([[transformation-politics|internal-evangelism dimension]]).

## Pitfalls
- **Reducing "trust" to a values statement.** Trust in this framework is the *result* of the first five elements being kept, not a standalone commitment. Delivering on Decisions/Outcomes/Disagreements/Promises/Surprises is what produces the sixth.
- **One-sided commitments.** Product-leader commitments alone don't produce partnership; the executive side of each element is equally load-bearing.
- **Overusing HICs.** Ch 26 explicitly warns: HICs are the exception path. Making them the norm burns the very capacity that produces outcomes.
- **Post-build surprises the PM could have previewed.** The single most-cited waste in Ch 26. If the PM has a working relationship with the executive, previewing prototypes on anything sensitive is a small ask that saves large rework.
- **"Backing off" as empowerment.** Ch 26's opening premise — treat this as an anti-pattern to name explicitly when it appears.

## Example
An adaptation applies naturally to a transformation kickoff:

> "As we begin this transformation, we want to establish a mutual model of how the product organization and executive team work together going forward. There are six things we want to agree on: (1) how decisions get made; (2) how outcomes get set and measured; (3) how disagreements get resolved; (4) how promises get made and kept; (5) how surprises get prevented; (6) how we build trust over time."

Then walk each element, name the mutual commitments in writing, and revisit the document quarterly (the model itself needs a check-in, not just the work).

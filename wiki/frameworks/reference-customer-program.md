# Reference Customer Program

> A structured program in which a product team works closely with **~6–8 hand-picked customers from the target market** through the entire product effort, so that at launch it has **at least six live, happy, referenceable customers** willing to publicly endorse the product. Called the **Charter User Program** in [[inspired|INSPIRED]] (1st ed, 2008) and the **Customer Discovery Program** in [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] (Ch 23) — same technique, canonical modern name is the latter. Also referred to as a Customer Advisory Board, Customer Council, or Voice of the Customer program.

## Why it matters
Nothing shortens a sales cycle like a set of marquee customers already using the product happily; conversely, a launch with zero references is a huge red flag ([[inspired|INSPIRED]] Ch 15). The program is Cagan's technique for **solving two problems with one mechanism**: getting deep, ongoing access to real target customers during discovery *and* producing the reference customers sales and marketing need at launch. It is the workhorse for **new products** and for **existing products entering a new market** ([[cross-functional-partnering]] · [[product-marketing]]).

## When to use it
- New product effort with meaningful **value** and **viability** risk.
- Existing product moving into a new customer segment / geography / vertical.
- Platform product — the *reference applications* variant (aim for 6 real applications built on your platform).
- Whenever you need durable, deep customer access for the trio and the beginnings of the sales-enablement narrative — both at once.

## How it works — the mechanics ([[inspired|INSPIRED]] Ch 15 → [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Ch 23)

**Goal.** End the program with **~6 live, happy, publicly referenceable customers** from your target market.

**Recruit.** Start with **8–10** at the beginning of the project (some will drop out). Cap participation around **10** — more than that and you can't work with each closely. For **consumer** products expand to **10–15** ([[inspired]] Ch 15). Recruit from existing customers, prospects, or a blend.

**The two-sided deal.**
- **For the customer:** early and significant product input; early access; typically reduced cost.
- **For you:** a set of users available for ongoing dialog and questions; access to their site/offices/users; agreement to attend periodic group sessions; agreement to deploy test versions promptly with timely feedback; and — if happy with what you delivered — agreement to serve as a **public reference customer** at launch.

**Non-negotiables** ([[inspired|INSPIRED]] Ch 15):
- **No prepayment.** Customers must not pay upfront to participate — this converts the relationship from partner to buyer of a "special" and destroys the arrangement ([[overcoming-objections|see the specials anti-pattern]]). You take their money **after** you deliver something they love.
- **Target market only.** Recruit customers from the market you're actually building for — **not early adopters**. Early adopters are more tolerant and can lead you to a product only they will love.
- **Not a custom build.** Every participant must know the goal is a general product, not a custom solution for them. (If the vendor can't build a real business, they'll be left with unsupported dead-end software — this is in their interest too.)
- **Partners, not customers-yet.** Treat them as development colleagues. Relationships from good programs last decades.

**Cadence.** Prototypes, on-site user testing at their offices, hundreds of detailed questions, release-candidate testing in their environment. Ship to them **before** general release and confirm they're **live and happy** before public launch.

**Platform variant.** For platform products aim for **six reference *applications*** rather than six customers — and work with each application partner to ensure their end-user application is itself successful (a great way: encourage each partner to run *its own* charter user program).

## The cross-functional collaboration ([[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Ch 23)
In the modern SVPG canon the program is one of the **eight PM ↔ Product Marketing collaboration areas** — the technique for jointly discovering a solution and its go-to-market at the same time. Roles ([[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Ch 23 · [[cross-functional-partnering]] · [[product-marketing]]):

- **Product Manager** — applies learnings to the product (discovery, prioritization, prototype iteration).
- **Product Marketing** — applies learnings to positioning, messaging, sales tools, and the sales process; typically helps identify candidates; runs the reference-customer conversion at launch.
- **Sales** — often surfaces initial candidates; the head of sales is a critical stakeholder for the "no specials" discipline ([[overcoming-objections|Ch 37]]).

The program becomes the shared object of trust between product, product marketing, and sales — the single most-cited answer across the [[cross-functional-partnering|six Partnering-with chapters]] to *"how do we know it will sell?"*

## Reality check — the program as an early demand signal
Cagan's line ([[inspired|INSPIRED]] Ch 15): if you can't recruit charter users, "it's very likely you are chasing a problem that isn't that important, and you will probably have a very hard time selling this product." Recruitment difficulty is one of the **earliest and cheapest** signals that the [[opportunity-assessment|opportunity]] is weaker than it looked — treat it as a discovery result, not an obstacle to work around.

## Anti-patterns
- **Too many participants.** Sales will lean on you to accept everyone who asks. Cap at ~10. Consider running a separate *early-release program* for those who just want the software early but aren't right for charter status.
- **Prepaying customers.** Turns partners into custom-work buyers; you now owe features, not learning.
- **Early adopters as charter customers.** Product ends up loved only by the fringe.
- **Custom-build drift.** Participants must know the goal is a general product from day one.
- **Ship without confirming live+happy.** Customers not actually using the product happily = no references at launch = a launch with the very red flag the program was built to prevent.
- **Product manager not attending.** [[inspired|Cagan]]: the PM should attend *every* user interview, site visit, usability test, and charter-user meeting that pertains to the product.

## Distinguish from
- **User interviews / customer visits** — one-off. Charter customers are a *durable* group across the whole project cycle.
- **Beta program / early-release program** — larger, less structured, primarily about hardening the release. Charter programs are small, deep, and start at the *beginning* of the effort.
- **Focus groups / surveys** — market-research instruments. Charter is a *product development partnership*.
- **Specials** — a one-off custom build for a single customer paying upfront. The charter program's non-negotiables (no prepayment; general product intent) exist specifically to keep the two from collapsing into each other ([[overcoming-objections|Ch 32 / Ch 37]]).

# Product Marketing Metrics

> How to measure whether PMM is doing a good job — [[loved|LOVED]] Ch 13. *"Unlike sales, which hits its numbers or doesn't, product marketing doesn't have many hard metrics showing a job well done. There are some shorter-term indicators, but for most product marketing outcomes, they take time to know if they worked."*

## Why it matters
Ch 13 opens with the honest problem: PMM's outputs take time to show up in the numbers. Positioning shifts, sales-cycle changes, brand-perception moves — all lag. The Ch 13 answer: manage expectations up front on what's wanted from the function, and use a metrics set drawn from **product · marketing · sales · financial** through the PMM lens.

Ch 13's framing: *"The best ways to measure product marketing come down to what you're trying to get from it. It's why managing expectations about what is wanted from the function is so crucial to do up front."*

## Product marketing objectives — Ch 13 example [[okrs|OKRs]]
Ch 13 gives five example PMM OKRs to calibrate what "good" looks like — adapt to your company's stage + goals:

- **Become recognized category leader** by major analyst firm(s).
- **Launch product to improve market awareness indicators by 10%** and enable adoption by [key new market] as % of customer base.
- **Position product as redefining [insert concept]** as evidenced by inbound or category mentions of [insert concept].
- **Enable sales to win >50% of competitive deals.**
- **Grow organic evangelism by 10%** on key digital and social platforms.

Ch 13's alignment rule: *"If you're most concerned about enabling revenue, product marketing OKRs will be more aligned with sales objectives. If you're most concerned about market positioning or building awareness, OKRs will focus more on work done together with marketing."*

**Time horizon distinction:** *"In general, sales and marketing metrics focus on shorter-term business need (like top of funnel size, pipeline, revenue) and product marketing focuses more on what's also important for the longer-term future of the business."*

## OKRs vs KPIs — Ch 13's disambiguation
Ch 13 pins down the vocabulary (many teams confuse them):
- **OKR (Objectives and Key Results)** — a goal-setting framework for defining objectives.
- **KRs (Key Results)** — specific KPIs with targets. Usually cover a subset of KPIs a team tracks.
- **KPI (Key Performance Indicator)** — a metric that measures something important.

Ch 13 worked example: *"Sales cycle time is a KPI. Decreasing sales cycle time by 10% (objective) to 45 days (key result) is an example of an OKR."*

Alignment rule: *"When using OKRs, ensure they create alignment across functions. In the case of product marketing, their OKRs must be aligned with product, sales, and marketing teams. Sometimes, OKRs are shared, particularly with marketing teams."* See [[okrs]] for the wiki's canon on OKR mechanics.

## The metrics stack (Ch 13)
Ch 13 organizes PMM's metric set into four categories. Coach reads each metric *through the PMM lens*.

### Product metrics
- **HEART metrics** (Happiness · Engagement · Acquisition · Retention · Task Success). Typically owned by product + UX. PMM watches adoption + retention for marketing implications. Example: product losing users during onboarding → PMM directs marketing to send re-engagement emails.
- **Customer funnel metrics.** Tracked by marketing or product (depending on GTM model). Look for stage-to-stage flow. If conversion is lacking, PMM directs specific tactics — targeted direct mail, new call script, video-based training.

### Marketing metrics
- **Customer journey engagement.** Content/pages/websites prospective customers engage with (including third-party sites). PMM examines with sale-stage movement to see what customers or sales need most.
- **Marketing qualified leads (MQLs).** In product-led motions, **product qualified leads (PQLs)** either replace or augment MQL definitions. PMM works with marketing on target-segment refinement + diagnosing lead-flow issues.
- **Inbound discovery.** Inbound organic search + direct search as % of visitors. *"The absolute numbers don't tell you much but their shifts over time indicate if more focus on awareness or positioning is required."*

### Sales metrics
- **Sales cycle time.** Look for trends that track with your chosen GTM model (don't expect an enterprise cycle to take a few weeks). *"You want cycles as predictable as possible."* Fast-close deals for well-qualified customers → document + repeat.
- **Win rates.** Closed-won vs lost + win rates vs specific competitors. If either lags, PMM often leads the diagnosis: training · tools · process · sales playbook · marketing activity mix · messaging · positioning · pricing · packaging · third-party evangelism — *"the full product marketing stack"*.

### Financial metrics
- **Conversion rates by product** (for multi-product companies). Monitor whether product mix aligns with where business needs to grow. Example: growing into a new vertical — % of new logos or new revenue from that vertical is the check.
- **Customer acquisition cost (CAC).** *"Should trend down or stay level if the marketing channel mix, messaging, and positioning are working. If CAC is increasing or unsustainable, examine customer segments, messaging, and the marketing mix."*
- **Lifetime customer value (LTV).** *"If the right customers are being targeted, the lifetime value of a customer versus the cost to acquire it stays at a healthy ratio for the type of business you're in."* Poor ratio → product issues (not enough value to stay) or positioning (expectations vs delivery mismatch) or sales-training (what customer was sold vs what they expected).
- **Retention.** Important but *lagging* indicator. NPS + periodic customer-satisfaction metrics can be leading indicators. Sub-target retention → PMM examines GTM materials for disconnects with customer expectations, sales process, price, packaging.

## Coaching prompt — the diagnostic chain
When a business metric is off, Ch 13's discipline: **trace it back through the PMM stack.**

- **Low win rate?** → sales training · tools · playbook · messaging · positioning · pricing · packaging · third-party evangelism.
- **Rising CAC?** → segments · messaging · marketing mix.
- **Bad LTV:CAC ratio?** → product value · positioning · sales-training expectations mismatch.
- **Retention drop?** → GTM material · sales process · price · packaging misalignment with customer expectations.

Each of these traces goes through PMM's four fundamentals + partnerships.

## Practice patience and persistence
Ch 13's honest closing: *"Good product marketing takes time, and measuring its effectiveness does too. Like all teams, product marketing does best when it knows how its success will be measured. This requires clarity on what a company expects from the role."*

The corollary: **PMM leaders must set + hold expectations up front** — that most work has 1-to-multi-quarter lag before it shows up in the numbers.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Measuring PMM only by lead volume.** Ignores positioning, category leadership, competitive win rate, sales-cycle predictability.
- **PMM OKRs disconnected from sales, marketing, product.** Ch 13's alignment rule: PMM OKRs *must* align across functions.
- **Confusing OKRs with KPIs.** Ch 13 pins down the distinction — OKR is the goal-setting frame; KPI is the metric; KR is a KPI-with-target inside an OKR.
- **Expecting PMM outcomes on quarter-1 timelines.** Most PMM outputs (positioning shifts, sales-process changes, brand-perception moves) lag 1-to-multi-quarter.
- **Ignoring product-side metrics.** PMM is the bridge from HEART / funnel signals to marketing action.
- **Ignoring financial-side metrics.** PMM has direct implications for CAC, LTV, retention.

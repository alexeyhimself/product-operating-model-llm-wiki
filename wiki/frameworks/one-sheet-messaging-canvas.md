# The One-Sheet Messaging Canvas

> **The collection point for messaging discovery + the artifact for final messaging.** [[loved|LOVED]] Ch 25. Separates messaging into individual building blocks — positioning · customer benefits · how a product does what it claims · proof — that can be used for any GTM purpose. *"You don't use all of them all the time, just what you need at the right time."*

## Why it matters
Ch 25 opens with the **CEO panic meeting** — losing head-to-head deals to a competitor with a much better story. Marketing shared what was working in email; head of product suggested a catchy tagline; sales didn't love any of the new ideas; the IC PMM tried to smooth transitions between ideas from senior leaders.

*"In an effort to rush to get the new story out, the presentation wasn't tested in front of any customers or used in an actual pitch. The end result was tolerable but not good."*

Lauchengco's diagnosis: *"This collaboration-to-mediocrity is all too common. Coming up with great messaging and the story to embed it in is a team sport, but it is product marketing's job to collect the inputs and craft a strong output. They are product managers to the product's story and how it gets messaged."*

The canvas is the artifact that turns messaging into an iterative process rather than a room-brainstorm event.

## Design principle — building blocks, not paragraphs
Ch 25's core principle: separate messaging into individual building blocks so each part can be used for any GTM purpose:
- **Positioning**
- **Customer benefits (pillars)**
- **How a product does what it claims** (per pillar)
- **Proof** (evidence the claims are true)

*"When the canvas is finalized, everything in it builds the case for why people should believe a product is a good one. The product's market position should also be clear."*

**Do NOT fill it out like a form.** *"At the start, document ideas worth testing. Then test, probe, and let it be a hot mess while ideas are refined with teams. Put them in front of customers in the mediums in which they'll see them. The process is iterative, and like any good discovery process, be prepared to throw away as many ideas as you keep."*

## The 7-step process (Ch 25)

### Setup — Messaging discovery
**Open-ended questions to find customer language + context.** Can be part of [[market-fit|market-fit discovery]]:
- *"How might they describe this to a friend?"*
- *"What problems do they experience that make them look for a better way of doing things?"*

Ch 25 recommends **micro-surveys** — short, one or two questions, always open-ended. Examples:
- *"Why didn't you try our service?"*
- *"What can we do better to meet your needs?"*

*"They capture what people think without any prompting."*

### Step 1 — Decide on most important customer segments
*"Your messaging is not for all your audiences, just the most important ones. Make it clear for them. There are plenty of other tools to reach additional audiences. This is how you avoid trying to say all things to all people and mean nothing."*

### Step 2 — Come up with starter messaging + key support messages (the pillars)
Think about what customers want to hear. What makes you credible and intriguing?
- A broad product benefit.
- A novel way your product does something.
- An ecosystem you're associated with.
- Technologies you unlock.

*"Look at all prior iterations for inspiration, and don't fall into the trap of just saying what you want to say. These pillars create the frame into which everything else falls."*

### Step 3 — List areas of value in customer-friendly language under each pillar
*"Don't just list features and what they do. Tie what a feature does to its benefits or use cases."* Example: explain a benefit is possible because of a unique way your product operates.

### Step 4 — Designate messages as appropriate for specific audiences
Not every message is right for every segment. **Identify a unique symbol for each segment.** Put it next to messages appropriate for that audience.

Ch 25 example: developer audience gets a square; APIs for data integrations get a square. Increase in developer productivity gets **both a square + a circle** (also meaningful to engineering leaders). *"The same messages can be used for multiple segments."*

### Step 5 — Provide evidence
*"Messaging is just as much about what others say about your product as what you say about it. Think about what enables evangelism."* Customer stories are easier for people to remember and share. Evidence can be:
- Use cases.
- Data about the product being better.
- Research or analyst quotes.
- Proven ROI.

*"What matters is that this section feels full of facts and makes anyone using the evidence feel that it's credible."*

### Step 6 — Test with customers in intended mediums
Email, website, presentation. If you have a sales force, **start there** — they can immediately use it and gauge customer reactions.

*"Listen to responses. You're not looking for customers saying 'This is great!' Rather you're looking for anything that drives curiosity and deepens conversation. If they don't say anything indicating interest, keep working the messages. You want to strike an emotional chord, discover third rails to stay away from, or find yellow-brick roads to follow."*

### Step 7 — Refine
Ch 25's Netflix reference: *"'Cancel anytime' — what works might not be related to your product."*

*"By this step, you may have landed on a tagline that's working. Only include it in the canvas if you want others using it and you're trying to drive consistency."*

**Practical technique** Ch 25 recommends: *"Practice what's on it out loud. Try to listen as someone new to your product would hear it. You're listening for overly jargonistic words and when things feel too forced."*

**Final check with CAST:**
- **Clear** — is what you do easy to understand? Does it drive curiosity? Is it clear vs comprehensive?
- **Authentic** — does this sound like it's from the customers' point of view? Will they feel known? Is it authentic vs authoritative?
- **Simple** — is it easy to understand what's different and compelling?
- **Tested** — was it tested in the context customers experience it?

See [[messaging]] for the full CAST treatment.

## Worked example — IndexTank (Ch 25)
Ch 25 walks through IndexTank (search-as-a-service startup, eventually acquired by LinkedIn).

Initial pillars targeted three segments — developers, operations leads, and (surprise third segment discovered in discovery) **developer evangelists** who tested new tech and were highly engaged in developer forums.

**But after testing on the website, they shifted.** The pillars remained the same, but the short positioning statement became *"Easily add powerful search to your site. Good search is good for business."* Why the shift:
- Curious app developers weren't paying customers.
- Better search enabled more revenue for larger websites → these were IndexTank's better customers.
- **The positioning statement shifted to what their most important customers needed to hear.**

Lauchengco's coaching payoff: *"The process isn't hard but requires some discipline for product marketing to not 'close' on messaging before it's been tested. Just as important are the deliberate decisions on how to apply learnings to final messaging. It might not be the best performing message. It might be what's better for the business and positioning."*

## Ch 25's FAQ
- **How long does the whole process take?** For startups: <1 week for the first iterable pass; up to a month for test + refinement. Updates frequent. For mature companies: months of testing possible; once set, tends to stay for the life cycle of a major release.
- **How much should customer input drive messaging?** *"A lot, but messaging is customer informed, not customer driven. Customer insights are inputs. Only you have all the pieces around them + market + technology + business needs."*
- **How many should I do if we're a multi-product company?** *"One for each product or product family."*
- **What if it goes beyond one sheet?** *"Part of a single-page canvas is to force constraint and prioritization. It pushes you to trade off what's most important versus the tendency to be comprehensive. There are other tools in the toolbox (the website, white papers, feature comparison charts) that can cover deeper information needs. The purpose of the messaging one-sheet is so the most important messages get reinforced consistently by everyone that uses product messaging to do their jobs. Concision matters."*

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Filling it out like a form** — Ch 25 explicitly warns. Iterate; let it be messy while ideas are refined.
- **Closing on messaging before testing.** The canvas is a hypothesis until customers see it in real mediums.
- **The CEO-vs-marketing-vs-sales meeting resolution** (Ch 25 opener) — collaboration-to-mediocrity when senior leader ideas are integrated without a testing loop.
- **Messaging for all audiences at once.** Ch 25 Step 1's discipline.
- **Feature-list under pillars.** Step 3's discipline — tie features to benefits + use cases.
- **Skipping evidence.** Messaging without proof is claims. Fill the evidence section with customer stories, data, analyst quotes.
- **Best-performing = right choice.** IndexTank's positioning-statement shift was to what the *right customers* needed to hear, not what performed best across all visitors.

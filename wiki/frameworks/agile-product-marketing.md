# Product Marketing in the Age of Agile

> How PMM and go-to-market teams stay aligned with product teams that ship continuously. Two techniques from [[loved|LOVED]] Ch 12: **the Release Scale** (shared vocabulary between product + GTM) and **agile marketing** (agile principles applied to marketing).

## Why it matters
Ch 12 opens with **two symmetric failure stories**:
- **Jade** — new PMM at her first product-team standup, points out a feature worth marketing. At the next week's standup she asks for an update; the PM says *"we already released it, per last week's release notes."* Jade scrambles marketing to email customers + do social promotion post-release.
- **Jim** — engineering lead whose teams did large performance + stability improvements across multiple release cycles. Waited to see marketing about the product's blazing performance — saw nothing. *"Jim wondered, after all this engineering effort, what gives?"*

Both are failures of expectation-alignment. Lauchengco's diagnosis: *"This is the challenge of product and go-to-market alignment in the age of agile. The velocity, lack of predictability, and lightweight forms of communication and documentation make it hard for both sides. It is particularly challenging with continuous delivery of product. Go-to-market teams rely on more predictable cadences and planning to do their jobs well. For product teams, what gets marketed and why doesn't always make sense or correlate to effort."*

The fix: *"clarifying expectations and defining a process that lets all teams categorize 'releases' together and act accordingly."*

## Vocabulary — Ch 12's disambiguation
Three terms Ch 12 pins down, because they're used loosely in practice:

- **Sprint** — a short, time-boxed period when a product team works to complete a set amount of work. May or may not include providing access to that functionality.
- **Release** — makes public a new product or combination of features that provides value to customers. Usually includes some coordinated GTM action across the company. *"Sometimes people call releases launches. This is because the term is used loosely in real life."*
- **Launch** (for GTM teams) — *"a major, cross-functionally supported release that the entire company gets behind."* Usually oriented around a set date allowing support from every interaction point. Creates a focal point for coordinated activity so efforts have outsized impact. *"Most companies won't attempt more than one to two major launches a year."*

Ch 12's guardrail: *"The distinctions between a minor release and a major launch are really important for everyone on the go-to-market side. What does or doesn't get done flows from how releases are categorized."*

## The Release Scale
> *"A simple tool whose sole purpose is to create shared vocabulary and expectations between the product and go-to-market teams."* Not a launch checklist — it's the vocabulary alignment that makes the checklist unnecessary.

**What it does:**
- Sets standards around **release "types"** and the GTM activities that support them.
- Clarifies the **broader marketing objectives** of any particular release.
- Clarifies where a release requires **more marketing attention** (high customer impact).
- Clarifies **lead times** marketing needs to do their job well.

**PMM's role:** the focal point ensuring release-categorization conversations happen with product. As companies grow, broader GTM or product-planning meetings become the venue for cross-functional debate (sales · CS · marketing · product · eng · ops all present).

*"When something rises to the level of a release, there is open debate about what category/level release it is. That then determines the amount of resource and support it commands from the go-to-market side."*

### Seven-step recipe for creating your Release Scale
1. **Decide on the calibration scale.** Levels, grades, names, numbers, or tiers — *"use something that doesn't require a legend for people to understand. Stick with hierarchies that have a clear mental map for the differences in levels."*
2. **Use known past releases as examples to define the levels.** *"This is an important and often missed step. Don't make this an academic exercise of a potential future release. By using known reference points, people have a known comparison to calibrate from."*
3. **Identify customer impact.** Most changes don't impact customers in potentially harmful ways, but some do. Degree might shape the level. *"Keep this simple: low, medium, high."*
4. **Define marketing objectives.** What is the market purpose of this release? *"Is it catching up to the competition?"* Makes clear why a small release might merit Level 2 whereas a similarly small release is only Level 1.
5. **Define typical resources and promotional vehicles used.** Get product teams knowledgeable on the range of promotional levers marketing uses. When is something worth a paid campaign vs an email to customers? When does a video get created? *"Level 1 defines the minimum amount of GTM work a team will do. Each subsequent level lists what gets added to the preceding level."*
6. **Articulate needed lead times.** *"People don't understand lead times required for marketing activities unless they've done them."* Website updates, legal reviews, copy editing, design, pitching press, getting CS ready — all take weeks. Make lead time visible.
7. **Talk about releases using the scale in planning meetings.** *"'From the product team's perspective, is this a Level 2 or Level 3 release?' Also, remind them that it's important for the company to have one or two Level 5 releases a year. What can be done to give the company an important GTM moment?"*

**Coaching payoff:** *"Without exception, every company I've encountered that uses some type of release scale says it vastly improves communication and expectation management between the product and go-to-market teams. It's an incredibly easy tool that leads to meaningful improvements in go-to-market execution — and less needless frustration."*

## Agile Marketing
Ch 12's second technique: apply agile principles to how marketing gets done. Ch 12's six principles Lauchengco lists (the marketing analogue of the Agile Manifesto's four):

1. **Respond to changes** versus following a plan.
2. **Rapid iterations** over Big-Bang campaigns.
3. **Testing and data** over opinions and conventions.
4. **Numerous small experiments** over a few large bets.
5. **Individuals and interactions** over "large" market segments.
6. **Collaboration** over silos and hierarchy.

### The highest-functioning version
*"Product marketers act like product managers with a marketing squad."* PMM leads a cross-functional group of specialists (communications, advertising, digital, web, design) in **weekly "scrum" meetings**.

Meeting discipline: *"This is a 'learnings in' not 'reporting out' type meeting."* How learnings apply to future work is part of the agenda. Something urgent — like a competitive-response tool — jumps to the front of the line vs sitting on a list.

### What agile marketing accomplishes
- **Data-driven forum** to discuss what marketing should start doing more of / stop doing.
- **Constantly examines** if marketing's outputs are meeting desired outcomes.
- **Consistent checkpoint** on whether the product is being portrayed accurately.
- **PMM as strategist plays a key role** — checks activities against strategies to make sure ideas / campaign assets stay on point.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **No shared release vocabulary.** Jade + Jim's failures: features shipping without marketing knowing; engineering effort without go-to-market activation.
- **Every release is treated the same.** No release scale → either everything gets Level-5 marketing (unsustainable) or nothing gets it (no visible momentum).
- **Big-bang launch culture.** Companies that only do 1–2 major launches a year and treat everything else as "release notes" miss the mid-tier of coordinated activity.
- **No Level 5 in a year.** *"Companies that lack big visible movements forward start to give the perception they've stagnated."*
- **Agile marketing = "no plan."** The scrum is a *learnings-in* meeting, not a lack of strategy. PMM still checks against strategy.

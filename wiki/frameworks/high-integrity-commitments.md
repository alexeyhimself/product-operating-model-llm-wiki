# High-Integrity Commitments

> Cagan's resolution of the **commitment tension**: stakeholders need dates and deliverables to run the business; product teams resist committing before they know what they'll build or whether it will work. The answer is to **delay the commitment until you've done enough discovery to commit with high confidence**, then honor the date ([[2012-12-15-cagan-high-integrity-commitments|canon]]).

## When to use it
- A senior stakeholder is asking for a date (a campaign launch, a contract obligation, a partnership integration, a board commitment).
- The team would be over-committing if they answered today.
- *Especially* when the org has been burned by missed Agile commitments and is questioning whether empowered teams can be relied on.
- Pairs with [[outcomes-over-output]] — high-integrity commitments are how a problem-focused team still meets the business's date-based needs.

## Steps
1. **Negotiate space** — ask the stakeholder for enough discovery time (typically 1–3 weeks) to investigate the solution and validate it against the four risks ([[the-four-big-risks|value, usability, feasibility, viability]]).
2. **Do real discovery in that window** — prototype, customer touches, engineer feasibility input, stakeholder viability check. Not slides; not handwaving.
3. **Then commit** — to a specific deliverable and date, with the [[delivery-manager|delivery manager]] tracking cross-team dependencies that affect when the work can actually start.
4. **Treat the commitment as inviolable** — if the team commits, the team ships. Honor the date or escalate immediately with a concrete reason.

## Inputs & outputs
- **In:** the stakeholder's need + space to discover.
- **Out:** a date + deliverable the team will honor, *or* an informed "we should not build this" with the evidence to back it.

## Pitfalls
- **Skipping the discovery window.** Compounds the original problem — now you have a commitment *and* you didn't validate the solution.
- **Treating high-integrity commitments as the *norm***. Cagan: "good product companies minimize these commitments." Most work should be outcome-objective work; high-integrity commitments are the exception, reserved for the cases where dates are load-bearing for the business.
- **No delivery manager.** Without [[delivery-manager|someone tracking cross-team dependencies]], even an honest commitment slips because of upstream contention the PM can't see.
- **Dressing feature-team work up.** If the team is given features-with-dates and called "high-integrity commitments," the framework is being used as cover for stakeholder-driven feature work.
- **Discovery time without homework.** A 3-week window with no real customer contact or prototype isn't discovery — it's a delay.

## How it relates to outcomes
[[outcomes-over-output]] is the default operating mode (problems with measurable outcomes); high-integrity commitments are the **exception path** the business uses when a date or deliverable is materially required. Both can live in the same operating model — most teams default to outcome objectives, with a small number of high-integrity commitments tracked separately ([[2015-09-07-cagan-the-alternative-to-roadmaps|canon]]).

# Pilot Teams

> The canonical SVPG technique for transforming to the product model: instead of a company-wide rollout, pick a small number of teams, set them up the new way, and let them produce a visible business outcome. **Risk-mitigation tool, political tool, and proof-of-concept all at once** ([[2024-10-29-cagan-transformation-as-a-project|Cagan]] · [[2024-11-21-cagan-transformation-politics|Cagan]]).

## When to use it
- Your org is starting (or restarting) a transformation to the product model.
- Leadership has aligned on the destination but not on *how*; pilots are the safest way to learn the implications.
- You need to **win hearts and minds** — pilots demonstrate "what good looks like" to skeptical stakeholders and executives.
- You suspect the org is at risk of running [[transformation-as-a-project|transformation as a project]] — pilots are the antidote.

## Steps
1. **Pick the people deliberately.** Hand-pick the [[the-product-team-trio|trio]]: a PM with real value/viability skill, a [[product-designer|designer]] with usability + holistic-CX ownership, a Tech Lead with the autonomy and disposition to be a [[empowered-engineers|missionary]]. Cagan: strong product leaders hand-pick the members.
2. **Pick the problem deliberately.** Not so simple that the product model isn't needed (no one will be impressed). Not so dependent on un-done tech-debt replatforming that the team can't move. The problem must be **meaningful to the business** (a business outcome), not just nice for customers.
3. **Pick the outcome and measure.** What business KPI will move? How will you measure it? *Before* the pilot starts.
4. **Size the team small.** Smaller teams are easier and faster on every axis — role clarity, coaching, communication, collaboration, decision-making, cross-team dependencies. Large pilot rosters signal cost-insensitivity to stakeholders.
5. **Train and coach.** Members need training on the model before the pilot starts, and coaching available throughout. Without coaching, pilots regress to feature-team behavior under stress.
6. **Trust stakeholders on the problem.** Stakeholders have lived with these problems for years. Pilot teams that spend months on problem discovery before solving are politically corrosive. Trust the stakeholder framing; race to discover a successful **solution** ([[2024-11-21-cagan-transformation-politics|canon]]).
7. **Establish a drumbeat of outcomes.** Monthly minimum. Real business results, not activity reports. Share failures as well as successes.
8. **Then scale** — second pilot, third pilot, more teams. Don't pretend one pilot is the transformation; it's the **first** demonstration, not the destination.

## Inputs & outputs
- **In:** leadership alignment on transformation; a discrete business problem; trio + leadership ready to be coached; training/coaching budget.
- **Out:** a visible business outcome attributable to the pilot; concrete learnings about what the model means for *your* org (the abstract becomes tangible); proof-of-concept the rest of the org has watched.

## Pitfalls
- **Picking a too-easy problem.** Stakeholders shrug; the pilot proves nothing.
- **Picking a too-hard problem.** Pilot fails for reasons unrelated to the model (e.g. replatforming dependency). Sets the transformation back.
- **No measurable business outcome.** "Improved morale" or "shipped faster" without a real KPI move is hard to defend to skeptics.
- **No coaching available.** Pilot teams regress under stress. A [[product-coach|coach]] (internal or external) needs to be reachable for the first months.
- **Large team that looks expensive.** Stakeholders perceive the model as adding overhead. Keep it small.
- **Long problem-discovery phase.** Cagan: trust the stakeholder framing of the problem; race to a solution. If the problem framing is wrong, you'll see it in solution testing within days, not months.
- **Treating the pilot as the whole transformation.** The pilot is a learning device. After it produces outcomes, the next question is what changes for the rest of the org — not "are we done?"
- **No senior sponsor.** Pilots without executive cover get squeezed by the larger org's funding, hiring, and review processes.

## How it relates to transformation politics
Pilot teams are the **political mechanism** of transformation. They:
- Let skeptical executives see *what good looks like* without committing the whole org.
- Give the product leader a place to grow trust before exposing more product managers (the [[product-leadership|product leader is judged by her weakest product manager]]).
- Concentrate the risk of early failure on a contained team rather than across the company.
- Produce the outcomes that fund continued investment in the transformation.

## Where it sits in the SVPG series
INSPIRED is the **first volume** of what the SVPG canon now treats as a four-book arc: **[[inspired|INSPIRED]] → [[empowered|EMPOWERED]] → [[loved|LOVED]] → [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]**. INSPIRED focuses on the **product manager and the product team**; EMPOWERED on the **product leaders**; LOVED on **product marketing**; TRANSFORMED on **moving to the model**. TRANSFORMED refines and codifies material that first appeared here (INSPIRED introduced the four discovery questions and Cagan's PM job description; TRANSFORMED restates them inside a formal 20-principle spine). Where INSPIRED is the origin of an idea, cite it as **primary/root** alongside TRANSFORMED.

## What it covers
The work of the modern product manager and cross-functional product team — the "right people, right product, right process, right culture." Audience: product managers, aspiring PMs, and the design/engineering peers they work with. Cagan explicitly targets **technology-powered products and services**, and treats every technology-powered company (retailer, media co, bank, healthcare) as a tech company.

## Key contributions grounded here as primary
- **The four risks — origin.** INSPIRED Ch 8 introduces discovery as answering **four critical questions**: *Will the user buy this (or choose to use it)? Can the user figure out how to use this? Can our engineers build this? Can our stakeholders support this?* — the source of what TRANSFORMED later restates as [[the-four-big-risks|value / usability / feasibility / viability]]. INSPIRED already uses "valuable, usable, feasible, and viable" verbatim (Ch 8; Ch 34 "Testing Business Viability"; Ch 6 the "two inconvenient truths"). Cite INSPIRED as **primary/root** for the four risks alongside TRANSFORMED.
- **MVP must be a prototype, not a product.** Ch 8's most-quoted assertion: *"The P in MVP stands for product, [but] an MVP should never be an actual product… The MVP should be a prototype, not a product."* Backs [[prototypes]], [[minimize-waste]], [[build-to-learn-vs-build-to-earn]].
- **Continuous discovery and delivery.** Ch 8 framing: two ongoing, parallel activities on every product team — discovery (PM + designer, engineers helping daily) and delivery (engineers, PM + designer helping daily). Backs [[product-discovery]] and [[product-delivery]].
- **The two inconvenient truths.** Ch 6: *"at least half of our ideas are just not going to work"* (good teams assume **three quarters** won't perform as hoped), and *"even with the ideas that do prove to have potential, it typically takes several iterations… we call that [[time-to-money|time to money]]."* Root of [[minimize-waste]] and [[time-to-money]].
- **The product manager's four deep-knowledge areas.** Ch 10: **customer · data · business · market/industry** — plus **smart, creative, persistent** and a ~2–3 month ramp with dedicated coaching. The "when a product fails, it's the PM's fault; when it succeeds, everyone did their job" framing. Backs [[product-manager]].
- **PM ≠ project manager, PM ≈ CEO (of the product, not a boss).** Ch 10 clearly articulates both — including that PM winning ideas rarely come from users or sales but from *intense collaboration with design and engineering*.
- **Team of missionaries, not mercenaries.** Ch 9 origin of the [[missionaries-vs-mercenaries]] phrase Cagan attributes to John Doerr; also grounded in Chs 31 (Evangelism), 45 (Hack Days), and 61 (Managing Stakeholders).
- **Good Product Team / Bad Product Team.** Ch 64 contrast — the wiki's [[feature-team-vs-empowered-team|feature-team-vs-empowered-team]] diagnostic is built on it (also carded as [[2014-06-13-cagan-good-product-team-bad-product-team|the SVPG article]]).
- **Pilot Team Technique.** Ch 59 is the book's dedicated treatment of what the wiki captures on [[pilot-teams]]. INSPIRED grounds the pilot-team canon that TRANSFORMED then folds into transformation strategy.
- **Opportunity Assessment (10 questions).** Ch 35 is the primary text for the [[opportunity-assessment]] framework.
- **Prototype taxonomy.** Chs 45–49 name the **four prototype types** grounded on [[prototypes]] (Feasibility · User/UX · Live-Data · Hybrid).
- **Head of Product · Head of Technology · Delivery Manager.** Chs 17, 18, 19 are the primary treatments of these leadership roles — Ch 19 in particular is the root of the [[delivery-manager]] competency (rebranding "project manager" to "delivery manager").
- **Customer Discovery Program.** Ch 39 is the origin of the technique TRANSFORMED Ch 23 lists as one of the eight PM ↔ PMM collaboration areas.

# Product Leadership

> The **managers and leaders of product management, product design, and engineering**. Empowered teams need *better* leadership, not less ([[2020-11-19-cagan-product-leadership-is-hard|Cagan, "Product Leadership Is Hard"]]). Canon: leadership has two halves — **management** (coaching + staffing) and **leadership** (providing the strategic context teams need to make good decisions). The long-form reference is [[empowered|EMPOWERED]].

## What "product leadership" means here
"Product leadership" refers to the **leaders of all three crafts** in a product organization — head of product, head of design, head of engineering — and the layers beneath them. Most leaders carry both management and leadership responsibilities; the mix tilts toward more leadership as seniority increases.

## Management responsibilities — coaching and staffing
- **Coaching** is "the *single most important responsibility* of every people manager." First-level managers typically spend ~50% of their week on it ([[2020-11-19-cagan-product-leadership-is-hard|canon]]). The mechanism is [[product-coaching]]: assess each person's skills ([[pm-competency]]), build a plan ([[coaching-the-pm]]), and run weekly 1:1s. Each person deserves a manager committed to growing their craft — engineers under engineering managers, designers under design managers, PMs under proven PM managers.
- **Staffing** — sourcing, recruiting, interviewing, onboarding, evaluating, promoting, and (when needed) replacing team members. HR supports; managers own. Bezos-line: "setting the bar high in our approach to hiring has been, and will be, the single most important element of Amazon's success."

## Leadership responsibilities — providing the strategic context
Empowered teams need *strategic context* to make good decisions. Senior leaders own the company-level context (mission, annual objectives); **product leaders own the rest**, and it is *not* an IC contribution:

1. **[[product-vision|Product Vision]] and Product Principles** — the future you're trying to create (3–10 years out, customer-improving) and the values/ethics/strategic preferences that guide trade-offs. The vision is the org's **shared goal** and recruiting tool; principles complement it.
2. **[[team-topology|Team Topology]]** — how work is split across teams to maximize empowerment: **loosely coupled, highly aligned**. One of the hardest decisions; sits with **head of product + head of technology together**.
3. **[[product-strategy|Product Strategy]]** — focus → insights → action → management; the lever that lets a small org outperform a much larger one. The output is a set of [[team-objectives|team objectives]].
4. **[[team-objectives|Team Objectives]]** — the 1–2 significant problems each team is asked to solve in the quarter. **The litmus test for empowerment** is that the team decides *how* to solve the problem.
5. **Ongoing evangelism** — communicating the strategic context relentlessly, in recruiting, onboarding, 1:1s, all-hands, board meetings, customer briefings. Evangelism is never "done."

**Lead by context, not control** (Netflix mantra Cagan endorses).

### Founder-style leadership and "experts lead experts"
Cagan's fullest recent statement of what "better leadership" means is [[founder-style-leadership]] — **[[product-sense|product sense]] + coaching** — his reframing of "Founder Mode" as neither micromanagement nor its hands-off opposite ([[2025-03-03-cagan-why-your-product-operating-model-is-broken|"Why Your Product Operating Model is Broken"]]). Corollaries:
- **Experts lead experts.** The prerequisite for leading a craft is knowing it; the "people-only manager" who administers salaries but can't help with the actual work is a toxic anti-pattern. Domain expertise, though, must not curdle into *domain dogma* (Shreyas Doshi) — distinguish law from lore.
- **The time split** ([[2024-04-14-cagan-escape-the-project-trap-agile-theatre|Escape the Project Trap]]): first-level leaders spend ~80% on coaching + staffing and ~20% on strategy; a CPO/CTO is the reverse (~80% strategic context). And **product strategy belongs to leaders, not teams** — 30 teams can't each own the holistic strategy without producing anarchy; teams own discovery and delivery strategy, informed by their insights.
- **The two things that get in the way of good work** ([[2024-06-01-product-collective-what-every-product-leader-needs-to-know|breakdown of the INDUSTRY keynote]]): people don't know *how* to do good work (→ coaching), or they aren't motivated (→ vision / strategic context) — exactly the two leadership duties above. And "you cannot be a good manager without being a good coach" ([[bill-campbell]]).

### Management vs leadership, and the leader's agency
- **Andy Grove's two failure modes** ([[2024-02-14-cagan-getting-leadership-up-to-speed-melissa-perri|Getting Leadership Up to Speed]]): organizations can't do good work for only two reasons — people **don't know how** (→ management/coaching) or **don't want to** (→ leadership/motivation). "You'll be judged by your weakest product manager."
- **Technology as cost center vs profit center.** The deepest transformation blocker is a CEO who sees technology as a **cost center** (Ford — a necessary evil) rather than a **profit center / enabler** (Tesla — "a computer on four wheels"). Digital-transformation efforts that default to automation and cost-cutting betray the cost-center mindset.
- **Leaders have more agency than they think.** Counsel to leaders stuck in a stakeholder-driven org: don't "fight back" against the CEO (you'll lose) — **demonstrate**. Raise your teams' skills, reframe stakeholder asks as problems-to-solve, and *show* the company outcomes it hasn't seen before ([[2024-03-28-cagan-hardest-challenges-product-strategy-product-direction|canon]]). "The real problem in most companies is not the CEO saying no — it's the head of product not knowing how to do this well."
- **The operator trap.** Of Shreyas Doshi's visionary / craftsman / operator leader types, the dangerous operator scales with **process** rather than **coaching** — the [[process-people|process-people disease]] ([[2022-12-07-cagan-product-lessons-jobs-musk-20vc|canon]]; see [[2021-10-28-cagan-process-people|Cagan's 2021 article]] for the third-contributor-type framing, and [[principles-over-process]] for the response). Product ops is not a substitute for leadership: standing up a product-ops org or a new CPO title to "check the box" is "rearranging deck chairs" — see [[product-ops]].

### In the exemplars (case studies)
- **Amazon — Single-Threaded Leaders.** Durable, empowered leaders with strong [[product-sense|product sense]]; "leaders develop leaders and take seriously their role in coaching others." Cagan: "more than anything else … the key to Amazon's ongoing success" ([[amazon-prime]]).
- **Google — "experts lead experts."** The **Tech Lead Manager** and **Group Product Manager** are technically-competent management units, so no separate "coordinator" layer is needed — the concrete embodiment of *better* management, not less. GPMs are "true [[missionaries-vs-mercenaries|missionaries]]" who earned the role through years of product success ([[google-product-model]]).
- **Apple — the cautionary counter-case.** When product judgment rests on **one** leader (Steve Jobs) and no durable product competency is built beneath, product quality is one departure away from collapse. The [[apple-role-of-product|Role of Product at Apple]] case is the succession/"CEO as head of product" warning — institutionalize product leadership (as Amazon/Google do) rather than resting it on a single person.

## Retired proposal — The "Product Council" ([[inspired|INSPIRED]] Ch 14, 1st ed, 2008)
> **Voice: wiki synthesis of canon lineage.**

[[inspired|INSPIRED]] 1st ed Ch 14 proposed a **Product Council** — a cross-functional executive body (CEO/COO, VPs of PM/design/marketing/engineering/site-ops/customer-service) that met to make product decisions across four milestones per effort: (1) select opportunities → (2) review opportunity assessments and issue go/no-go on discovery → (3) review prototypes + user-testing + cost estimates and issue go/no-go on engineering → (4) review final product + launch plans and issue go/no-go on launch. Cagan credited the origin to **Maynard Webb** (then eBay COO) and framed the council as a way to give management the visibility/oversight they needed *without* micromanaging.

**Cagan largely retired this proposal in later canon.** In [[empowered]] and [[transformed]] the executive-level product decision function is instead distributed across (a) [[product-leadership|product leaders]] providing [[strategic-context|strategic context]] (vision, principles, topology, strategy, team objectives, evangelism), (b) empowered [[the-product-team-trio|trios]] making the solution and prioritization decisions, and (c) the [[partnering-with-executives|six-element executive partnership]] (Decisions · Outcomes · Disagreements · Promises · Surprises · Trust) that keeps executives *frequently, high-quality-ly* involved without the four-milestone go/no-go gating. The 2008 four-milestone gate is a **project-model artifact** the model actively replaces. The one strand of Ch 14 that persists is *"leadership owns strategic context; teams own solutions"* — but it lives on those pages, not on a "Product Council" page. **Cite Ch 14 as `[[inspired]] Ch 14 (1st ed, 2008)` and flag as superseded**; don't create a `product-council` concept page.

## Common failure modes
- **Empowerment-as-absence** — interpreting "empowered teams" as backing off. Empowered teams need *better* leadership, not less.
- **Skipping strategic context** — pushing decisions down without giving teams what they need to decide well. Teams flail and revert to feature-team behavior.
- **Coaching <10% of the week** — the manager isn't actually managing; they're an IC with a direct-report inbox.
- **HR-led hiring** — leaders defer staffing to HR; the bar drops; the rest of the model unravels because empowered teams require competent people.
- **Topology by accident** — Conway's-Law team boundaries instead of a deliberate, dependency-minimising design. Pair with [[team-topology]].
- **"Communicated" vision** — leadership thinks vision is done after one town hall. Evangelism is constant.
- **No real product strategy** — the org has a business strategy and a roadmap but no product strategy ([[2022-09-23-moore-changing-how-you-decide-which-problems-to-solve|canon]]).
- **The bigger gap than the trio.** In many transformations the *leadership* gap is larger than the IC gap — often the org doesn't really have product leadership at all yet.

## Relationships
- **The trio.** Leaders staff and coach the trio; they don't replace the trio's discovery work. If leaders are still telling teams *how* to solve problems, empowerment is theater.
- **Product coaches.** External [[product-coach|product leadership coaches]] are the typical lift for transformation-era leaders whose own coaching network is thin; SVPG's human-coach network now concentrates here ([[product-coaching]]).
- **CEO and CFO/CIO.** Many transformation failures originate at the C-suite ([[2021-04-27-cagan-product-vs-project-teams|project-team root causes]]). Product leadership reaches across to finance and the business owners to unwind project-based funding.

## In transformation contexts
Cagan: "product leaders really are the key to successful transformation" ([[2024-11-21-cagan-transformation-politics|canon]]). Specific political dimensions to remember:
- **"The product leader is judged by her weakest product manager."** Be careful which PMs are exposed to stakeholders, and when.
- **Start with humility** — initial stakeholder meetings should be about *learning* their concerns and constraints, not selling the new model.
- **Trust stakeholders on the problem** in pilots; race to a successful solution. Long problem-discovery against stakeholder will is politically toxic and usually unnecessary.
- **Hold the *Decide* dimension change for last** — change how the org *solves* and *builds* first; let those wins earn the trust to take on *decide*.
- See [[transformation-politics]] for the full eight-dimension framing, and [[overcoming-objections]] for the 11 constituency-specific objection patterns Cagan enumerates in [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Part X (Chs 36–46). Ch 38 (Objections from the CEO and Board) and Ch 44 (Objections from the CIO) are the most direct product-leadership material — CEOs/board on connecting product work to business outcomes without a roadmap, CIOs on the cost-center → profit-center mindset shift.

## The CEO's role — Ch 5
Cagan's Ch 5 of *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* is his sharpest short statement of why the CEO's disposition matters for transformation. *"Probably the most important lesson we have learned in 20 years of helping companies with transformations to the product model is the absolutely critical role that the CEO needs to play."* The nuance:
- **The CEO does *not* need prior product-model experience**, and does not need to spend a lot of time on the transformation itself.
- **But every CEO will say she supports the transformation** — *"most don't truly realize what this means until much later in the effort."*
- **The scope is wider than product/engineering.** Transformation impacts sales, marketing, finance, HR, legal, business development, compliance, and manufacturing. Not every leader in those areas will be enthusiastic; some will passively resist, and a few will actively resist. They all report to the CEO, and *"that's whom they will look to decide if this is truly important and necessary."*
- **The [[bill-campbell|Bill Campbell]] line Cagan grounds this on:** *"The company cares about what the leader cares about."*
- **The delegation anti-pattern:** *"Too often, the CEO delegates ownership of the transformation to a CIO, a chief digital officer, or a chief transformation officer. And while these individuals can influence decisions and actions within their organization, unless the stakeholders report to them (which is rarely the case), this same problem will result."* Consistent with the [[keys-to-successful-transformation|Ten Keys' Key #1]] naming this as the *"digital-transformation-VP"* anti-pattern.
- **The blunt guidance:** *"If this is something your CEO is unwilling or unable to do, then you can likely save yourself a lot of time, money, and effort by reassessing your readiness."* Feeds [[transformation-readiness]] Lens 2 (leadership readiness).
- **The upside case for the CEO:** a company operating in the product model improves the situation for everyone — pride in products, more for marketing to position, more for sales to sell, financial impact, morale, retention. There's *"a strong case to be made to justify the active help and support of the CEO."*

## The executive partnership (Ch 26 → framework page)
Part VI, Ch 26 of *TRANSFORMED* is Cagan's fullest treatment of the *product-team ↔ executive* relationship — organized around six named elements: **Decisions · Outcomes · Disagreements · Promises · Surprises · Trust**. The wiki treats this as its own framework page: [[partnering-with-executives]]. It's the coaching artifact for a product leader establishing (or repairing) the working model with an executive team. Ch 26's opening premise is worth remembering when coaching leaders who conflate empowerment with executive-back-off: *"'Please just back off and give the product teams space to do their work'… ignores the reality that executives have very real needs when it comes to running the company responsibly and effectively."* In the product model teams need *frequent, high-quality* executive interaction — the **nature**, not the frequency, changes.

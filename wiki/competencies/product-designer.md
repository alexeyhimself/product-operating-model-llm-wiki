# Product Designer

> In an [[empowered-product-teams|empowered product team]], the product designer is **accountable for usability risk** and the **holistic customer experience** — every interaction users and customers have with the product. The designer is a member of the [[the-product-team-trio|trio]], not a screen-production service. Canon: [[2023-03-15-cagan-product-model-competencies|Cagan, Product Model Competencies]] (the risk ownership) and [[2016-08-30-jones-the-product-designer-role|Jones, The Product Designer Role]] (the five attributes of the modern designer).

## Accountabilities
- **Usability risk** — the designer personally owns the question: can the user easily learn, use, and perceive the value of the solution? (One of the [[the-four-big-risks|four big risks]].)
- **Holistic experience (UX > UI)** — every touchpoint, across the customer journey, before and after first use. UX is bigger than UI; some teams call it "customer experience" to make the point.
- **Prototyping** — designers own prototypes as the team's *primary canvas* for communicating and validating ideas. Modern prototyping rarely requires engineers.
- **User testing** — built into the weekly cadence. Broader than usability testing; designers also test **value** (will customers actually use or buy this?).
- **Interaction + visual design** — both, with working competence in each. Strengths can vary, but a designer who can only do one half of the craft is a half designer.

> **What the designer is *not*:** an "internal agency" handed PM requirements to mock up; a screen factory measured on velocity; a "UI specialist" who hands UX off to someone else; a usability-only role with no opinion on value.

## The five attributes of the modern designer (Jones, 2016)
1. **Product orientation** — continuous collaboration with PM and engineers across all phases; sits with the trio; measured on **product success**, not design output.
2. **Holistic experience design** — broad view of the customer journey across all touchpoints and over time. Considers onboarding, sharing, support, offline service, perceived responsiveness.
3. **Prototyping** — uses prototypes as the primary canvas for communicating intent. Comfortable with multiple prototyping tools at multiple fidelities.
4. **User testing** — weekly cadence; tests value as well as usability; collects insights not specifically being looked for.
5. **Interaction and visual design** — both, with the toolset to work at different fidelities. Especially important in mobile where the two are deeply entangled.

## Common failure modes
- **Internal-agency mode** — recreating the external-agency model in-house: requirements come down, mocks go up. Loses every benefit of in-house design.
- **UI-only practitioner** — treats UI as the whole job; the team has no one watching the broader UX.
- **Late involvement** — pulled in after PM has "defined the problem." By then most of the high-leverage solution decisions have already been made implicitly.
- **Skips user testing** — only tests when a prototype is "ready"; misses the steady flow of insights weekly testing produces.
- **Treats prototyping as engineer territory** — ships a screenshot to engineers rather than a prototype. Loses the communication fidelity prototypes provide.

## Relationships
- **The trio.** Equal partner to [[product-manager]] (value + viability) and the [[engineers|tech lead]] (feasibility). Designs *with* the team, not *for* the team. The whole trio owns UX — the designer leads it.
- **PM boundary.** Prototyping, wireframing, and user research are **the designer's job, not the PM's** (Cagan is explicit on this in [[2020-06-12-cagan-product-management-start-here|Product Management – Start Here]]). PMs who do design either don't understand the designer role or don't understand the PM role — or both.
- **Engineers.** Engineer participation in prototyping and user testing is encouraged; the more directly engineers see real users, the more innovation surfaces ([[empowered-engineers]]).
- **Leadership & coaching.** Designers are coached by experienced design managers; treat design as a craft worth coaching ([[product-coaching]]).

# Product Coach

> An experienced product person who develops *others* — product teams, product leaders, or a transformation's senior leadership. Defined by [[marty-cagan|Cagan]] in [[2023-01-11-cagan-becoming-a-product-coach|Becoming a Product Coach]] (2023). The defining line: a coach teaches you **how to do the thing**, and does not do it for you ([[coaching-vs-contracting]]).

## Accountabilities
- **Develop the person, not the deliverable.** Raise a PM's, leader's, or team's level of competence so they can do strong product work themselves — the opposite of a contractor or fractional leader who produces the artifact. See [[coaching-vs-contracting]].
- **Hold them to the model's principles**, diagnose where they are, and prescribe what to try next — for a PM, via the [[pm-competency|assessment]] → [[coaching-the-pm|coaching plan]] loop.
- **Earn trust.** Especially at the leadership/transformation altitude, the relationship *is* the work; nothing changes until the coach has earned the client's trust.

## Prerequisites (Cagan's hard line)
1. **Relevant product experience — non-negotiable.** "I do not see how a person can be an effective product coach without actual, relevant product experience." This is why an agile coach who was never a PM/designer rarely helps with [[product-discovery|discovery]], and why a CEO won't trust a transformation to someone who hasn't "been there and done that." Generic skills (listening, difficult conversations, growth mindset) help *only on top of* that foundation.
2. **A genuine desire to help others** — not flexibility, being your own boss, or variety. Without it, motivation doesn't survive the lean early years.
3. **Runway** — typically 2–3 years to reach consistent revenue; alternatives are in-house coaching, joining an agency, or moonlighting.

## Competencies — weak vs strong
| Competency | Weak | Strong |
|---|---|---|
| Foundation | "Agile/career coach" with no product experience | Years in the trenches at strong (or many) product companies; deep grasp of principles, concepts, techniques |
| Mode | Does the work for the client (contractor/fractional) | Develops the client to do the work themselves ([[coaching-vs-contracting]]) |
| Focus | Everything to everyone | One [[types-of-product-coaching|type of coaching]] for one type of client; a clear "superpower" |
| Relationship | One-off engagement | Ongoing relationship that builds trust and develops judgment over months |
| Responsiveness | Replies when convenient | Responds to every inquiry within 24 hours (Cagan's bar) |

## Types of product coach
Three primary types, by altitude — see [[types-of-product-coaching]] for the full treatment:
- **Discovery coach** — the foundation; helps product teams learn to discover solutions worth building. Most demand. (Former PMs/designers.)
- **Product leadership coach** — vision, team topology, strategy, objectives, staffing/coaching. (Former heads of product/technology.)
- **Transformation coach** — guides senior leadership through the culture/funding/staffing change from project to product. (Former senior leaders the CEO will trust.)

## Common failure modes
- **Coaching without experience** — the agile/career/management "coach" with no product background; "rarely helpful."
- **Slipping into contracting** — taking deliverable engagements (write our strategy, be our fractional CPO) instead of coaching; doesn't scale and doesn't develop anyone ([[coaching-vs-contracting]]).
- **Starting with training courses** — productized cohorts/bootcamps depend on a base of clients and expertise built first; new coaches who lead with courses "struggle to fill seats."
- **Being everything to everyone** — no focus, no clear superpower, weak positioning.
- **Blind spots about one's own ability** — many fail by overestimating their qualifications; Cagan's antidote is an honest assessment from someone clearly qualified.

## Reference — the business of coaching
For completeness (not core to the model): coaches structure as **independent / in-house / partnership / agency** ([[svpg|SVPG]] is a partnership). Cagan stresses *you are your product* — a strong LinkedIn profile and a website that answers the client's ten questions (how do I find you, who are you, your superpower, testimonials, how you help, trust, responsiveness…). Pricing favors **quarterly subscriptions** (~$10–25K/client/quarter, 4–7 clients) and **packaged outcomes** (discovery/vision/strategy sprints) over hourly/daily billing — "they are paying for your years of hard-earned experience," not face time.

## Relationship to model-as-product-coach
[[model-as-product-coach]] is Cagan's Feb 2026 answer to the coach *shortage* this article diagnoses — a foundation model configured with project files + instructions + strategic context can serve "at least as good as most managers." But two points from this article temper that: the experience prerequisite is *why* the model needs strong project files (this wiki) and explicit instruction to prioritize the [[overview|product model]]; and the [[coaching-vs-contracting|coaching-vs-contracting]] line is exactly the [[model-as-product-coach|PM-theater]] trap — use the model to *learn to do the work*, not to produce your artifacts.

# Leading Product Marketing

> The competency of **leading** a product-marketing organization: where it reports, how the role's scope is defined at each stage, hiring, career development, and inclusive team norms. [[loved|LOVED]] Part Five (Chs 26–28). *"The four fundamentals anchor the role, but how they work in a company's operating environment is scoped by the managers leading them."*

## Why it matters
Ch 26 opens with **Mala Sharma's shock** arriving in Silicon Valley in 1996: *"No one knew daily or weekly sales figures. There was no accountability between a market action and its business impact. The metric for success for product marketing (or marketing for that matter) seemed to be: if product collateral was getting done, everything was good enough."*

Mala had trained as a Unilever brand manager in India — a virtual general manager who knew weekly sales, costs, and key business drivers. She realized *"no one around her had any idea of the strategic opportunities product marketing could unlock simply by better understanding customer sentiment, competitors, and markets."*

In her first PMM role she kicked off quantitative + qualitative customer research, found a gamer niche that changed packaging + pricing + GTM strategy. The product's success *"changed how her company thought about the role."*

At Adobe as Director of PMM for Photoshop, PMs did feature planning then handed to PMM for messaging + sales enablement. Mala showed up at every meeting to understand market opportunity better than anyone: *"How do we grow this business? Is pricing an issue? What's the product's go-to-market? She modeled what strategic product marketing looks like in every meeting and started shifting perceptions."*

**Mala's insight for the wiki:**
> *"Product marketers succeed when they are willing to challenge a conversation based on customer insights. They, of course, need the data, market and customer insights to do that. They must be unafraid to put difficult choices on the table for teams with whom they collaborate. This, in turn, constantly improves product go-to-market thinking and empowers product marketing to find new ways to unlock business growth."*

## Where should PMM report? (Ch 26)
Ch 26's two-factor answer:

### Factor 1 — What business problems are you trying to solve?

**Organize under marketing when:**
- Product is well-established in market; growth requires refined customer segmentation.
- Marketing strategies (partnerships, product suites) and much more GTM coordination.
- PMM acts as an overlay across products.
- Markets + customers drive GTM thinking; products become a portfolio.

**Organize under product when:**
- Continuous development, highly technical products, or the company struggles to communicate about its product.
- Reduces friction for PMM to get better product info to GTM teams.
- Easier for PMMs to be voice of market to product teams.
- **Added benefit:** helps increase product teams' market acumen.

### Factor 2 — Which leader has the capacity to help PMM reach its potential?
*"If you're lucky enough to have an extraordinary leader with a unique combination of superpowers — either their experience or leadership capabilities span multiple areas — take advantage."*

- **Market-savvy CPO** who sees more strategically than the CMO → put PMM under the CPO even if markets are mature.
- **CMO with deep credibility with product leadership** → have PMM report to that CMO to model healthy collaboration.

Ch 26: *"The credibility lent to the function by reporting to an extraordinary leader is part of what helps the function succeed."*

## Defining the scope of the role (Ch 26)
Ch 26's staging heuristic:
- **Early product life cycle** → the versatile generalist is very valuable; broad application of discovery + hands-on marketing.
- **As companies mature + repeatable GTM emerges** → PMMs specialize around vertical markets, marketing/distribution channels, or customer segments.

**Team size:** grows with product success + market complexity. Mature multi-product co: PMM specialists per core-GTM area · a PMM for a major vertical · PMMs per major customer segment.

**Ch 26's rule:** *"Define the role's scope by what is most important for where the business is going and how the team is organized. Then communicate it clearly to teams outside of product marketing."*

**Ch 26's "PMM reorg" advice:** don't start with a full reorg. Pair your best product leader with your best PMM leader in your most important market segment. Have them try the model + tools. Adjust for the org's unique situation, then roll out a version that works. *"Gives a working reference point for everyone to understand how product marketing is intended to function."*

## Ch 26's role-fixing shifts (when redefining the PMM charter)

### PMM ↔ PM dysfunction
*"Trust in product marketing's knowledge of the customer and market is foundational for this relationship to work well."*

Symptoms undermining trust: lack of curiosity about the product, data not used in discussions, marketing needs inserted into product discussions seeming random.

Fix mechanics: attend team meetings to observe interactions first-hand; have direct conversations with product counterparts to diagnose. **Focus as much on shifts in processes as on clearer expectations of what "good" looks like for both sides.**

Ch 26 nuance: *"A lot of product managers don't understand just how hard it is to create a product's story, its messaging, or go-to-market. For them, the difference between one choice of words or a particular activity versus another can feel arbitrary."*

### PMM ↔ GTM teams (sales + marketing)
*"No go-to-market plan survives contact with its customers. You can't predict the actions of others, and product marketing must direct its response."*

Frustration when PMM focuses on plan execution more than reacting to what's happening in market. **Fix:** standard processes for how priorities get decided between teams — e.g., weekly meetings where sales can impact how quickly they get a new sales tool or shift the plan.

### PMM ↔ Executive leadership
*"Most executives don't have a clear idea of what product marketing does or should do."*

**Fix:** PMM leaders **define what their team does relative to company priorities** and **connect the GTM actions driven by their teams**. Start with a clear product-GTM plan; specify how activities support business goals; state how the team's work should be measured (shorter-term quarterly · longer-term annual).

## Inclusive team norms (Ch 26)
Ch 26's deepest content. Team composition is not just diverse hiring — it's about **enabling the team to see things they might not otherwise see but deeply matter.**

Ch 26 reference: **Google's Project Aristotle** (2012) — hundreds of teams studied over a year. *"Performance was less about who is on the team and more about how the team worked together."* Five factors correlated to consistently better performance:

| Factor | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Psychological safety** | Confidence that it's safe to take risks with asking questions, offering new ideas, disagreeing, or sharing personal stakes. |
| **Dependability** | Team members reliably complete quality work on time and don't shy away from being held accountable. |
| **Structure + clarity** | Clear understanding of job expectations and what it takes to fulfill them. |
| **Meaning** | Purpose in one's work. Meaning is personal. |
| **Impact** | Feeling that contribution matters + they're making a difference. |

**Why this matters for PMM specifically:** *"Product marketers operate so cross-functionally. They must feel confident to influence outcomes with teams where they don't share a reporting structure. They must feel safe disagreeing."*

Ch 26's leader-behavior list:
- Make quiet people feel heard.
- **Speak last instead of first** to ensure others share their opinions.
- Embrace different points of view even if you don't agree.
- Don't defend against opinions you disagree with.
- Show candor, respect, clarity, and willingness to have hard conversations whenever necessary.

*"When leading product marketing, examine not just the business goals and functions of the role. Look at who sits around a team's table and how the team functions to enable great performance."*

## Hiring PMM talent (Ch 27)
Ch 27's three-candidate framing — **the curious · the communicator · the collaborator** — for an early-stage startup, Lauchengco recommended the curious hire.

**Trade-off framing:** *"Everyone wants to hire unicorns — mythical product marketers who have it all and can bend spoons with their minds. They exist, but they are rare. More common is to optimize a hire around what's most important for your company's stage and how much you can grow or develop skills."*

### Ch 27's assessment moves per Ch 7 skill

| Ch 7 skill | Evidence to look for | Interview question |
|---|---|---|
| **Deep customer curiosity + active listening** | Ability to adapt POV based on new info | *"Can you talk through a time when you had a plan but changed it based on something new you learned?"* Push back on assumptions — do they reconsider? |
| **Product curiosity + technical competence** | Talks about products unprompted; diverse job/industry choices | *"What were you looking for when you jumped from [X] to [Y]? What product do you love right now?"* |
| **Strong on strategy + execution + business savvy** | Business results tied to accomplishments | *"What do you consider your biggest success so far? How did you know it was a success?"* |
| **Collaborative** | Systems, not just relationships, that drive results | *"What is the best product/sales/product marketing relationship you've seen in action? What made it good?"* |
| **Strong written + verbal communication** | Use exercises to probe; presentation exercises | Ask questions that move them off their prepared talk track. |

### The signature assessment question (Ch 27)
Lauchengco's every-candidate question — asked *for nearly 30 years*:
> *"Tell me about a product or company you think is doing really great marketing. It can be anything. And tell me why you think it's great."*

Then the corollary: *"Now pretend you are a marketing leader at a competitor to the 'great marketing' company. What two or three things would you consider most important to do in order to compete with [insert their first named company] well?"*

**What to listen for:**
- **Breadth of marketing toolset.** Do they bring in brand, customers, product, clever campaigns? *"If a candidate doesn't talk much beyond an advertising campaign or can't get specific about why they think a product is better, they've failed. If the candidate only goes through the four P's (product, price, promotion, place) without any original thinking, I consider this a fail."*
- **How do they deal with rapid change?** Ability to think on their feet with new ideas.
- **Are they open to new information?** Shift the target customer or a market factor — does that change anything they do?
- **How do they manage constraints?** Add a budget constraint. *"If you only had a $250,000 budget, would it change what you do and how you prioritize?"*

**Ch 27's "breadcrumbing":** you suggest examples or introduce new data to lead them a bit and see what they do with it.

### Ch 27's calibration
*"At the entry-level stage (straight out of Master's or MBA program or an exceptional undergrad) about one or two in ten are dynamic marketing thinkers. The ratio improves to more like one in five, sometimes one in three when you're working with a really good recruiter for director level or above product marketers."*

*"Hiring well is the most impactful thing leaders can do for product marketing."*

## Career guidance (Ch 28)
Ch 28's staging is compact:

| Stage | Years | What good looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Early career** | 1–5 | Broad, rapid learning; interpret market signals; detailed concrete feedback OK. Lead initiatives (website redesign · campaign strategy). **Next-level readiness:** manage someone (interns count) OR manage complex high-impact projects that visibly move the business forward. |
| **Mid-level (Director)** | 5–12 | Lead across functions; visible leader across the company; lead major launches; multiple product lines or solution marketing. Best leaders teach key marketing skills to others; results eventually come from leading others. **Signal:** requested by name for complex cross-functional projects. |
| **Senior (VP+)** | 10+ | Versatile across scenarios; systems + processes that let teams perform better; act as swiftly as markets demand. **Discussable failure** with lessons learned. |

### The Director-to-VP gap (Ch 28's "I'm a Great Director of Marketing" essay)
Lauchengco's essay — the widely-read blog embedded in Ch 28 — she was the director who wondered why she wasn't a VP. The CEO was Ben Horowitz. Four things she wishes he'd told her:

- **Stop focusing on marketing excellence.** *"Marketing leadership is not about individual marketing abilities — it is about your team's ability to perform and your ability to enable that performance."*
- **The company > the team.** At higher levels, the "we" needs to be cross-functional. *"Are you defining excellence within the company's broader goals and multiple functions' abilities to succeed?"*
- **Redirect to "why" versus "what."** Great marketing leaders spend at least as much time helping the org understand the why as they do planning and reporting.
- **Don't be an expert, be open.** *"Experts feel closed. Leaders feel open."* Balance communicating expertise while inviting participation.

Lauchengco's practical closing: *"If you find yourself butting up against a title ceiling, ask for brutally honest feedback from people who want to see you succeed. Then forge a plan with a peer, coach, mentor, or manager."*

# Adobe — Creative Suite → Creative Cloud

> Cagan calls this *"one of the most impressive transformations I've ever seen"* — and by the numbers (market cap $13B → $269B) *"one of the most financially successful in industry history."* Told first-person by [[lea-hickman|Lea Hickman]], who was appointed head of product management for Creative Cloud. What makes it structurally rare: **the innovation story *drove* the transformation story** — the need to invent Creative Cloud is what forced the company to change how it built, solved, and decided. Source: *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* Part IX (Transformation Story, by Hickman).

## Cast
- **CEO:** Shantanu Narayen — had to make (with the CFO) what Hickman calls *"potentially a bet-the-company bet."*
- **CFO:** Mark Garrett — critical early convert.
- **CTO:** Kevin Lynch — one of three senior leaders who called the offsite that started it.
- **SVP Platform Business:** David Wadhwani.
- **SVP Creative Business:** Johnny Loiacono.
- **Head of product management, Creative Cloud:** Lea Hickman ([[lea-hickman|SVPG partner]] today; this was the transformation she led before joining).
- **Head of engineering:** Kevin Stewart.
- **Company origin:** Founded 1982; through many technology generations (printers, PCs, etc.); when Hickman was there (2007–2014), digital-media portfolio included Adobe InDesign, Illustrator, Dreamweaver, and Flash Authoring.
- **At the time:** Creative Suite was ~$2B+ annual revenue; Adobe was the clear industry leader; publicly traded.

## Starting state — a stick and no carrot yet
Hickman's list of forces:
- **Upgrade-driven revenue model** — data indicated customers might find their existing tools "good enough"; upgrade likelihood declining.
- **Very long product cycles** — originally 18–24 months between major releases; point releases eased revenue recognition but couldn't match competitors who had adopted continuous releases.
- **New competitors** — **Sketch, Aviary** — simpler, modern design tools; their value proposition of *simple, modern* resonated with customers who used only a fraction of Adobe's tools' capabilities.
- **Technology shifts** — iPhone had been out a few years; cloud services rising. *"The idea that the cloud was going to be where users (designers) stored their data, versus their desktop or laptop, was very powerful because it would unshackle users from specific devices."*
- **Mobile authoring debate** — Adobe was evangelizing Flash Authoring + Flex + Adobe Air as a way to build write-once run-anywhere mobile apps; but the industry was diverging: native apps performed better, standards-based frameworks were rising. *"How could we build native applications ourselves when we recommended that our customers build using our proprietary frameworks?"*
- **Buyer + user shift** — print → digital, pro → hobbyist; retail → download.
- **The Steve Jobs open letter (April 29, 2010)** — Apple would not permit Flash on iOS devices. *"A substantial blow"* — Creative Professional customers loved Apple products.

*"Knowing changes are needed is not the same as being willing and able to change… So far all we had was the stick. We knew we also needed a carrot."*

## What changed — offsite + Marissa + all three dimensions

### The senior-leadership offsite
CTO Kevin Lynch, SVP Platform David Wadhwani, SVP Creative Johnny Loiacono called an offsite. Each product category to present and demo new product ideas beyond desktop tool offerings — specifically **mobile apps and hosted services**. At the offsite it became apparent that each area had a *different* vision and none aligned. Leadership realized they needed a **unifying vision** *and* a product leader to drive it. Hickman was asked.

### The Marissa visiontype — the carrot
Hickman: *"our product vision started taking shape once we realized that instead of focusing internally about what we wanted to accomplish for our business, we should instead start talking about designer customers and how we could improve their lives."*

The team wrote an aspirational **"day in the life"** narrative about a freelance graphic designer named **Marissa**, running a small shop, being discovered on creative.adobe.com by a Nike creative director who reached out with a proposal request. Marissa leverages Adobe tablet applications, cloud syncing, and desktop tools to make a compelling proposal; wins the bid; then sources other designers and photographers to help.

**The visiontype became the guiding force.** *"The belief was that if we did the right thing for our designer customers, then they would stay with us and new customers would join us. We knew we had effective mechanisms to capture the value."*

The vision was shared **externally** with key customers and **internally** with stakeholders — this was equally load-bearing, because the required business-model change would ripple through sales, marketing, and finance. **First conversion targets: CEO Shantanu Narayen and CFO Mark Garrett.** *"We knew that if they were not convinced, there was simply no way the rest of the company would go along with such disruptive changes."*

### Changing how they build
- Waterfall product cycles (12–18 months) → **continuous development and delivery**.
- Cloud-based deployment means all customers on the same version — no fall-back to a previous version if something breaks; engineers had to *"constantly keep the service running and working properly."*
- Significant architectural changes in how products were designed, built, tested, deployed.
- Real resistance from strong engineers initially; they stepped up — but Hickman is explicit: *"this was not easy and not without early resistance."*

### Changing how they solve problems
Adobe already had strong engineers, strong designers, and *"an empowered engineers culture"* — but many layers of people between engineers and actual designer customers. Product capabilities often didn't achieve hoped-for adoption.

The change: **cross-functional product teams of PMs, designers, and engineers**, working closely with product marketing, **engaging directly with users** to understand challenges and discover solutions.

Hickman is clear about the PM role change: *"there were already many strong product managers in the organization, but historically their role was more focused on building business cases, capturing requirements, and working with product marketing to prepare for the upcoming release. They also had a significant project management responsibility to report progress on the milestones. But we needed the role to change to be primarily about product discovery and the necessary focus on value and viability."* The PM shift was as material as the delivery shift.

### Changing how they decide
Product strategy identified two major problem areas:
1. **Identity management** — multiple identity systems were cumbersome for users and blocked the usage data needed for personalization and license management. A **common Adobe ID** across content, services, and tools everywhere.
2. **Cloud-based syncing** — files and data from mobile, tablet, cloud, desktop with no loss of data or fidelity.

### Product planning — 20+ products
The Creative Suite was 20+ products. Approach: **migrate some, redesign and rebuild others, strategically acquire** components where the technology or team could accelerate the vision.

**Suite Tax** — cross-team coordination muscle already existed (every product cycle they had shipped both individual products and Suites focused on customer segments). Real collaboration on shared elements (identity, files) was possible because of it.

**Design system with fully coded components** for consistency where required; individual product teams could experiment where consistency wasn't needed.

### Changing how they go to market
The subscription business model change was as consequential as the technology change:
- Old cycle: 12–18 months development + long, complex, coordinated GTM (feature-highlight planning, messaging, positioning, demos, press tours, collateral, packaging).
- Subscription cycle: continuous release means no time for that.
- **Feature discoverability** — no more launch marketing machine to announce features.
- **Direct-to-customer communication** — customers previously informed via press + analyst channels (press tours, industry pundits, favorable reviews driving upgrade decisions). Direct-to-customer meant customers had to discover and learn new capabilities themselves — a new kind of product responsibility.
- Impacted enterprise sales, channel/retail partnerships, product marketing, and more.

## Outcome
Hickman measures the transformation by **customers, revenue, and shareholder value**:
- **Customers: 6M → 26M subscribers** (end of 2021).
- **Revenue: $2B/year → $11.5B** (2021).
- **Market cap: $13B → $269B.**
- *"The business outcomes were clear, and this transformation is considered one of the most financially successful in industry history."*

## Transferable lessons
1. **A "stick and carrot" narrative works.** Hickman gave a name to the two halves: the stick was Sketch, Aviary, mobile, the Steve Jobs Flash letter, download-vs-retail; the carrot was the Marissa visiontype. Executive sponsorship required both — the stick alone won't do it (*"knowing changes are needed is not the same as being willing and able to change"*).
2. **Visiontypes are load-bearing artifacts, not slide decks.** A specific-fictional-customer day-in-the-life narrative outperforms abstract vision statements. Marissa let the CEO, CFO, board, employees, and customers all see the same future.
3. **Business-model change is a first-class transformation dimension.** Adobe's GTM chapter is the reminder that SaaS/subscription changes cascade through sales, channel, PMM, finance, customer communication, and feature discoverability — often as expensive as the product change.
4. **Pre-existing collaboration muscle matters.** The "Suite Tax" — years of shipping integrated Suites — meant cross-team coordination was already possible when identity + syncing required it. Where this muscle doesn't exist, the equivalent has to be built first.
5. **The PM role redefinition is not optional in a transformation.** Even Adobe's already-strong PM population had the wrong job — business cases + requirements + project management + upgrade-release choreography. Discovery + value + viability was the new job. Hickman names this specifically as a change she had to lead.

## In your context
_If your CEO asked you today: "show me the visiontype for where our company is going in three years — the day-in-the-life narrative that will justify a bet-the-company decision" — could you? Marissa is the canonical example of what that artifact looks like._

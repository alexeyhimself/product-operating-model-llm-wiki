# Kaiser Permanente — Get Care Now

> The book's counter-example to the "you can't do this in a regulated industry" objection ([[overcoming-objections|Ch 46]]). A US healthcare and health-coverage nonprofit serving **12M+ patients** launched a nationwide, 24/7, on-demand virtual care solution — **Get Care Now** — **four months from inception to nationwide launch**, three months to the next iteration. Source: *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* Ch 47 (Innovation Story).

## Starting state
Kaiser Permanente is one of the nation's largest nonprofit healthcare + coverage organizations, serving 12M+ patients. In 2019 the digital organization began a *large-scale transformation to the product operating model* — building a digital product + technology group focused initially on **reimagining the patient health journey**. Product teams organized around the patient journey, automating manual processes and streamlining digital experiences, producing measurable improvements in patient satisfaction, medication + care plan adherence, and operational efficiency.

**Then, within a year of launch, COVID-19.** Care delivery had to change almost overnight — public health emergency + significant in-person care limitations. **The transformation was ~12 months old when it was existentially tested.**

## What changed
KP had telehealth options for several years, but they had fundamental constraints:
- **Business-hours only** — after-hours meant urgent care or the ER.
- **Home-market only** — no virtual care when traveling.
- **Inconsistent across markets** — technical, regulatory, clinical, operational constraints.

**The problem: potential for care gaps to increase during the pandemic was very real.** KP needed a solution that delivered *"the right care, at the right time, no matter the market they lived in, or their ability to get to a medical office, or the time of day"* — the eventual **Get Care Now**: a single, nationwide, 24/7, on-demand virtual care solution.

### Discovery structured around the four risks
- **Value / motivation:** meeting patients' needs during pandemic-elevated health risks.
- **Usability (two-sided):**
  - *Patients* — wide range of tech comfort; often experiencing stress at the moment they need care. They needed to understand and *choose* Get Care Now over familiar clinic or ER visits.
  - *Clinicians* — new avenue of care; needed to be confident they could deliver the full patient experience before/during/after the visit; medical, regulatory compliance, and operational needs met.
- **Value-in-context learning:** how long patients would wait for an appointment; which conditions were suited to virtual vs required in-person.
- **Viability (deepest layer):** ensuring a clinician *licensed to provide telehealth in that specific regional market* was available; clinician tech setup for secure at-home or in-office interaction with patients; documentation + handoff practices between clinicians; clinical protocols around **acuity, triage, assessments, and documentation** built into both patient and clinician experiences.
- **Feasibility:** integrating underlying **scheduling** and **EMR** systems; clinician tools; scaling data extraction + aggregation (clinician capacity, appointment schedules, patient health info) for 12M+ patients.

### How the work actually ran
- **Product teams worked side by side with clinicians and business operations** — regional-market partnerships, not central-only work.
- **Continuous ideation and rapid testing** to converge on solutions across the many parties.
- Operational issues addressed one-by-one; feasibility risks last.
- **Gradual rollout across existing markets**, then nationwide.

## Outcome
- **Four months from inception to nationwide launch.** Next iteration deployed three months after launch, using real usage data from patients and clinicians.
- **24/7 access to virtual care nationwide**, every state, from patients' care providers.
- **Member satisfaction 9.6/10.**
- **88% NPS.**
- **36%+ of usage between 5pm–8am** — patients saved from ER visits, ER strain reduced.
- Cagan's frame: *"too many people believe that in regulated industries like healthcare, it is impossible to do this level of technology-powered innovation in any amount of time, let alone in just a few short months. Kaiser Permanente was able to demonstrate this ability to themselves, to their senior leadership, and to the broader industry."*

## What it illustrates
- concepts: [[transformation]] (12-month-old transformation withstood existential pandemic test), [[empowered-product-teams]] (regional-market partnering with clinicians and ops), [[product-discovery]] (structured around the four risks across two user classes — patients and clinicians), [[the-four-big-risks]] (unusually rich viability chapter: licensing per state, clinical protocols, documentation practices), [[team-topology]] (regional teams collaborating with a central digital org), [[collaboration]] (the interior discipline of trio + clinicians + ops).
- principles: [[assess-product-risks]], [[embrace-rapid-experimentation]], [[test-ideas-responsibly]] (in a healthcare context, this is load-bearing), [[collaboration]].
- **Answers the [[overcoming-objections|Ch 46 objection]]:** *"we're in a regulated industry — we can't test with customers / release this often"* — KP is Cagan's proof-of-existence.

## Transferable lesson
Regulated-industry innovation *is* the multi-stakeholder viability chapter — patients, clinicians, operations, licensing/regulatory bodies, EMR + scheduling systems, and safety-critical clinical protocols. What KP did that most healthcare orgs don't: they **worked the viability layer as discovery**, in parallel with usability and feasibility, alongside clinicians and ops in each regional market — rather than treating regulation as a wall to be handled last by compliance/legal. Also: a **12-month-old transformation was enough** — Get Care Now didn't require mature process, just working discovery + delivery muscles.

## In your context
_If your industry has visible regulatory constraints, are your product teams treating regulation as a wall to work around (compliance/legal handles it) or as a **viability discovery input** (compliance/legal are discovery partners)? Kaiser's model is the latter._

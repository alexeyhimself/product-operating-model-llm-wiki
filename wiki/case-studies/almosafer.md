# Almosafer — Istiraha Marketplace

> The Middle East's clearest existence proof that the product model works far from Silicon Valley. A Saudi/regional travel company (part of Seera Group) that had spent 2018–2020 transforming from brick-and-mortar agencies into a real product organization used those muscles, when COVID closed borders, to invent an entirely new marketplace category (rentable *istiraha* courtyards) — a *new solution to a new problem*. Source: *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* Ch 14 (Innovation Story).

## Starting state
Almosafer began as brick-and-mortar travel agencies in the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, serving personal, business, government, and religious travel across the region. Post-internet, large global travel providers had economies of scale and technology, but did little to accommodate local needs, customs, or behaviors. Product leader **Ronnie Varghese** and technology leader **Qais Amori** decided in 2018 that competing meaningfully required a digital team capable of *discovering and delivering solutions superior to the global providers*. The transformation began in 2018.

By early 2020 the company had built *"several new and essential muscles"*: continuous customer interviews, data collection + analysis, and rapid prototyping — the ability to discover solutions their customers loved because they met local needs the global players didn't understand. In flights alone the business had grown to $1B. Then the pandemic closed borders.

## What changed
The pandemic exposed an underlying customer need the teams had already spotted: families and friends needed a safe way to gather. In Saudi culture, an ***istiraha*** — "resting place," a large open courtyard for grilling, cool shade, family gatherings — was the traditional venue. There was no registry, no listing, no way to reserve. And critically, unlike hotels or airlines, istirahas are rented afternoon-to-evening on the *same day*.

The team recognized the strategic difficulty upfront: *"the vast majority of products are new solutions to existing problems; this was a new solution to a new problem."* The market itself had to be developed — supply, awareness, buying behavior — in parallel with the product. Cross-team work with business development and marketing was necessary to build supply, while product ran problem + solution discovery in the demand direction. Product coach **Hope Gurion** (later profiled in Ch 34) worked with the team. Discovery and delivery were done remotely; the trust and skills they'd already built made the remote shift viable.

## Outcome
Product-market fit was demonstrated for the new marketplace category, then growth. Istirahas became *"an integral and rapidly growing category of the marketplace."* Almosafer holds ~**70%+ regional market share** across travel today, and continues expanding to more countries in the Middle East. Beyond financials, the case was framed by the book as *proof* that talent in a region not known for tech innovation can, when working in the product model, produce results equal to anywhere in the world.

## Transferable lesson
The muscles built during a normal transformation are what let a company execute an *innovation* under crisis. Almosafer's 2018 transformation didn't pay off primarily through istirahas; it paid off through *the team's ability to invent istirahas*. Also: a *new solution to a new problem* is much harder than the base case — market development runs in parallel with discovery, and the coaching + trust to run that in a remote-forced environment must be pre-existing, not stood up when needed. Cagan's regional point ("talent can be found all over the world") is the wiki's counter to "the model won't work here."

## In your context
_When the next crisis hits your org, will your teams have the discovery, delivery, and cross-functional muscles to invent something new — or will they be trying to build them under pressure? Almosafer's answer to that question predated the pandemic by two years._

# Datasite (Merrill Corporation) — Sales-Driven → Product-Driven

> The book's transformation story for the class of company most people believe *cannot* transform: an old, family-founded, sales-driven, financial-services company. Marty told [[christian-idiodi|Christian Idiodi]] he could place him as head of product at almost any marquee company — Christian chose Merrill Corporation because he wanted to prove it was possible. Together with CEO Rusty Wiley, CPO peer Thomas Fredell, and head of engineering Jeremiah Ivan, they transformed Merrill into **Datasite** — an M&A SaaS platform now considered an industry leader. Sources: *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* Part VII (Transformation Story, by Idiodi) + Ch 35 (Innovation Story: patented redaction).

## Cast
- **CEO:** Rusty Wiley — sponsored the transformation after a prior outsourced transformation attempt failed.
- **CPO / product leader:** Christian Idiodi ([[christian-idiodi|SVPG partner]] today; this was the transformation he ran before joining).
- **CPO peer / cross-functional partner:** Thomas Fredell.
- **Head of engineering:** Jeremiah Ivan (had worked with both Idiodi and Fredell before).
- **Trusted internal ally:** Doug Cullen, head of sales — helped Idiodi name the root cause.
- **Company origin:** Founded 1968 in Minneapolis by Roger Merrill and his wife. Financial printing services (annual reports, prospectuses, proxy statements, SEC filings), document management, and virtual data rooms.
- **Scale at intervention:** 4,000+ employees across 23 countries, grown by acquisition rather than organically.

## Starting state — five compounding problems
Idiodi's diagnosis of Merrill on arrival:
1. **Heavy reliance on legacy business.** Financial printing declining as digital took over.
2. **Limited technological innovation.** Lagged competitors on new products.
3. **Legacy operating model.** Project-based execution + command-and-control approach limiting motivation and innovation.
4. **High debt.** Limited investment capacity.
5. **Reputation damage.** Lawsuits + regulatory investigations around data breaches and info-security.

**Idiodi's insight: those were symptoms.** The root causes were three:

### A slow-moving train — the sales-driven root cause
Head of sales Doug Cullen to Idiodi: *"Merrill is, first and foremost, a sales-driven company… salespeople own the clients, they also own everything else. If we decide to solve a customer problem, it's only because the salespeople want it solved. If we decide to build a new piece of technology, it's only because the salespeople want it built. Sales takes the lead in everything, and everyone else simply follows."*

**Emblematic story: the 7th version.** A new technology platform managed by a director of sales, outsourced to a firm in India. **6 years, >$6M, <$30K in revenue on release.**

### A disempowered, mercenary culture
Engineering outsourced. Top-down decisions. Teams handed roadmaps of features and projects. Physical environment reinforcing it — *"high cubicles and lack of sunlight… isolation and disconnect… hierarchical floor layout… lack of shared spaces… low morale, and even lower engagement."* Employees unwilling to take risks or suggest new ideas for fear of rejection.

### A lack of focus
Grown by acquisition into a portfolio *"profitable overall but with no unifying synergies or theme."* Sales couldn't sell a bigger picture. Merrill *"had too many balls in the air. Without a defined vision and strategy… it also was nearly impossible for Merrill to decide which products or services to invest in, which markets to target, and which initiatives to prioritize."*

**The wake-up call:** competitor **Workiva** offered a modern cloud-based solution and started winning quarterly-filing customers.

## What changed — three dimensions

### Changing how they build
- Legacy platform: *"fragile, obsolete, and simply unable to support the demands of the market."*
- Reinvested in a **microservices-based cloud architecture**.
- Waterfall → Agile; monolith → microservices; infrequent → CI/CD.
- **Reduced operational costs by 30%.**
- **The pivotal story:** a major client called irate about a security bug; Idiodi warned the CEO to expect the client CEO's call; engineering **located, implemented, tested, and deployed a fix in <1 hour**. At 10:00am the client called back — apologizing that they'd been mistaken; no CEO call needed. Rusty Wiley's response: *"the new muscles within the company to identify and diagnose a problem are impressive. Our ability to resolve that problem is also impressive. But what really stands out for me is the fact that our engineering team was able to respond to our customer's needs as fast as they did."* An inflection point for the transformation.

### Changing how they solve problems
- **Fund people/teams, not projects.** Problems to solve, not features to build.
- **Eliminated business analyst and product owner roles.**
- **Hired and coached professional product managers.**
- **Staffed real product design competency** — hired professional product designers.
- **Insourced engineering** (from outsourced).
- **"Troika" model** — PM + designer + Tech Lead work collaboratively with sales and marketing on a shared problem. (The book's language for [[the-product-team-trio|the trio]].)
- **Built product discovery competency** — a major new muscle.
- Enabled product + tech people to talk with customers directly.

### Changing how they decide
- Idiodi's frame: *"When companies say that everything is important, that they have too many priorities, the standard excuse for not getting anything done is to point the finger… when the truth is what they really have is a focus problem."*
- Compelling **product vision** — several years' horizon; strategy revisited quarterly.
- **Context + insights** as the decision inputs (not reactionary or opportunistic business requests). *"Insights tell you what's happening. Context tells you why it's happening."*
- **Divested and shut down businesses** to focus. This is the load-bearing decision behind the rebrand.

## Ch 35 — the follow-up innovation (patented redaction)
Post-transformation Datasite had the muscle to attempt a technically hard product no one else had solved.

**The problem:** existing document redaction tools removed or masked text/images *but not the metadata* — a determined attacker could forensically recreate the redacted data. In 2019 a highly-visible legal case had a poorly-redacted document filed publicly, catalyzing industry awareness.

**Discovery:** an engineer figured out a novel approach *"inspired by an approach used in Google Maps"*. Feasibility prototype → shown to product team and leaders. Then customer discovery — **12 customer discovery partners in Europe and North America** (London, Paris, New York) — early sales/marketing/service involvement.

**Outcome:** patented technology that automatically redacts (and can unredact) documents or entire virtual data rooms, removing both data and metadata with no possibility of identifying what was removed. **Significant market differentiator.**

## Outcome (transformation)
- **2020 rebrand: Merrill Corporation → Datasite.** Rebrand reflected divesting the legacy financial printing business to focus on the SaaS platform for the M&A community.
- Before: months-to-years to decide/build/deliver new capabilities. After: *"introducing new value to its customers virtually every day."*
- **2019 revenue +30%+**, facilitating >10,000 M&A deals annually.
- Datasite is *"not only the industry leader, but is highly admired for its culture of innovation."*

## Transferable lessons
1. **In sales-driven legacy companies, the sales power center is not just a problem — it's *the* root cause.** The 7th-version-of-the-platform story (6 years, $6M, $30K revenue) is the archetypal warning.
2. **Physical environment is a signal.** Idiodi named the cubicles and floor plan as evidence of disempowerment; culture change often has to be visible in the space to be believable.
3. **Focus is often *strategic divestment*.** Merrill's transformation was as much *"what we sold and shut down"* as *"what we built."* The rebrand recognized the strategic focus.
4. **The <1-hour bug fix as leadership evidence.** When the CEO can *feel* the difference between the old and new speed, transformation gains political air cover. Look for the story that will do this in your org.
5. **Coaching a head of sales as ally, not opposition.** Doug Cullen was Idiodi's earliest ally, not an obstacle. Naming the diagnosis truthfully required trust with the person whose organizational power was being repositioned.

## In your context
_If someone at the top of your sales organization sat down with an outside product leader and honestly answered "who decides what gets built here?" — what would they say? Doug Cullen's answer at Merrill was the entire diagnosis in a paragraph._

# Assess Product Risks

> Before building, deliberately consider the core product risks — **value, usability, feasibility, and (business) viability** — and address the biggest ones in discovery.

## The belief behind it
Product management is, at heart, about **managing risk**. For every product idea four risks can sink it: **Value** (will customers want it?), **Usability** (can they use it?), **Feasibility** (can we build it?), **Viability** (can our business support it — sales, marketing, finance, legal, ethics?). Identifying which risks are real lets the team test the right things cheaply (see [[the-four-big-risks]], [[2024-03-21-huryn-product-model-first-principles-part-2]]).

## What it looks like when followed
For each meaningful idea the trio asks all four risk questions, finds the *biggest* risks, and tests those first. The [[product-manager|PM]] is explicitly accountable for **value and viability** (*TRANSFORMED* Ch. 10); usability sits with the designer, feasibility with engineers — but **anyone** may raise an objection on any risk (Cagan even encourages engineers not to build something they're unconvinced is valuable or ethical).

## What violating it looks like
Jumping straight to build with no risk assessment; treating "can we build it?" as the only question; or turning risk into a bureaucratic checklist rather than a way of thinking.

## Tensions & trade-offs
- **⚠️ Four vs five risks (flag):** Canon = **four** risks, with **ethics part of business viability** ("ethical risk is part of business viability risk"). Huryn lists Ethics as a fifth for emphasis but quotes Cagan's reasoning against expanding the taxonomy: "once you get to more than 4... it just becomes a checklist and not a way of thinking." The wiki keeps the canonical four and treats ethics (and go-to-market) as in-scope lenses *within* viability — see [[the-four-big-risks]].
- Responsibility vs. participation: the PM *owns* value/viability, but ownership doesn't silence others — raising risk objections is everyone's job.

# Innovation over Predictability

> Optimize the culture for **innovation**, not for delivering a predictable feature schedule — because "100% predictability = 0% innovation."

## The belief behind it
Organizations fixated on shipping as many features as predictably as possible crowd out innovation. Cagan quotes Henrik Kniberg: "100% predictability = 0% innovation." And: "Companies that focus on predictability will tell you it's the stakeholder's job to worry about whether features are valuable... But strong product companies know... it goes to the nature of technology-powered products — stakeholders and customers don't know what's possible. It is the engineers who know what is just now possible" (*TRANSFORMED*). **High-integrity commitments** (real dates when truly needed) should be the **exception, not the rule** — see [[2024-03-21-huryn-product-model-first-principles-part-2]].

## What it looks like when followed
The org accepts uncertainty as the price of innovation; engineers' knowledge of "what's now possible" shapes what gets built; date commitments are rare, deliberate high-integrity commitments rather than the default operating mode.

## What violating it looks like
The feature factory dressed as discipline: every team committed to a packed quarterly roadmap with dates; innovation squeezed out; "predictability" treated as the highest virtue and stakeholders left to worry about value.

## Tensions & trade-offs
- Predictability isn't worthless — some commitments (partners, contracts, regulation) genuinely need dates; the principle is that these are **exceptions**, made as high-integrity commitments, not the norm.
- Built on [[placing-bets]] (you can't guarantee outcomes on a schedule) and protected by [[learning-over-failure]].

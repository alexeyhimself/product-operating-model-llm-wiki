# Empowered with Problems to Solve

> Give product teams **problems to solve** (with desired outcomes), not solutions to build — and hold them accountable for the results.

## The belief behind it
The people closest to the technology and the customer make better decisions about *how* to solve a problem than stakeholders handing down a feature spec. Empowerment only works when it is paired with accountability for an **outcome**, otherwise "autonomy" becomes unaccountable activity. *Empowered:* a team is "given a problem to solve, rather than solutions to build, and, most importantly, are held accountable for the results" (Field/canon — see [[2024-03-16-huryn-product-model-first-principles-part-1]]). *TRANSFORMED* (Ch. 8) names two kinds of problems — **customer problems** and **company problems** — each paired with **desired outcomes**.

## What it looks like when followed
The team's charter is a [[team-objectives|team objective]]: a significant customer or company problem plus clear measures of success (desired outcomes / key results). The team decides the solution through [[product-discovery|discovery]] and is judged on whether the outcome moved — not on whether a predetermined feature shipped.

## What violating it looks like
The feature factory: roadmaps are lists of features with dates, "requirements" arrive fully formed from stakeholders, and success = "we shipped it." Teams are measured on output and velocity, never on whether a customer or business problem actually improved.

## Tensions & trade-offs
- **Wiki synthesis:** "any metric you can influence counts" (Huryn's CES example) is pragmatic, but watch that proxy metrics don't quietly replace real customer-behavior change — see the outcomes nuance on [[outcomes-over-output]].
- Empowerment requires competent teams and leaders who lead with context; granting it before the team is ready (or without a real outcome) produces chaos, not ownership. Pairs with [[trust-over-control]] and [[transparency]].
- **"Opportunities" vs "problems to solve" ([[teresa-torres|Torres]] / [[continuous-discovery-habits|CDH]] Ch 2).** Torres deliberately widens Cagan's *problems-to-solve* framing to **opportunities** — customer needs, pain points, **and desires**. Her example: Disneyland, ice cream, mountain biking aren't fixing *problems*, they're addressing *desires*. Terminology-only difference; both frame the trio's charter as *understand the customer's situation, then decide how to intervene* — not *build the assigned feature*. When coaching, use whichever term the team already uses; press for the discipline underneath (frame from the *customer's* perspective, not the company's — CDH Ch 6 anti-pattern).

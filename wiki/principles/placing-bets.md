# Placing Bets

> Strategy is not a science: assigning a problem to a team doesn't guarantee it gets solved, so **play the odds** — sometimes assigning one critical problem to multiple teams.

## The belief behind it
There is no guarantee the objectives you set will be met. Cagan: "If you have a critically important problem to solve, you might assign that problem to multiple product teams in the hope at least one of them will make substantial progress in the quarter" (*TRANSFORMED* Ch. 16). In the Q&A he clarifies product strategy has **two facets**: (1) deciding the most important problems to solve (the core), and (2) **placing bets** — realizing solving isn't guaranteed, so you might assign one problem to several teams, or have one team chase quick wins while another takes a riskier, higher-potential path (see [[2024-03-16-huryn-product-model-first-principles-part-1]]).

## What it looks like when followed
Leaders talk in terms of odds and portfolios of bets, not certainties; the most important problems may get redundant or differentiated coverage (safe + risky); missing a bet triggers learning, not blame.

## What violating it looks like
Treating a roadmap as a delivery guarantee; single-threading every critical problem and acting shocked when it doesn't land; punishing teams for the inherent uncertainty of hard problems.

## Tensions & trade-offs
- Betting vs. [[focus]]: focus narrows *which* problems; betting may *duplicate* effort on the few that matter most — they are complementary, not contradictory.
- Multiple teams on one problem costs capacity; justified only for genuinely critical problems.
- Underlies [[innovation-over-predictability]]: bets imply you accept you can't promise outcomes on a schedule.

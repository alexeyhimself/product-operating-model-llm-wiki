# Learning over Failure

> In discovery there is no "success" or "failure" — only **"what did we learn?"** Reframe failed experiments as validated learning.

## The belief behind it
A culture that punishes failure kills the experimentation innovation depends on. Cagan: "When you run an experiment in product discovery, there is no such concept as success or failure; there is only the question 'What have we learned?'" (*TRANSFORMED*). Huryn adds Alberto Savoia (*The Right It*): "A good failure is when the value of the lesson is greater than the cost of the lesson. A bad failure is when the value of the lesson is much less than the cost." Discovery is **maximum validated learning with minimum effort** — most experiments are *expected* to "fail" — see [[2024-03-21-huryn-product-model-first-principles-part-2]].

## What it looks like when followed
Experiments are evaluated by what was learned, not by whether the idea "won"; cheap, fast "failures" are celebrated as good learning; the team optimizes lesson-value per unit cost (good failures over bad ones).

## What violating it looks like
Blame for ideas that didn't pan out; hiding negative results; "we can't run that experiment, what if it fails?"; expensive failures (building the whole thing) instead of cheap ones (a prototype) — bad failures by Savoia's definition.

## Tensions & trade-offs
- "No failure, only learning" is about *experiments*, not a blanket excuse for poor execution or ignored risks — make it a **good** failure (lesson > cost), per [[minimize-waste]].
- Requires the psychological safety of [[trust-over-control]] and the cheapness of [[embrace-rapid-experimentation]] to be real.

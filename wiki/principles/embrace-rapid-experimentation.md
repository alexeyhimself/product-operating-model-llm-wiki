# Embrace Rapid Experimentation

> Address product risks by running **small, rapid, inexpensive experiments** — the output of which is a **validated product backlog**.

## The belief behind it
Since most ideas won't work ([[minimize-waste]]), you test them cheaply rather than build them blindly. "The heart of product discovery is rapidly testing product ideas for what the solution could be... An experimentation culture not only helps you address risks, but it is absolutely central to innovation. Often the idea that succeeds started out like an idea that had little chance" (Cagan, *TRANSFORMED*). The result is concrete: "the output of product discovery is a **validated product backlog**" (Cagan, *Time-Boxing Product Discovery*) — see [[2024-03-21-huryn-product-model-first-principles-part-2]].

## What it looks like when followed
Risky assumptions become hypotheses tested with prototypes and lightweight experiments in days, not sprints; only validated ideas reach the delivery backlog; surprising long-shot ideas get a cheap shot because experimentation is cheap.

## What violating it looks like
Treating the sprint backlog as the experiment ("ship it and see"); confusing building with learning; an idea pipeline with no validation step, so engineering becomes the place where you discover ideas don't work.

## Tensions & trade-offs
- Speed vs. responsibility: rapid experimentation is bounded by [[test-ideas-responsibly]] in established companies.
- Experiment results must be read as learning, not verdicts of success/failure — see [[learning-over-failure]].

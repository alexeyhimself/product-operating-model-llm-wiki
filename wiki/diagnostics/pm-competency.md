# PM Competency — Gap Analysis

> [[marty-cagan|Cagan]]'s technique for assessing a [[product-manager|product manager]] ([[2019-04-08-cagan-coaching-tools-the-assessment|Coaching Tools – The Assessment]]): rate each skill twice — how **important** it is for this role/company, and the PM's **current capability** — and coach the biggest *weighted gaps* first. The foundation that feeds the [[coaching-the-pm|coaching plan]]. The book-length primary is [[empowered|EMPOWERED]] Ch 8, which frames the same technique inside the coaching engine.

## The Ch 8 framing (EMPOWERED)
Ch 8 grounds the assessment inside two premises the article restates but doesn't spell out:

- **Urgency of the need.** *"Empowered product teams depend on competent product people, and if you don't develop your people and provide growth opportunities, there are usually other companies that will. I have always been a big believer in the old adage that 'people join a company but leave their manager.'"*
- **Why gap-analysis format.** *"This format acknowledges that not all skills are equally important, not all gaps are equally significant, and expectations change with the level of responsibility. This tool is intended to help focus the attention where it is most needed."* This is why the wiki's rubric uses *importance* × *capability*, not a single capability score — the gap-weighted-by-importance is the whole point.
- **Why product knowledge first.** *"I like to cover product first because product knowledge is the foundation for everything else. Without competence in product knowledge, the rest doesn't really matter."*
- **How process skills evolve.** *"New product managers are expected to know the basic techniques, but strong product managers are always developing their skills and learning new and more-advanced techniques. Much like a good surgeon is constantly following the latest learnings on surgical skills and techniques, a strong product manager always has more to learn."*
- **Why people skills separate competent from truly successful.** Ch 8 explicitly labels the People/Process/Product pillar it comes last as *"the difference between competent and truly successful."* The wiki uses this ordering in the table below.
- **The Chapters ingested pair with [[coaching-mindset]].** Ch 8 (the assessment technique) presumes Ch 7 (the mindset). Without the mindset the assessment becomes a scorecard-for-management-purposes, not coaching material.

## Why it matters
Empowered teams depend on competent PMs; weak ones default the team back to a feature factory. "If you don't develop your people … there are usually other companies that will." This diagnostic protects [[empowered-with-problems-to-solve]], [[outcomes-over-output]], and [[collaboration]] by making PM development deliberate rather than left to annual reviews (which Cagan calls inadequate for development).

## The skill taxonomy (people / process / product)
Assess in this order — **product knowledge first**, because it's the foundation ("without competence in product knowledge, the rest doesn't really matter").

**Product knowledge** — table stakes (~2–3 months to ramp a new PM):
1. **User & customer** — a company-acknowledged expert on target users/customers?
2. **Data** — skilled with the data tools; an acknowledged expert in how the product is actually used?
3. **Industry & domain** — knows the landscape, competitors, relevant trends?
4. **Business & company** — understands marketing, sales, finance (revenue + cost), services, legal/privacy; do stakeholders feel understood?
5. **Product operational** — an acknowledged expert on how the product works (could demo, onboard, handle support)?

**Process skills & techniques:**
6. **Product discovery** — strong grasp of the [[the-four-big-risks|risks]]; tackles them *before* build; collaborative; outcome-focused; qual + quant?
7. **Product optimization** — for live products, drives rapid improvement (A/B, funnel)?
8. **Product delivery** — understands the supporting role to engineers and product marketing?
9. **Product development process** — understands discovery + delivery end-to-end and the product-owner responsibilities?

**People skills & responsibilities** — the difference between competent and truly successful:
10. **Team collaboration** — genuine, trust-based work with designer and engineers; involves them early?
11. **Stakeholder management** — mutual trust/respect with stakeholders incl. senior leadership?
12. **Evangelism** — shares vision/strategy; motivates team and stakeholders?
13. **Leadership** — influences and inspires people who don't report to them, especially under stress?

## Rubric — the gap analysis
For **each** of the 13 skills, assign two ratings on a **1–10** scale:

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Importance** | Where the PM *needs to be* for this role/company. Set by the manager/org. Rises with seniority — e.g. stakeholder management ≈ 7 for a PM, ≈ 9 for a senior PM. |
| **Capability** | Where the PM *currently performs*. Assessed by the manager; the PM should *also* self-assess. |

The **gap = importance − capability**, weighted by importance. A skill rated 8 importance / 4 capability is a large gap on a critical skill — coach it now. A large gap on a low-importance skill can wait.

> **Coach the top 3 gaps first**, then move to the next set. Re-rate as gaps close; when the PM is ready to grow, raise the importance bar to the next level's expectations (PM → senior → GPM → principal). GPMs get an extra category: developing a few PMs.

**Notes & cautions (from canon):**
- The taxonomy is a **customizable default** — adjust for your culture/industry (e.g. media: break out product↔editorial explicitly).
- **Don't rely on the self-assessment alone** — gaps between self- and manager-assessment are common and exactly what to discuss; a manager who avoids that "is abdicating their responsibilities."
- Cagan dislikes the "associate PM" level: competent on these dimensions → they're a PM; not yet → get them to competence fast (2–3 months) or help them find a better-suited role.

## Socratic questions
The coach asks to place the PM — one at a time, without leading:
1. Which of the 13 skills matter *most* for this specific team and company right now — and why those?
2. Pick your strongest and weakest pillar (product / process / people). What's your evidence for each?
3. On `<skill with suspected gap>`, where would you rate your capability 1–10 — and where would your designer, an engineer, and a key stakeholder rate you?
4. The last time you `<met a customer / read the data / previewed a concern with a stakeholder>` — what happened, and what did it change?
5. If we could only close *one* gap this quarter, which would unlock the most — and what would "closed" look like?

## What "good" looks like
A PM who is the team's acknowledged expert on customer, data, domain, business, and product; tackles the four risks before engineers build; collaborates as a trusted peer with the trio; has earned mutual trust with stakeholders; and leads a team that doesn't report to them. Importance and capability are aligned on the skills that matter for the role.

## Interventions & experiments
- **Large gaps in product knowledge:** run the [[coaching-the-pm|coaching plan]]'s onboarding moves — customer visits (≥15), business-model canvas, friend in finance, dogfooding.
- **Process gaps:** read *[[inspired|INSPIRED]]*, then scenario drills on the [[the-four-big-risks|four risks]]; CSPO only for product-owner mechanics.
- **People gaps:** observe real trio + stakeholder interactions and debrief privately; the narrative tool and a presentation-skills class for evangelism.
- **In all cases:** weekly one-on-ones to review progress; top-3 focus; re-rate and advance. Full per-skill detail on [[coaching-the-pm]].

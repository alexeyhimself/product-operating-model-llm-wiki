# Model Maturity — Diagnostic

> Place a *whole organization* against the Product Operating Model. Unlike the team-scoped [[feature-team-vs-empowered-team]], this diagnostic rolls up across teams, leadership, and culture: the **three transformation dimensions** ([[2022-09-17-moore-changing-how-you-build|build]] · [[2022-09-20-moore-changing-how-you-solve-problems|solve]] · [[2022-09-23-moore-changing-how-you-decide-which-problems-to-solve|decide]]), the **five concepts**, the **four competencies**, and the **four cultural principles**. Use to set baselines, sequence transformations, and resist the most common transformation failure — adopting titles without competencies ([[2023-03-15-cagan-product-model-competencies|canon]]).

## Why it matters
Most companies are in the **project model**, the documented source of most product failures ([[2015-06-05-cagan-product-fail|canon: 10 root causes]]; [[2023-08-17-cagan-from-projects-to-products|canon]]). The two inconvenient truths — half of ideas don't work, even the good ones take many iterations — are unavoidable; weak orgs pay the full project-model cost on every miss ([[2013-03-17-cagan-the-inconvenient-truth-about-product|canon]]). Transformation is the long-term path that converts the four big risks from "discovered after launch" into "addressed before build." This diagnostic exists to (a) reveal the *actual* maturity (vs. self-image), (b) sequence change without overreach, and (c) avoid the "checkbox transformation" — superficial relabels masking unchanged behavior.

## What "maturity" measures
Three dimensions × four lenses each. A mature org scores **3 or 4** across virtually all twelve cells; a project-model org scores 1–2 across most.

## Rubric (org-level levels)
| Level | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Project model** | Stakeholder-driven roadmaps; sales-driven specials; PMs are project managers; designers do "lipstick on the pig"; engineers are mercenaries; risk lives at the end ([[2015-06-05-cagan-product-fail|canon]]). The 10 reasons for weak product ([[2010-08-16-cagan-top-10-reasons-for-weak-product|canon]]) hit hard — usually 7+ of them. |
| **2** | **Checkbox transformation** | Adopted titles and ceremonies (squads, OKRs, "discovery") without competencies, strategy, or empowerment ([[2023-03-15-cagan-product-model-competencies|canon: "many people have adopted the new titles… without learning the new competencies"]]). 4–6 of the 10 weak-product reasons still present. |
| **3** | **Operating in the model** | Most teams are durable, empowered, trio-led, with continuous discovery and delivery. Strategy is real (focus + insights + actions + management). Leaders coach. 1–2 of the 10 weak-product reasons remain. |
| **4** | **Exemplary** | Sustained innovation engine — discovery and delivery hum; engineers are the first source of just-now-possible ideas; strategy survives leadership transitions; the org is a place strong product people compete to join. Like Amazon, Google, Apple, Netflix — different cultures, same product fundamentals ([[2019-04-24-cagan-the-greatest-coach|canon]]). |

## Cross-cutting symptoms
**Top 10 reasons for weak product** ([[2010-08-16-cagan-top-10-reasons-for-weak-product|canon]]) — count how many are present:
1. Lack of product vision. 2. Stakeholder-driven roadmap. 3. Missing rapid test-and-learn culture. 4. Weak UX design competency. 5. Little actual customer expertise. 6. No product discovery process. 7. Missing true collaboration. 8. Focus on dates/features over business results. 9. Lack of corporate courage. 10. Weak product leaders. *5+ present → Level 1. 2–4 → Level 2. 0–1 → Level 3+.*

**Ten keys to success** ([[2016-05-03-cagan-product-success|canon]]) — count present:
1. Empowered product teams. 2. Product vision and strategy. 3. Focus on business outcomes. 4. Competent product manager. 5. Collaboration-driven solutions. 6. Discovery: learn fast. 7. Focus on key risks. 8. Right role for an MVP (test, not product). 9. Delivery: release with confidence. 10. Obsess over customers. *9–10 → Level 4. 7–8 → Level 3. 4–6 → Level 2. <4 → Level 1.*

## Socratic questions
1. Sketch the journey of a customer-impacting idea from origin to live, in your org. Where does each of the [[the-four-big-risks|four risks]] get addressed? *Where they get addressed is where your model maturity lives.*
2. Pick three teams. Are they durable? Are they given problems or features? Do they meet customers? *If "yes/yes/yes" is < 50% of teams, you're not in the product model.*
3. Read the [[2010-08-16-cagan-top-10-reasons-for-weak-product|10 reasons for weak product]] aloud. How many apply? Which is the worst?
4. Read the [[2016-05-03-cagan-product-success|10 keys to success]] aloud. How many of these does your org *actually* live?
5. Where is the **risk** in your process — at the end (waterfall/big-bang) or distributed early via discovery?
6. If you removed all the ceremony tomorrow — sprints, OKR docs, "discoveries," squads — what would the work *actually* look like? That's your real model.
7. What's the failure mode? Output without outcomes (Level 2 — checkbox), no output (Level 1 — project model bureaucracy), or genuinely working but slow to compound (Level 3 — still maturing)?
8. Which dimension is weakest — **build**, **solve**, or **decide**? Strengthen that one first ([[2022-09-23-moore-changing-how-you-decide-which-problems-to-solve|Moore]]: "decide" is the largest gap in most transformations).

## What "good" looks like
A whole-organization Level-3+ posture: durable trio-led teams across the org, given small numbers of problems with measurable outcomes; running continuous discovery and continuous delivery; product leadership delivering strategic context and active coaching; a focused, insight-driven product strategy quarterly-managed; a healthy missionary culture; instrumented, reversible, customer-impact-aware releases. The two inconvenient truths ([[2013-03-17-cagan-the-inconvenient-truth-about-product|canon]]) are *budgeted for* — half the bets don't work, the good ones need iteration, and the org is set up to absorb both.

## Interventions & experiments
- **Level 1 → 2 is dangerous** because the org can checkbox itself into looking like Level 2 without actually improving. The standard failure: ceremony adoption without competency development. Resist.
- **Level 1 → 3 (real change):** sequence per the three dimensions. (a) Start with **decide** — strategy + leadership readiness ([[product-strategy-quality]], [[leadership-readiness]]); without these the rest can't land. (b) Build the **solve** dimension — install real trio competencies on a pilot team; measure on real outcomes; expand. (c) Refit **build** — pay down CI/CD debt; instrument; institute Gentle Deployment and customer-impact assessment. ([[2022-09-23-moore-changing-how-you-decide-which-problems-to-solve|Moore]]: address the leadership/decide gap explicitly — it's the largest gap in most transformations.)
- **Level 2 → 3:** the transformation here is *de-theaterizing*. Audit each ceremony against canon: does it represent the competency it claims? OKRs without empowerment is theater. "Squads" without durable cross-functional ownership is theater. "Discovery" that's PM + designer with no engineer and no customers is theater. Replace theater with competency development ([[2023-03-15-cagan-product-model-competencies|canon]]).
- **Level 3 → 4:** sustaining work. Invest in leadership development pipeline ([[2019-04-24-cagan-the-greatest-coach|canon]]); strengthen insight infrastructure ([[2020-02-19-cagan-product-strategy-insights|canon four sources]]); celebrate teams that *killed* low-ROI initiatives (corporate courage — [[2010-08-16-cagan-top-10-reasons-for-weak-product|canon #9]]).
- **Common traps:** (1) "We can transform one team in the bubble" — you can pilot, but real transformation needs leadership change; (2) "Process is the lever" — process amplifies what's already there; weak orgs that buy SAFe get faster failure ([[2015-11-25-cagan-missionaries-vs-mercenaries|Cagan]]); (3) confusing **agile** (an engineering practice) with **the product model** (an organization-wide operating model) — [[2015-06-05-cagan-product-fail|canon: "Agile finally enters too late"]]; (4) "We are special; this doesn't apply" — every successful product company is using this model under different cultural wrappers ([[2019-04-24-cagan-the-greatest-coach|Bill Campbell's quote]]).

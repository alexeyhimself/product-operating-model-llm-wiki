# Engineering Empowerment — Diagnostic

> Cagan calls empowered engineers **"the most important thing"** — if he could change one thing about a feature-team org, it would be this ([[2020-04-20-cagan-the-most-important-thing|canon]]). This diagnostic isolates engineer-empowerment specifically, distinct from [[feature-team-vs-empowered-team|whether the team overall is empowered]] and from [[team-collaboration-health|whether the trio collaborates well]] — a team can clear both of those and still treat engineers as the last stop before shipping rather than a source of ideas.

## Why it matters
Cagan's claim is strong and specific: engineers, because they work with the enabling technology daily, are **the single most important source of innovation** in a strong product company — more than PMs, more than designers, more than leadership. [[amazon-prime|Amazon Prime]], [[spotify-discover-weekly|Spotify's Discover Weekly]], and much of what [[google-product-model|Google]] ships trace back to an engineer (or a small group of engineers) proposing something that wasn't on any roadmap. An org that keeps engineers out of framing isn't just under-collaborating — per Cagan, it's cutting itself off from its best source of new ideas.

## Signals & symptoms
**Healthy:**
- Engineers routinely propose solutions, not just feasibility opinions on someone else's solution.
- Engineers are in the room for customer conversations and discovery work, not just delivery.
- At least one shipped win in recent memory originated as an engineer's idea, not a roadmap item.
- Engineers can articulate the current [[product-vision|vision]] and [[product-strategy|strategy]] — the *why*, not just the *what*.
- A dual-track career ladder exists, so strong engineers don't have to become managers to grow.

**Warning:**
- Engineers see new ideas for the first time at sprint planning, framed as "can we build this" rather than "here's a problem."
- Engineers are consulted on feasibility but rarely asked what's *possible*.
- "Engineering-driven" is used inside the org as faint criticism rather than a compliment.
- Engineering headcount is partially or fully outsourced under fixed-scope contracts.

**Red flag:**
- Engineers never meet customers or see raw data; all input is pre-filtered through the PM.
- The org treats engineering as a cost center to be minimized, not a source of value.
- Engineers describe their job as "building what we're told," with no expectation otherwise.
- The only way to grow as an engineer is to stop being one and become a manager.

## Rubric
Cagan's own single-question test anchors Level 1 vs. everything else: *"If the first time your engineers see a product idea is at sprint planning, you are clearly a feature team, and your engineers are not empowered in any meaningful sense."*

| Level | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Coders** | Engineers receive finished specs or designs; first exposure to an idea is sprint planning, framed as an estimation exercise. No customer or data access. Outsourcing, if present, makes this the default. |
| **2** | **Consulted** | Engineers are asked for feasibility input before a design is finalized, but the *problem framing and solution direction* are already set without them. "Can we build this?" is common; "what's possible here?" is rare. |
| **3** | **Empowered** | Engineers are in the room from the first framing of a problem, alongside the [[the-product-team-trio|trio]]. They have direct customer and data access, propose solution ideas, and can articulate the vision and strategy behind their work. |
| **4** | **Source of innovation** | Above, plus engineers are a *recognized, credited* source of the org's best ideas — leadership can point to specific product wins that originated with an engineer, and a real dual-track career ladder keeps the strongest engineers technical. |

## Socratic questions
Ask one at a time. Listen — don't lead.

1. Think of the current quarter's biggest initiative. When did engineers first see it — as a fully-specified ticket, a rough problem statement, or something in between? Who decided what to build?
2. Tell me about a product idea that originated from an engineer in the last year. If you can't think of one, why not — is it that it doesn't happen, or that it wouldn't be recognized as such?
3. When did an engineer last talk directly to a customer, or look at raw usage data themselves rather than a summarized report?
4. Can your engineers state the product vision and current strategy in their own words?
5. If a strong engineer wanted to keep growing without becoming a manager, what's the path?

## What "good" looks like
Engineers who see problems (not finished specs), sit in on customer and discovery work as a matter of course, are recognized for proposing solutions the org later ships, and have a real career path that doesn't force a move into management to keep growing. The org can point to at least one significant win that traces back to an engineer's initiative.

## Interventions & experiments
- **If at Level 1 (coders):** the single highest-leverage change is moving engineers' first exposure to a new problem earlier — literally invite the engineering lead(s) to the very first framing conversation, before any spec exists. If engineering is outsourced, this diagnostic is close to moot until that structural decision changes; flag it as the root cause, not a coaching gap.
- **If at Level 2 (consulted):** change the question asked of engineers from "can we build this" to "what's possible here" — a small wording shift that signals a real change in what's being asked of them. Give engineers direct (not PM-filtered) access to at least one customer conversation or data source per discovery cycle.
- **If at Level 3:** build the habit of crediting engineer-originated ideas explicitly when they ship — visibility reinforces the behavior. Audit whether the dual-track career ladder is real or exists only on paper.
- **If at Level 4:** the risk is complacency at the org level — as the company scales, protect the practices (customer access, early framing involvement) that got here; they erode quietly under delivery pressure.
- **Common pitfall:** treating "engineering-driven" as something to correct. Cagan's explicit counter: engineering-driven orgs are often the *easiest* to transform, because they already have the hardest part — genuine engineering respect and access — right; the gap is usually product/design maturity, not too much engineering influence.

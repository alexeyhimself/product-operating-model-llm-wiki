# Vision Quality — Diagnostic

> Place an organization's (or product line's) **vision** on a rubric. Canon: [[2020-08-10-cagan-product-vision-faq|Cagan's Product Vision FAQ]] gives unusually concrete, checkable criteria — timeframe, scope, ownership, refresh cadence — which this diagnostic formalizes into levels and Socratic questions.

## Why it matters
Vision is what makes [[missionaries-vs-mercenaries|missionaries]] instead of mercenaries — it's "the single most powerful recruiting tool for strong product people" ([[empowered|EMPOWERED]] Ch 3) and the thing that keeps [[empowered-engineers|empowered engineers]] and the [[the-product-team-trio|trio]] oriented toward the same destination without being told what to build. A weak or absent vision is one of the quieter reasons a team that's technically "empowered" (given problems, not features) still drifts, because problems without a shared destination don't add up to anything.

## Signals & symptoms
**Healthy:**
- Anyone on a product team can describe the vision in their own words, including *how* it improves customers' lives — not just what the product does.
- The vision has held steady for 2+ years without material change.
- New hires cite the vision as part of why they joined.
- Leadership (CEO included) can articulate it, not just the head of product.

**Warning:**
- The vision reads like a mission slogan ("organize the world's information," "make customers' lives better through automation") — inspiring-sounding but not concrete about *how*.
- The vision has quietly become "this quarter's priorities" or a disguised roadmap.
- Different teams under the same product line describe genuinely different visions.
- The vision was written once, communicated once, and never mentioned again.

**Red flag:**
- No one below the head of product could state the vision if asked cold.
- The "vision" is framed around revenue or company priorities, not customers.
- The vision changed twice in the last year without a genuine discovery-driven pivot behind either change.
- There is no vision — teams orient entirely off the current quarter's roadmap.

## Rubric
| Level | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Absent or roadmap-as-vision** | No stated multi-year vision, or "the vision" is really just the current roadmap restated. Teams have no shared destination beyond this quarter. |
| **2** | **Slogan vision** | A vision exists and sounds inspiring, but doesn't say *how* customers' lives improve — no one can act differently because of it. Often unowned below the CEO. |
| **3** | **Working vision** | Concrete, customer-improving, 2–5 year horizon (5–10 for hardware), owned by the head of product with real CEO buy-in. Most teams can restate it accurately. Hasn't changed materially in over a year absent a genuine pivot. |
| **4** | **Missionary-generating vision** | Above, plus it's visibly a recruiting and retention force — people cite it as why they joined and stayed — and it survives leadership turnover because it's genuinely shared, not one person's pet phrase. |

## Socratic questions
Ask one at a time. Listen — don't lead.

1. State the product vision in your own words — not the official wording, your understanding of it. What's the timeframe, and how does it describe customers' lives improving?
2. Ask two more people on different teams the same question. Do the three answers agree?
3. When did the vision last change? Was that because of a genuine discovery insight (a "vision pivot"), or did it just quietly drift with the roadmap?
4. Who owns this vision — who wrote it, who evangelizes it, and when was it last mentioned in an all-hands or onboarding session?
5. If a new hire asked "why does this product exist, and where is it going in five years," could you answer without checking a slide?

## What "good" looks like
A vision 2–5 years out (5–10 for device companies), stated in terms of how customers' lives improve, owned by the head of product with genuine CEO buy-in, unchanged for a year or more absent a real pivot, and restated consistently — with the *how*, not just the *what* — by people well below the leadership team who wrote it.

## Interventions & experiments
- **If at Level 1 (absent/roadmap-as-vision):** this is a leadership gap, not a team gap — see [[leadership-readiness]]. A [[product-coach|product leadership coach]] or a facilitated 1–2 day offsite with the right 5–15 people (CEO, heads of product/tech/design/marketing/sales, strong ICs), informed by written narratives on the nine crafting factors, is Cagan's own prescription. Don't let a single person draft it alone and announce it.
- **If at Level 2 (slogan vision):** rewrite for concreteness, not inspiration — force the *how*. If the current vision can't answer "how does this improve a customer's life, specifically," it needs another pass before it's worth evangelizing further.
- **If at Level 3:** the risk is under-evangelizing — vision that's right but rarely repeated fades from teams' daily framing. Build it into onboarding, all-hands, and 1:1s as a matter of habit ([[product-leadership|evangelism is never "done"]]).
- **If at Level 4:** the risk is leadership succession — a vision this good is often tied to the person who articulated it. Deliberately test whether other leaders can carry and evangelize it, not just the originator.
- **Common pitfall:** treating vision as a fill-in-the-blanks canvas exercise. Cagan is explicit that there's no paint-by-numbers template — crafting a real vision is a creative act, not a form.

# Model-as-Coach Readiness

> Assesses whether an individual's (or org's) use of a foundation model as a product coach matches Cagan's prescription — the right *framing*, the right *context*, and the right *stance* — so the coaching is relevant and actually builds competence, rather than accelerating [[product-management-theater|theater]]. For a PM, leader, or founder configuring AI as a coach.

## Why it matters
[[model-as-product-coach|Model-as-coach]] is only "as good as most managers" *when configured well*. Out of the box a model averages across the majority of online content — which is about the **project** model — and gives self-contradictory advice. The difference between a career game-changer and confident nonsense is entirely in the setup. This diagnostic protects [[coaching-vs-contracting|coaching (not contracting)]] and [[product-sense]].

## Signals & symptoms
- **Healthy:** the model is told which operating model to teach (product, not project), told to act as a *coach* (critique over affirmation), pointed at trusted voices, *and* loaded with your strategic context; the user seeks critique and questions outputs.
- **Warning:** product-model framing but no strategic context (generic advice); or the user accepts outputs without challenge.
- **Red flag:** using AI to generate PRDs/roadmaps and speed the project model; treating output as canonical; no coach framing at all.

## Rubric
| Level | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Project-model accelerant | Using AI to write PRDs, aggregate feedback into a roadmap, herd the backlog — speeding the project model. Theater, faster. |
| 2 | Generic product prompting | Asks product questions but hasn't told the model *product not project*, hasn't set a coach stance, and provides no strategic context. Answers are fuzzy and inconsistent. |
| 3 | Configured coach | Model instructed: (a) teach the **product** operating model, (b) act as a **coach** (challenge, don't affirm), (c) prioritize trusted voices (Torres, Doshi, SVPG); **strategic context loaded** (vision, strategy, team topology, role/domain). Used to build [[product-sense]] and seek critique. |
| 4 | Exemplary | Level 3, plus: disciplined critique-seeking (not affirmation); awareness of non-determinism (cross-checks, re-asks); and — for **leaders** — pairs the AI coach with a **human** leadership coach for politics/relationships. |

## Socratic questions
1. When you open the model to coach you, what have you told it about *which* way of working you're trying to learn?
2. Have you asked it to challenge you and point out flaws — or does it mostly agree with you?
3. What does it know about your product vision, strategy, team topology, and your specific role and domain?
4. The last time it gave you an answer, did you act on it — or interrogate it for what it might be getting wrong?
5. If you're a leader: who helps you with the people/politics an AI "doesn't even know it doesn't know"?

## What "good" looks like
Cagan's full prescription ([[model-as-product-coach]]): **project files** (this wiki) + **project instructions** (tell it to prioritize the product operating model; act as a coach) + **your strategic context** (vision, strategy, team topology, role/industry). Used to build product sense fast, prepare for decisions, and seek critique — 7×24. For leaders, plus a human leadership coach.

## Interventions & experiments
- **If at level 1–2:** add the three prompt keys (product-not-project; coach-not-cheerleader; prioritize trusted voices), then load a one-page strategic-context doc. Cagan: it only takes a few minutes and "then it's amazing."
- **If at level 3:** practice critique-seeking; re-ask important questions to expose non-determinism; capture where the model is weakest for your domain.
- **If a leader:** arrange a human product-leadership coach for the political/relational work; keep the AI coach for everything else.

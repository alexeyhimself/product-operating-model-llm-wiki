# Product Strategy Quality — Diagnostic

> Assess an organization (not a single team) against the four pillars of strategy canon: **focus → insights → actions → management**, all in service of the [[product-vision]]. Most orgs have a *goal* (e.g. "double revenue") and a *roadmap* (the tactics) but no product strategy at all ([[2016-07-16-cagan-vision-vs-strategy|Cagan]]). Pair with [[leadership-readiness]] — strategy is a leadership artifact, and weak strategy is usually a leadership symptom ([[2023-12-05-cagan-alternatives-to-product-leaders|canon]]).

## Why it matters
Empowered teams need [[2020-01-09-cagan-coaching-strategic-context|strategic context]] (mission, scorecard, objectives, vision, strategy, principles) to make good decisions. Without strategy, the org defaults to feature teams serving stakeholders, OKRs collapse into "ship these features by Friday," and product leadership is reduced to roadmap negotiation ([[2010-08-16-cagan-top-10-reasons-for-weak-product|canon: #1 source of weak product is lack of vision/strategy]]). This diagnostic protects [[focus]], [[powered-by-insights]], [[transparency]], [[placing-bets]], and [[empowered-with-problems-to-solve]].

## Signals & symptoms
**Healthy:**
- A small number — 2–3 — of truly important problems the org is solving this year ([[2020-02-18-cagan-product-strategy-focus|canon]]; Rumelt).
- Quantitative, qualitative, technology, and industry insights are actively cultivated and shared ([[2020-02-19-cagan-product-strategy-insights|canon four sources]]).
- Insights convert to **team objectives** (problems with measurable outcomes) — not feature lists ([[2020-02-20-cagan-product-strategy-actions|canon]]).
- Leaders actively manage strategy: weekly 1:1s with PMs to remove obstacles, escalate decisions, share learnings ([[2020-02-21-cagan-product-strategy-management|canon]]).
- A [[product-vision]] exists, is inspiring, and is 2–5 years out (or 5–10 for hardware) ([[2016-07-16-cagan-vision-vs-strategy|canon]]).
- [[product-principles|Product principles]] / manifesto in place to inform trade-offs ([[2006-01-02-cagan-the-product-manifesto|canon]]).

**Warning:**
- "Strategy" = a prioritized roadmap negotiated with stakeholders.
- The org has 10–30 simultaneous "high-priority objectives" — a signal of *prioritizing, not focusing* ([[2020-02-18-cagan-product-strategy-focus|canon]]).
- Insights are confused with reports — data is collected but rarely *connected* to action.
- OKRs exist but the key results are "ship X, launch Y."
- Vision and strategy are described interchangeably; or there's a vision but no strategy bridging it to the work.

**Red flag:**
- The Pandora prioritization pattern — splitting engineering capacity "fairly" across stakeholders ([[2020-02-18-cagan-product-strategy-focus|canon: cited as the classic case of *no* strategy]]).
- "We've already decided everything; just give us a roadmap."
- Strategy outsourced to a management consultancy with no follow-through ([[2020-02-19-cagan-product-strategy-insights|canon: rarely relevant; rarely owned]]).
- Founder/CEO is the only strategist; no leadership team can articulate strategy independently.
- No vision at all, or a vision that hasn't been updated in years and feels stale.
- "[Peanut-butter strategy"](https://www.svpg.com/transformation-fail/) — capacity smeared evenly across stakeholders rather than concentrated on a few bets.

## Rubric
| Level | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | **No strategy** | Stakeholder-driven roadmap as the planning artifact. No real focus, no insights process, no team objectives in OKR form, no vision worth quoting. Leadership "manages" by triaging requests. |
| **2** | **Strategy in name** | A vision exists; "strategy" is a slide deck of themes. Many simultaneous priorities. Insights collected, not leveraged. Teams given features dressed as OKRs. Leaders manage timelines, not strategy. |
| **3** | **Sound strategy** | Focused on a few critical problems; four sources of insights actively mined ([[2020-02-19-cagan-product-strategy-insights|canon]]); insights converted into team objectives; weekly leadership engagement removing obstacles. Vision is alive — used in recruiting, all-hands, and decisions. Principles in place. |
| **4** | **Exemplary** | Above + the org regularly produces *insights worth marketing on* — the strategy is a competitive weapon. Insights are circulated across teams; leadership all-hands routinely shares the top learning of the week. Strategy survives leadership transitions. |

## Socratic questions
1. Name the 2–3 most important problems this organization is solving *this year*. If you can name more than 3, which would you cut — and which one would the CEO refuse to cut? ([[2020-02-18-cagan-product-strategy-focus|canon: pick your battles]].)
2. What insight is your current strategy *built on* — quantitative, qualitative, technology, or industry? Where did it come from? Who else in the company knows it? ([[2020-02-19-cagan-product-strategy-insights|canon four sources]].)
3. Show me the path from vision → strategy → team objectives → today's work. Where does the chain break?
4. If a team's OKRs got fully delivered, would the business outcome necessarily move? Or just the features?
5. When did you last *kill* an in-flight initiative because the insight changed? When did the org last reallocate capacity from "consensus priority" to "highest-impact insight"?
6. Walk me through your last weekly 1:1 between the head of product and a PM — was it strategy-active management ([[2020-02-21-cagan-product-strategy-management|canon]]) or status-reporting?
7. Read your product vision out loud. Does it inspire? Could it recruit a strong engineer next month? ([[2016-07-16-cagan-vision-vs-strategy|canon: vision is one of our most effective recruiting tools]].)
8. Does the org have a written [[product-principles|product manifesto / principles]]? When was it last referenced in a trade-off decision? ([[2006-01-02-cagan-the-product-manifesto|canon]].)

## What "good" looks like
A small number (2–3) of *focused* problems the whole org can name. Behind each, an *insight* identified by the leadership team — pulled from data, customers, enabling technology, or industry — that explains *why* this is the move. The insight becomes **team objectives** (OKRs) the relevant trio owns end-to-end. The head of product runs weekly 1:1s with PMs that actively manage progress, remove obstacles, and circulate learnings across teams. The product vision is a 2–5-year aspiration ("stubborn on vision, flexible on details" — Bezos, via [[2016-07-16-cagan-vision-vs-strategy|Cagan]]) that's quoted in recruiting and decisions. A short product manifesto guides trade-offs ([[2006-01-02-cagan-the-product-manifesto|canon]]). Strategy is reviewed quarterly and survives leadership transitions.

## Interventions & experiments
- **If at Level 1 (no strategy):** the org needs to first *understand it has no strategy*. Use Rumelt's bar and Cagan's four-pillar frame ([[2020-02-17-cagan-product-strategy-overview|canon]]) as the diagnostic mirror. The first intervention is almost always to **slash the priority list** from 20–30 to 2–3 — this is the Focus pillar. If leadership won't make the choice, no other intervention will stick.
- **If at Level 2 (strategy in name):** (a) Run a focus workshop — list every "priority" the org is funding; force a vote-down to 2–3 ([[2020-02-18-cagan-product-strategy-focus|canon]]); (b) For each, name the *insight* and its source — if there isn't one, the priority is a stakeholder ask, not strategy ([[2020-02-19-cagan-product-strategy-insights|canon]]); (c) Convert each priority into a **team objective** (problem + measurable outcome) — give the team the problem, not the solution ([[2020-02-20-cagan-product-strategy-actions|canon]]); (d) Stand up the head-of-product weekly 1:1 cadence to *manage* the strategy ([[2020-02-21-cagan-product-strategy-management|canon]]).
- **If at Level 3 (sound):** invest in **insight infrastructure** — instrumentation, research, technology scouting, and an industry-read habit ([[2017-07-14-cagan-analyzing-product-strategy|canon: Stratechery as one source]]). Adopt the [[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|written-narrative]] discipline for strategy decisions so reasoning is testable.
- **Common traps:** (1) buying a strategy from a consultancy — the people are smart, but rarely deep enough on your business to find real product insights ([[2020-02-19-cagan-product-strategy-insights|canon]]); (2) confusing **prioritizing** (rank-ordering a long list) with **focusing** (cutting to a short list); (3) leaving strategy to the CEO alone — leadership team must own and articulate it; (4) ten "north stars" — by definition there can be only one.

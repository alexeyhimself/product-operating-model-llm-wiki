# Leadership Readiness — Diagnostic

> Assess product leadership (managers of PMs/designers/eng; head of product; founders acting as head of product) against the canon's bar: **leadership** (the five elements of strategic context) + **management** (staffing, coaching, objectives) + **active strategy management**. Canon framing: [[2015-06-27-cagan-leadership-vs-management|*Leadership vs. Management*]] (2015); [[2020-11-19-cagan-product-leadership-is-hard|*Product Leadership Is Hard*]] (2020); [[2023-12-05-cagan-alternatives-to-product-leaders|*Alternatives to Product Leaders*]] (2023). The whole model rests on this layer — Cagan: "empowered product teams don't require *less* management, they require *better* management" ([[2018-10-31-cagan-empowered-product-teams|canon]]).

## Why it matters
Strong PMs are almost always found in companies with strong product leaders. The reverse holds: weak PMs are almost always a direct result of weak product leaders ([[2023-12-05-cagan-alternatives-to-product-leaders|canon]]). When leadership can't or won't provide [[2020-01-09-cagan-coaching-strategic-context|strategic context]] or coach, the org defaults to (a) command-and-control + stakeholder roadmaps + feature teams + mercenaries, (b) leading through process, or (c) leading through proxies (PMOs, process people) — all three are documented failure modes ([[2023-12-05-cagan-alternatives-to-product-leaders|canon]]). This diagnostic protects [[trust-over-control]], [[empowered-with-problems-to-solve]], [[focus]], and the entire competency layer ([[product-leadership]], [[product-manager]], [[product-designer]], [[engineers]]).

## Signals & symptoms
**Healthy:**
- Leaders deliver the five **leadership** elements ([[2018-10-31-cagan-empowered-product-teams|canon]]): product vision, product strategy, product principles, product priorities, product evangelism.
- They deliver the three **management** elements: staffing (hire/develop/replace), coaching (weekly 1:1s), objectives (problems with measurable outcomes).
- Weekly 1:1s with PMs are *sacred* — 30+ minutes, never canceled ([[2019-02-08-cagan-coaching-tools-the-one-on-one|canon]]).
- Leaders coach using written-narrative discipline ([[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|canon]]); they have strong opinions but make space for the PM to think.
- Leadership runs active strategy management: removing obstacles, escalating decisions, circulating learnings ([[2020-02-21-cagan-product-strategy-management|canon: servant leadership]]).
- Leaders are themselves credibly experienced PMs/designers/engineers — they can coach to competence ([[product-coach|canon: experience prerequisite]]).
- Strategic context is part of onboarding for every new PM ([[2020-01-09-cagan-coaching-strategic-context|canon]]).

**Warning:**
- 1:1s exist but are status updates dominated by the leader talking.
- Coaching reverts to micro-managing; the leader makes the calls and "delegates" execution.
- Vision exists but is rarely communicated; new hires can't articulate it.
- Strategic context is fragmented — PMs know the company mission but not the scorecard, or know objectives but no strategy.
- Performance reviews bring surprises (feedback should never surprise — [[2019-02-08-cagan-coaching-tools-the-one-on-one|canon]]).

**Red flag:**
- Founder/CEO is the only product strategist; no leadership team exists.
- Leadership team is structurally absent — head of product reports to head of engineering, or there is no head of product.
- Stakeholders own product decisions; "head of product" facilitates ([[2023-12-05-cagan-alternatives-to-product-leaders|canon: the most common failure mode]]).
- Process people / PMOs have filled the leadership vacuum ([[2023-12-05-cagan-alternatives-to-product-leaders|canon: leading-with-process disease]]).
- "Empowerment" is interpreted as "leave teams alone" — teams flounder without context.
- Annual reviews are the development mechanism (Cagan: inadequate; weekly is required).
- Leaders cannot articulate the [[product-vision|vision]] or [[product-strategy|strategy]] without a deck.

## Rubric
| Level | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Leadership absent** | No head of product, or head of product is a project manager. Stakeholders own product decisions. Process people fill the vacuum. PMs are unmanaged or managed by engineering managers who can't coach product. |
| **2** | **Leadership in title** | Heads exist but lead through command-and-control or through process. 1:1s are status. Strategic context is fragmented. Vision is stale or absent. Performance review is the development tool. Empowerment is "leave teams alone." |
| **3** | **Servant leadership** | Five leadership elements present and active. Weekly 1:1s sacred. Strategic context onboarded. Active strategy management. Leaders coach to competence. Org has visible missionary culture. |
| **4** | **Exemplary** | Above + leaders are themselves continuously coached; they measure themselves by promotions and successors produced ([[2019-04-24-cagan-the-greatest-coach|Bill Campbell's yardstick]]). The leadership team is the company's best evangelist for the vision. Insights flow up and across teams routinely. Departing leaders leave a stronger bench than they arrived to. |

## Socratic questions
1. State the [[product-vision]] for your area without notes. State the [[product-strategy]] — the 2–3 problems you've chosen to focus on this year, and the insight behind each.
2. When was your last 1:1 with each direct report? Were you the one talking more, or were they?
3. Pick a PM on your team. What are their top three skill gaps right now ([[pm-competency|importance × capability]])? When did you last actively coach to one of them?
4. Describe the last week. What obstacles did you remove for your teams? What decisions did you escalate? What insights did you circulate to other teams? ([[2020-02-21-cagan-product-strategy-management|canon]].)
5. Are you a strong enough PM/designer/engineer yourself that the people you manage learn from you? If not, who is coaching *you* on that gap?
6. Read your last performance review of a direct report. Was anything in there a surprise to them? (If yes — feedback failure.)
7. If you were hit by a bus tomorrow, would two people on your team be ready to step into your role? What gaps would they need to close, and what's your plan to close those? ([[2019-04-24-cagan-the-greatest-coach|the Coach Campbell yardstick]].)
8. If a team came to you tomorrow and said "we don't think this stakeholder request will work" — what happens next? ([[2014-06-13-cagan-good-product-team-bad-product-team|canon: team should not need permission to push back]].)

## What "good" looks like
A head of product who can speak the vision/strategy without notes, runs sacred weekly 1:1s with each direct report, coaches with the written-narrative tool, removes obstacles for teams, escalates decisions stakeholders try to short-circuit, and circulates learnings across the org. Each of their direct reports does the same with their PMs/designers/engineers. The org has a leadership bench — multiple credible candidates for any leadership role. Leadership turnover doesn't break strategy because strategy and vision are jointly owned. New PMs are onboarded into the five elements of strategic context. The whole leadership team measures itself, in part, by who they've developed and promoted ([[2019-04-24-cagan-the-greatest-coach|canon]]).

## Interventions & experiments
- **If at Level 1 (absent):** the org needs to hire or promote credible product leaders — there is no software substitute. Until then, expect feature teams to dominate ([[2023-12-05-cagan-alternatives-to-product-leaders|canon: no real alternative]]). Founders acting as head of product can be effective at small scale; budget for transition early — premature hiring usually fails because the new leader doesn't yet have founder-level product knowledge ([[2023-12-05-cagan-alternatives-to-product-leaders|canon]]).
- **If at Level 2 (in title):** (a) Establish the **weekly 1:1 as sacred** ([[2019-02-08-cagan-coaching-tools-the-one-on-one|canon]]) — never canceled, primarily for the PM's development, leader listens more than talks; (b) Write down the five elements of strategic context ([[2020-01-09-cagan-coaching-strategic-context|canon]]) — if any are missing, fix the missing one before adding more cadences; (c) Run the [[2019-04-08-cagan-coaching-tools-the-assessment|PM assessment]] on each direct report and build a [[coaching-the-pm|coaching plan]]; (d) Force the written-narrative habit for significant decisions ([[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|canon]]) — it surfaces leader gaps as well as PM gaps; (e) Tighten obstacle-removal — leaders make a weekly list of teams' blockers and personally drive each.
- **If at Level 3:** invest in **leadership coaching** — get the head of product their own coach, internal or external ([[2026-02-04-cagan-product-coaching-and-ai|canon: SVPG concentrates human coaching on product leaders]]). Build the bench by giving 1–2 direct reports stretch assignments now.
- **Common traps:** (1) substituting process for leadership — PMOs and frameworks don't coach ([[2023-12-05-cagan-alternatives-to-product-leaders|canon]]); (2) leadership = pulling, management = pushing; weak orgs over-index on management, exemplary orgs do both ([[2015-06-27-cagan-leadership-vs-management|canon]]); (3) confusing servant leadership with abdication — leaders must coach, prioritize, and remove obstacles, not "stay out of the way"; (4) thinking annual reviews can develop people — weekly is the only cadence that works ([[2019-02-08-cagan-coaching-tools-the-one-on-one|canon]]).

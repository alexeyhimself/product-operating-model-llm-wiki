# Stakeholder Relationship Health — Diagnostic

> The PM owns **business viability** ([[the-four-big-risks|canon]]) — and viability is the consent and constraints of stakeholders (sales, marketing, finance, legal, support, security, ops). When the PM has earned stakeholder trust, viability is solvable; when not, the team defaults to design-by-committee or stakeholder-driven roadmaps. Canon: [[2014-06-13-cagan-good-product-team-bad-product-team|*Good Product Team / Bad Product Team*]] (2014); [[2010-01-26-cagan-regaining-your-product-mojo|*Regaining Your Product Mojo*]] (2010); [[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|*The Written Narrative*]] (2019).

## Why it matters
Stakeholder dynamics determine which of two failure modes a team falls into when viability gets hard: (a) **design-by-committee** — PM convenes execs and lets the loudest win, producing weak compromise products ([[2016-05-03-cagan-product-success|canon]]); (b) **stakeholder-driven roadmap** — execs/stakeholders dictate features and the team becomes a feature factory ([[2010-08-16-cagan-top-10-reasons-for-weak-product|canon #2]]). Healthy relationships convert stakeholders from sources-of-requirements into *constraints the team understands and creatively solves around*. This diagnostic protects [[empowered-with-problems-to-solve]], [[focus]], the PM's value+viability ownership ([[product-manager]]), and the integrity of [[product-strategy|product strategy]].

## Signals & symptoms
**Healthy:**
- Stakeholders feel *understood* — the PM has visibly done the homework on each function's constraints (sales, finance, legal, marketing, support).
- Stakeholders bring problems and constraints, not solutions and deadlines.
- The PM uses [[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|written narratives]] for significant decisions; arguments are made with data, not blustering.
- When stakeholders disagree, the PM brings prototypes and user evidence to the conversation — not opinions.
- "Mutual trust/respect with stakeholders incl. senior leadership" ([[2019-04-08-cagan-coaching-tools-the-assessment|PM Assessment item]] #11) rates 7+ for senior PMs.

**Warning:**
- Stakeholders show up with feature requests by name and dates attached.
- The PM avoids difficult conversations until they explode in meetings.
- Decisions made by the highest-paid person in the room ([[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|canon]] — sign of poor PM homework).
- The CEO is "the only person who knows all the constraints" ([[2016-05-03-cagan-product-success|canon]]).
- Product is treated as a service organization fulfilling stakeholder orders ([[2010-01-26-cagan-regaining-your-product-mojo|canon]]).

**Red flag:**
- Stakeholders bypass the team entirely — go directly to engineers.
- The "roadmap" is a "fair" allocation of engineering capacity across business units (the peanut-butter pattern).
- Big features arrive as fait accompli from quarterly business reviews.
- PM is regarded as a project manager / "herder of cats" ([[2019-08-29-cagan-product-vs-feature-teams|canon]]).
- Stakeholders publicly blame the team for outcomes the team was never empowered to influence ([[2019-08-29-cagan-product-vs-feature-teams|canon]]).

## Rubric
| Level | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Stakeholder-driven** | Roadmap = stakeholder requests; PM is order-taker; design-by-committee meetings; no homework. Product treated as service org. |
| **2** | **Tense / transactional** | PM negotiates with stakeholders but has not earned trust; conversations are positional; trade-offs decided by seniority not insight. |
| **3** | **Trust-based** | PM has demonstrated customer / data / domain / business expertise. Stakeholders bring problems, constraints, and context — and trust the team to find the *how*. Written narratives precede significant decisions. |
| **4** | **Exemplary** | Above + stakeholders are missionaries for the product strategy. PM is regarded as a future leader of the company ([[2019-08-29-cagan-product-vs-feature-teams|canon]]). When a stakeholder is convinced an idea won't work, they call the PM first. The PM proactively previews concerns so stakeholders never feel blindsided. |

## Socratic questions
1. Name your top five stakeholders. For each: what's their #1 constraint right now, and how is your work helping them with it?
2. When did you last *change a stakeholder's mind* using data or a prototype? Tell me the story.
3. When did you last *get a stakeholder's mind changed* by their evidence? What did you change?
4. The last big decision — was it made by the people in the room, by the highest-paid person, or by the strongest written argument? ([[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|canon]].)
5. Is there a stakeholder who would say "the PM understands my world better than anyone outside my org"? Name them. If not — that's the gap.
6. Are you using the written-narrative tool for significant decisions? If not, why not? (Most PMs resist this because it's painful — and the ones who need it most resist it most — [[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|canon]].)
7. From the [[2010-01-26-cagan-regaining-your-product-mojo|10 mojo moves]]: when was the last time you brought *seven actual customer conversations* into a meeting with an executive?

## What "good" looks like
The PM is the company's acknowledged expert on customers, data, and product, *and* is regarded as the person who understands how each stakeholder function works. Significant decisions are preceded by written narratives that surface the homework. Trade-offs happen with prototypes, data, and explicit principles ([[2006-01-02-cagan-the-product-manifesto|canon]]) — not seniority. Stakeholders bring problems and constraints, and trust the team to find the solution; the team brings evidence back. When the team chooses *not* to do something a stakeholder wanted, the stakeholder can explain *why* — because the PM previewed the reasoning. The relationship runs both directions; the PM hears stakeholder constraints first, not last.

## Interventions & experiments
- **If at Level 1 (stakeholder-driven):** the org-level fix is leadership + strategy ([[leadership-readiness]], [[product-strategy-quality]]) — there is no PM hack that survives a stakeholder-roadmap operating model. While that's being addressed, the [[2010-01-26-cagan-regaining-your-product-mojo|Mojo 10]] are the PM's individual moves: customer expertise; rapid-response team; UX design; data not opinions; involve executives weekly; evangelize.
- **If at Level 2 (transactional):** (a) Run a stakeholder mapping — top 5–10 stakeholders, their constraints, what they need to succeed; (b) Schedule a single 30-min listening session with each per quarter; ask what they're trying to achieve; (c) Adopt the **written-narrative habit** for the next significant decision ([[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|canon]]) — it forces homework and gives stakeholders a real artifact to react to; (d) Stop convening "alignment meetings" with no narrative — those become design-by-committee.
- **If at Level 3:** invest in *previewing* — proactively show stakeholders evolving prototypes and data so they're never surprised by a decision; build personal credibility by being early to flag risks to their function (legal/privacy/security/sales) before they're problems.
- **Common traps:** (1) confusing "consensus" with "viability solved" — viability means *workable for the business*, not *unanimously approved*; (2) PM as therapist — endless listening without converting input into prioritized action erodes trust as much as ignoring stakeholders; (3) over-reliance on the CEO/founder to broker stakeholder relationships — non-scalable and a tell of weak PM ([[2016-05-03-cagan-product-success|canon]]); (4) running narratives as PowerPoint — Cagan: the written narrative makes argument gaps obvious; PowerPoint hides them ([[2019-03-11-cagan-coaching-tools-the-narrative|canon]]).

## When a specific stakeholder is objecting
When a specific constituency is publicly resisting the model (CEO, board, LOB, sales leader, CFO, CIO, CMO, CS leader, PMO director, or internal), use [[overcoming-objections]] as the coaching companion — Cagan devotes an entire Part of *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* (Chs 36–46) to per-constituency objection patterns and SVPG's responses. Rebuilding a Level-1 → Level-3 relationship almost always requires an objection-handling conversation first, before the mojo moves or written-narrative discipline will land.

## What "healthy" looks like (Ch 25 → partnership hub)
*[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* Ch 25 (Partnering with Stakeholders) is Cagan's clearest positive statement of the healthy state this diagnostic scores against: **empowered teams as collaborative partners with stakeholders (no longer subservient, still dependent)**; the PM engaging directly with each relevant stakeholder to learn constraints; **showing the prototype before building** when a proposed solution might impact a stakeholder; transparency (stakeholders welcome in user testing, prototype reviews, product data). *"Sometimes, getting a solution that works not only for customers, but also for multiple stakeholders — each with different needs — may require many iterations to identify a solution that solves for all parties. Further, the solution that solves for all parties may end up being something that none of the stakeholders had imagined was possible."* Ch 25 also names two anti-patterns central to this diagnostic: **committees don't innovate** (Level-1 design-by-committee root cause); and **expecting stakeholders to trust a PM who hasn't done the work** — *"is not only unrealistic, but also unwise."* Product leadership owes stakeholders a competent PM. The full Part VI framing sits on [[cross-functional-partnering]].

# Cagan — Tools and Processes

> **Citation:** Marty Cagan · "Tools and Processes" · svpg.com, 2018-07-26 (follow-up to [[2018-06-30-cagan-revenge-of-the-pmo|*Revenge of the PMO*]]; closes with a nod to Ken Norton's "The Tools Don't Matter") — [read the original](https://www.svpg.com/tools-and-processes/). Slim provenance card; the article is **not reproduced** here.

## Role
**Primary (SVPG canon).** Cagan's clearest published statement that **there is no *one right way* of doing product** — and therefore every tool/method/process **embeds beliefs about teams, leadership, and how value is created**. Written to answer two objections to *Revenge of the PMO*: (1) "any tool can be used for good or bad" and (2) "you always said culture matters more than process — why complain about a process?"

## What it backs
- concepts: [[principles-over-process]] (tools/processes encode belief systems; process people institutionalize a specific one), [[product-culture]] (choices about tools shape empowerment/transparency/innovation), [[small-frequent-uncoupled-releases|small frequent releases]] (the Scrum-cadence example)
- competencies: [[product-ops]] (why tool selection is not a junior job — see the roadmap-tool trap), [[product-leadership]]
- frameworks: [[okrs]] (the OKR-tool example: good tools *enforce* transparency; bad ones let teams hide), [[outcome-based-roadmap]] (the roadmap-tool trap — request-tallying tools push teams toward stakeholder-driven prioritization)
- entities: [[marty-cagan]]
- **Companion to:** [[process-people]] — this article names *no-one-right-way* as the reason "standardizing process across a large organization just means institutionalizing a poor fit for most teams." The 2021 essay generalizes that observation to the *people* who do the standardizing.

## Notes for the coach
- **The central claim:** every tool/method is *trying to make certain things easier and other things harder*. That "harder" side is the tell — it reveals which behaviors the author of the tool wants to encourage or suppress. Vendor marketing won't tell you; you have to look at what the tool *makes hard*.
- **OKR-tool example (good):** to support transparency, the tools make it easy to search whether any other team is working on a given objective — and most make it *hard not to be transparent*. Cagan considers this a good thing. Someone with a different view of empowerment might not.
- **Roadmap-tool example (bad):** tools that make it easy to "tally requests from stakeholders and prospective customers so the PM can make a data-driven decision" institutionalize [stakeholder-driven prioritization](wiki/concepts/outcome-based-roadmap.md), which Cagan considers **exactly the wrong approach to winning products**. Teams forced to use such a tool "have to fight the tool to do good work."
- **Scrum/Agile-cadence example:** moving to two-week releases is a fundamental cultural shift (design, engineering, QA, deployment, product marketing). Teams that don't share the underlying belief in [[small-frequent-uncoupled-releases|frequent small releases]] circumvent it — QA per sprint, but only actually release every 5–6 sprints — surfacing what they *actually* believe.
- **The article's punchline:** "You have to look closely at what behaviors and activities the tool, method or process is actively encouraging, and which they are discouraging, and you need to be sure this is aligned with what you want for your team and your organization." This is the *specific mechanism* by which [[principles-over-process]] operates in tool/method choice.

# Cagan — Product Discovery

> **Citation:** Marty Cagan · "Product Discovery" · svpg.com, 2007-09-25 — [read the original](https://www.svpg.com/product-discovery/). Slim provenance card; the article is **not reproduced** here. **The article that coins the term** — Cagan's earliest published use of "product discovery" as the deliberate replacement for "requirements and design."

## Role
**Primary (SVPG canon) — root/origin.** Predates [[2009-10-12-cagan-product-discovery-plan|The Product Discovery Plan]] (2009, the operational how-to already grounding [[product-discovery]]) by two years. Where the 2009 article gives the *mechanics*, this 2007 article gives the *definition and the reason the discipline exists at all* — it is the wiki's earliest-dated primary source for the concept the page is named after.

## Notes for the coach
- **The two-part definition, verbatim in spirit:** (1) discover whether real users want this — validate the market opportunity; (2) discover a product *solution* that is usable, useful, and feasible — design and validate it with customers and engineering. Market discovery is usually the easier half; **solution discovery is where most of the real difficulty lives** — a point the wiki's [[product-discovery|discovery page]] already makes via later sources (2020's "most discovery time is solution work"), now traceable to this 2007 origin.
- **The pharma analogy** — a strong teaching device: drug companies rarely struggle to find a worthwhile problem (saving/extending/improving a life); the entire uncertainty is in solution discovery, and the industry prices that uncertainty in. Software teams face the same solution-discovery uncertainty but, unlike pharma, often refuse to acknowledge or budget for it.
- **Quote magnet — the core economic argument:** without discovery, a company uses "the full engineering team for full release cycles to build the software that is then QA'ed and deployed into production systems... they are using the engineering organization to build a very, very expensive prototype, and they use their live customers as unwitting test subjects." This is the earliest, sharpest statement of why [[minimize-waste]] matters and why a cheap prototyper-led discovery phase (weeks) should precede expensive full-engineering delivery (months).
- **Causal link to startup failure**, stated plainly here: most startups don't have the runway to spend 1–2 years discovering the right solution the expensive way — "so they hire their engineers, take their best shot, and see what happens." A concrete line to use when coaching a founder who's skipping discovery "to move fast."
- **Management's two fears**, named directly: (1) discovery is unpredictable — no guaranteed deliverable, unlike Scrum's sprint cadence; (2) engineers "sitting around" during discovery looks like idle expensive resource. Cagan's counter: it's exactly this reasoning that causes the *real* waste (the expensive-prototype pattern above).

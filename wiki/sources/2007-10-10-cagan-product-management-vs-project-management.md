# Cagan — Product Management vs. Project Management

> **Citation:** Marty Cagan · "Product Management vs. Project Management" · svpg.com, 2007-10-10 — [read the original](https://www.svpg.com/product-management-vs-project-management/). Slim provenance card; the article is **not reproduced** here. A 2007 historical piece on why the two roles get conflated and why internet-services companies need to separate them.

## Role
**Primary (SVPG canon) — historical.** The origin explanation, from early in Cagan's writing, of *why* product management and project management get combined (a holdover from the shipped-software era, where Microsoft's "Program Manager" title covered both) and why the web-services "train" release model breaks that combination. Complements the later [[2016-12-13-cagan-product-manager-vs-product-owner-revisited|PM-vs-PO]] distinction with an earlier, different-axis one (PM vs *project* manager, not PM vs product *owner*).

## Notes for the coach
- **The historical explanation:** shipped-software companies (Microsoft) had product manager cover project management because a "product" and a "project" were the same granularity and frequency. Early internet companies (Netscape, Yahoo) copied this — and it broke, because web services run many releases in parallel on a shared codebase.
- **The "train model."** Internet companies moved to frequent, smaller releases on a common code base running faster than any single project's duration — "parallel development and the software train model of releases." A release train needs its own dedicated, cross-cutting project management, distinct from any one product manager's scope. Some companies called the train's project manager its **"conductor."** This is a useful pre-history for [[delivery-manager|the delivery manager role]] (formally named by Cagan in 2014) — the train/conductor language is an earlier, more concrete image of the same underlying need.
- **The conclusion, stated plainly:** "if you use the train model, and you have project managers dedicated to the release trains, you generally don't need product managers to cover project management too." For companies still shipping packaged software, Cagan still recommends separating the roles — but for a different reason: **discovery-oriented product management vs. execution-oriented project management are different natures of work that attract different people.**
- Distinct from [[2016-12-13-cagan-product-manager-vs-product-owner-revisited|PM vs. PO]] (product manager vs. Agile product owner) — this article is about product manager vs. *project* manager, an older and separate conflation.

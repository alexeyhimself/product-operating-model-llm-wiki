# Cagan — Transformation Politics

> **Citation:** Marty Cagan · "Transformation Politics" · svpg.com, 2024-11-21 — [read the original](https://www.svpg.com/transformation-politics/). Slim provenance card; the article is **not reproduced** here.

## Role
**Primary (SVPG canon).** Cagan addresses transformation politics head-on — referencing Christian Idiodi's framing: "every problem is a people problem." Eight named political dimensions, each with specific guidance: Product Leaders, Product Managers, Change Management, Pilot Teams, Product Discovery, Product Strategy, Keeping The Lights On, Internal Evangelism. Source for the **sequencing refinement** that updates Moore's earlier "pursue all three dimensions in parallel" guidance — Cagan recommends holding the *Decide* change until the *Solve* and *Build* changes have won stakeholder confidence.

## Notes for the coach
- **"Trust the stakeholders on the problem"** — counter-intuitive but politically essential for pilot teams. Stakeholders have lived with the problems for years; spending months on problem discovery before solving is politically corrosive. Cagan: the pilot team should *trust* the stakeholder's framing of the problem, then race to discover a successful solution. Caveat: if testing reveals the problem is wrong, the team will see it quickly.
- **Hold strategy change for last** — "you will likely have better results holding off on changing how you decide which problems to solve until after you have largely won the hearts and minds of the stakeholders and executives." This is the sequencing refinement of [[2022-05-31-moore-transformation-defined|Moore's earlier 'pursue in parallel' guidance]]. Both are canon; Cagan's later guidance applies to politically constrained orgs (= most legacy orgs).
- **"The product leader is judged by her weakest product manager"** — flag for any leadership-readiness conversation.
- **Drumbeat of outcomes** — orgs typically need 1–3 years for transformation, but cannot wait that long for results. Internal evangelism = constant cadence of real business outcomes, no less than monthly, *not* activity reports. "Don't confuse business results with activities."
- **"Keep the lights on"** — every product team has a stakeholder obligation to handle minor items that keep the business running. If they don't, stakeholders will insist on resources for it one way or another. Easy to under-budget in pilot teams.
- **Pilot team design checklist** — pick people, problem, outcome, measure, training, coaching, *size* (smaller is faster/easier on every axis), *not* heavily dependent on un-done tech-debt replatforming, problem that's meaningful to the business (not just to customers).

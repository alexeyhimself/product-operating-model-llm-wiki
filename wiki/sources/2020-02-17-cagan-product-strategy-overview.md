# Cagan — Product Strategy: Overview

> **Citation:** Marty Cagan · "Product Strategy – Overview" · svpg.com, 2020-02-17 — [read the original](https://www.svpg.com/product-strategy-overview/). Slim provenance card; the article is **not reproduced** here.

## Role
**Primary (SVPG canon).** Cagan's foundational essay on what product strategy is and why so many companies don't have one. Defines product strategy as **how we make the [[product-vision|vision]] a reality, while meeting the needs of the business**. Names the four things product strategy requires: **focus** (tough choices), **insights** (study and thought), **action** (converting insights into team objectives), and **management** (servant-leadership without micromanagement). This four-part decomposition is what later becomes the [[principles-of-product-strategy|four product-strategy principles]]. Recommends Rumelt's *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy* and quotes him on "bad strategy is the active avoidance of the hard work of crafting a good strategy."

## Notes for the coach
- **The four pillars** (focus, insights, action, management) directly map to the [[principles-of-product-strategy|four product-strategy principles]]. Use this article to teach the *reasoning behind* the principles, not just the labels.
- **The "Underpants Business" gag** Cagan cites is a useful icebreaker when a user shows you a "strategy" that is really a feature list and a hoped-for outcome.
- **Quotable Rumelt.** "Bad strategy is the active avoidance of the hard work of crafting a good strategy. One common reason for choosing avoidance is the pain or difficulty of choice." Use when leadership keeps adding initiatives instead of cutting them.
- **Strategy vs. tactics distinction.** "Strategy doesn't cover the details; those are the tactics we'll use to achieve the goal." Helpful when a user conflates a roadmap with a strategy.
- **Cagan's own ranking.** He calls product strategy harder and more important than product discovery — even though discovery is his favorite activity. Useful when prioritizing where to coach a product leader.

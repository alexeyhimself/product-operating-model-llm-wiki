# Positioning

> *"The place your product holds in the minds of customers."* [[loved|LOVED]] Chs 21 + 24. The long game to [[messaging|messaging]]'s short game. *"Positioning always starts with elements of messaging, but it's made real through the combination of all the activities reinforcing a bigger story."*

## Why it matters
Ch 21 opens with **Brendan O'Connor at an RSA cocktail party.** Former Chief Trust Officer at Salesforce, Security CTO at ServiceNow — now co-founder of AppOmni, testing a new-cloud-security talk track on every industry peer who asked *"What are you up to these days?"*.

Nearly everyone's response, even from deeply respected experts, was: *"Is it similar to what [company X] is doing?"* or *"Oh, you mean like what [existing cloud security tool] does?"*

Lauchengco's takeaway: *"It was a powerful reminder that people need to start with what's familiar to understand what's new."*

## Positioning vs Messaging — the load-bearing distinction
- **Positioning** — the place your product holds in the minds of customers. How they know what you do and how you differ.
- **Messaging** — the key things you say to reinforce your positioning. Makes you credible so people want to learn more.

**Positioning is the long game. Messaging is the short game.** Messaging adjusts to what's relevant now / a given context / a specific campaign. In aggregate, it reinforces the desired position.

*"Positioning should be thought of as the outcome of all the activities done for a product go-to-market, not just its messaging. But because messaging is within your control and so essential to telling the story of your product, it's the focus for the rest of this section."* (Ch 21)

## Positioning takes time — the Microsoft Office case (Ch 21)
When Microsoft Office was introduced, Lauchengco's team *"changed every reference of desktop productivity application to integrated office suite. It was part of a systematic effort to reinforce the story we wanted told: integrated office suites were the category."*

*"I was sick of writing and saying it after a couple years and remember thinking surely the market knows this by now? But our sales numbers told another story. Our biggest revenue months happened just as we were about to launch our new version nearly two years later."*

The generalizable rule: **the dogged positioning investment sets you up to accelerate more quickly through the fat parts of the bell curve** ([[technology-adoption-life-cycle]]).

## Where positioning gets made real
Ch 21's operational insight: positioning isn't only in messaging.
- **Sales evaluation processes** — an assessment guide with strict criteria reinforcing your position frames how customers look not just at your product but at competitors.
- **Sales training on competitive response** — how sales answers competition questions creates positive or negative bias for the rest of the buying process.
- **Word-of-mouth** — comparison sites, reviews, ratings, social postings, shares, forums, others' content, employee buzz.
- **Unseen activity** — dark-funnel influence beyond visible channels.

*"This collective sentiment has enormous impact on how the world thinks about a product."*

## Discover your position — the iterative process (Ch 21)
Positioning is **discovered**, not declared:
- **Test talk tracks in real conversations** (Brendan at RSA).
- **Look for gaps in market understanding** — you're identifying what your messaging must bridge.
- **Listen for how people place you** — they'll compare you to what they already know.
- **Adapt based on every conversation.**

*"Iterative learning like this lets you discover what customers need to hear. You're looking for gaps in market understanding so you know what your messaging must bridge."*

## Right message, right time — the timing balancing act (Ch 24)
Ch 24 pairs two Lauchengco stories:

### Microsoft Office Internet-ready features
Pre-launch focus groups asked people to stack cards describing features by value. *"Not one of the dozens of participants had Internet-ready features anywhere close to the top of their card stack."* But *"inside the tech bubble, the Internet was all everyone was talking about."*

The team faced the classic dilemma: market ahead of what customers valued (to push the market) or market what they told us they prioritized now?

*"It felt risky at the time, but we decided Internet-ready features should lead our marketing, despite what customers told us. It didn't take long for it to be clear we'd made the right call."*

### Loudcloud (the failed early move)
Loudcloud was *"the first to offer Internet infrastructure as a service and talk about services in the cloud."* Focus groups' closest analog was "managed service" — but they felt that wasn't big enough. They tried to change minds:
- *"Cloud like a power utility that you turned on and used dynamically on-demand."*
- Marc Andreessen (co-founder + co-author of the first widely used browser) as spokesperson.
- WIRED cover story.

*"But it wasn't enough. Back then, not enough people recognized the sea change we saw. There wasn't common language to build on or a groundswell in the industry of others doing similar things. In 2000, the world was struggling to comprehend a cloud service — a ubiquitous concept today. We were too early."*

**Lauchengco's rule:**
> *"Whether or not your story is right for right now requires judgment steeped in deep knowledge of customers, the industry, and technology trends. If you're pushing the boundaries of what people believe, you need to show the industry is moving with you. **Competition isn't a crowd; it's confirmation.**"*

*"Concrete evidence — industry trends, customer stories, adoption, pundits, data — is at the heart of messaging and positioning feeling 'right' at any given time."*

## Create new category vs redefine existing? (Ch 24, Table 24.1)
Ch 24 pushes back on the "must define a new category" school. Lauchengco: *"In general, creating a truly new category is really hard and demands a lot of persistence, time, and resources. There are far more examples of great success stories from those who redefine existing categories than of those who defined something truly new."*

**Ch 24 Table 24.1 examples:**

| Created new category | Redefined existing category |
|---|---|
| Amazon (the store) · AWS · Netflix · RedHat | Google → surpassed Yahoo · iPhone → surpassed Blackberry · Facebook → surpassed MySpace/Friendster · Salesforce → surpassed Siebel · Slack → surpassed Hipchat · Spotify → surpassed Pandora · SpaceX → now used by NASA · Microsoft in nearly every business · Tesla redefining electric cars |

**Ch 24's guidance:** *"True category creation is not a marketing event. It requires an industry shift and others to follow. To create a new category, you need great product strategy, business strategy, marketing strategy, exceptional leadership, resources, luck, and consistent execution over time. There must be ample evidence you can point to outside of your company — and in your customer's world — that confirms a shift is occurring."*

**The alternative:** *"In technology, all categories break into smaller subcategories. The boundaries constantly adjust to new technology, trends, and many new companies jumping in every year. That's why it's easier to redraw lines around existing categories than it is to create new ones. You have that 'You are here' marker from which to anchor conversations."*

Whichever path: *"It must be brought to life with a complete story. It should include industry thought pieces, data, videos, customer stories, and evangelists who your audience finds credible."*

## Who to leverage (Ch 24)
Ch 24's three internal partners in positioning:

- **Product managers** — hold the product vision + strategy. They should have a clear sense of how the future intersects with what a product does now. Inspire story ideas + ensure technical accuracy.
- **Sales** — critical ally in developing messaging that works. *"They have to say it daily in a way that makes customers feel like they're not being sold. Don't hand sales finished messaging; collaborate on it together and validate it with the field."* Ch 24 case: a company where CEO's messaging vs marketing's vs head-of-sales' were A/B-tested — sales lead's version outperformed CEO's by **1000%**. *"The alternatives relied too much on jargon and felt less authentic."*
- **Search trends + techniques** — search trends · journey testing · targeted ad buys (test *directions*, not variations on a theme) · keyword audits.

## The balancing act
Ch 24's closing: *"The earlier in a product's life you are, the more you must tell a story using the pain and problems that make you necessary. Create mental connectors to help people understand what you do. It's also the time to define or redefine how the world sees your category."*

*"The more mature you are, the more understood your product and category is. Product messaging can be more aspirational in mature categories. It's equally important to focus on building toward a longer-term position that redraws the goalposts of your category and leans them toward your company's vision."*

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Announcing your positioning as a marketing event.** Positioning is earned across every interaction, over time.
- **Trying to create a category before the industry is moving with you.** Loudcloud in 2000. Wait for confirmation from competition, adoption, pundits, data.
- **Ignoring how customers actually compare you** to what they already know (RSA cocktail party).
- **Marketing what focus groups ask for.** Sometimes the strategic call is to lead the market. Judge based on deep customer + industry + technology knowledge.
- **Assuming category creation is the only ambition.** Redefining existing categories has more winners.
- **Jargon-first messaging that felt endorsed by the CEO** — sales-tested authentic language wins.
- **Positioning as a one-and-done exercise.** Even mature companies constantly redraw goalposts.

# PMM Fundamental 4 — Evangelist

> **Enable Others to Tell the Story.** The fourth of Martina Lauchengco's four fundamentals of [[product-marketing|product marketing]] ([[loved|LOVED]] Ch 6). *"The systematic enabling of influence through others."* Evangelism is a team sport; PMM is the catalyst and director on behalf of the product.

## Why it matters
Ch 6 opens with **Quizlet**: used every day in nearly every country to study for medical school, pass beautician exams, memorize grocery store codes; **1 in 2 US high schoolers** have used it. For its first 10 years, the company *"didn't spend a dime on marketing."* The evidence is on social — #quizlet and #thanksquizlet: teachers sharing photos of excited students playing Quizlet Live; graduates with *"Thanks Quizlet"* on their mortarboards. *"No one asked these people to do it. They're just so genuinely grateful for what the product does, they want to share it with the world."*

The Quizlet case is the extreme end of organic evangelism. Most products don't get there without deliberate PMM work. Ch 6's core claim: *"the mediums have evolved and expanded in reach and importance. Their impact is far beyond what official company marketing channels can do."*

## What "evangelism" means here
Lauchengco's definition: *"the systematic enabling of influence through others."* This includes:
- **Traditional amplifiers of GTM reach** — sales, press, investors, analysts.
- **The go-to-market engine's specialists** — social media marketers, content marketers, PR, analyst relations, technical evangelists, community managers, field marketing managers, event managers, partner marketing, customer success, sales managers.

For the PMM: *"the fourth fundamental is about identifying the most important parts of this mix and applying them strategically to bring a product to market."*

## Enabling Others
*"Some evangelists matter more than others."* Priority is contextual:
- **If you have a direct sales force**, sales is *"product marketing's top priority to enable."*
- But sales can't win alone if **analysts** (from research firms) and **editors** (in app stores or Tier 1 publications) aren't enabled with equal care.

The key question: **what type of advocacy matters most for your product's go-to-market, and what enables + activates it?**

### Ch 6's Word case — pre-launch evangelism activation
When Microsoft Word became *"the best reviewed word processor of its time"*, the pre-launch PMM work activated evangelism deliberately:
- **A detailed evaluation guide**, sent whenever the product went to reviewers. A similar guide made available to sales reps.
- **Meeting the most influential pundits in person** and answering their questions live.
- **In-depth training of the field** on the sales presentation and demo.
- **Partnering with product on the beta program** so reference customers provided testimonials that informed direct-mail messaging and ads.

Lauchengco's key line: *"Word couldn't claim leadership; pundits, reviewers, partners, and customers had to give it to us. It took consistent effort enabling the right key influencers to get there."*

### Product-led growth as a form of evangelism
Ch 6 explicitly points out that Slack's initial evangelists were *users getting teammates to join Slack workspaces and immediately getting the team productive*. PLG is often led by growth and/or product teams — but *"product marketing must make sure the rest of the product's evangelism efforts complement that GTM model and are effective."*

### Sales reps as evangelists — with the right ammunition
Ch 6's discipline for enabling sales: *"having a story and tools that let them sound like genuine advocates; they don't want to sound like they're hard selling."* And critically — *"they also need to arm their internal champions with tools that let them evangelize on the product's behalf when seeking internal approval. I've seen many internal 'sales decks' of internal advocates selling their leadership team on why expensive new software should be purchased — it's not always the rep doing that selling."* Enable the champion, not just the rep.

### Reduce friction to access information
*"Effective evangelism isn't just about finding the best marketing channels; it's about reducing friction to access information that lets people effectively assess and advocate for your product. Where should a sales rep look for the most up-to-date competitive responses? Will people like or share a video more if it's on YouTube versus a company website?"* PMM designs the paths, not just the messages.

## Evangelism vs Promotion
*"Almost every team is good at cranking out content that talks about the product. What's often lacking is making it compelling, credible, or desirable for others to talk about."* Ch 6's Table 6.1 contrasts product-first with evangelism-focused:

| Promoting Product | Enables Evangelism |
|---|---|
| Customer case studies stating key challenges + key results | **Interest-grabbing customer stories** (video, user-generated content, photo-based on social) |
| Product demo that walks through features by product screens | **Demo through a day-in-the-life of a customer** |
| Sales deck about the company, product, marketecture, and key features | **Slides showing what's changed in the world** that makes the need urgent and important, and how a customer like this one found value |

*"Case studies have a role — they just can't be the only way your customer stories get told."*

Lauchengco's counterintuitive framing: *"Sometimes it's important to lead with what makes people care."* Start a sales script with customer stories — not because it's more believable (though it is), but because *"whomever they're speaking with might learn something. Don't forget that people engage in discussions to learn, not to be lectured or sold to."*

## Tailor Evangelism Tools to Your Product's GTM
What enables evangelism depends on **who is talking and in what context**. Ch 6's examples:
- **Sales** — needs a well-defined playbook to engage on-message, use the right tools, follow good process. *"This can make prospects fans even if they don't become customers."*
- **Prospective customer switching from an existing product** — wants a community of like-minded people; support/community forums where they can ask candid questions.
- **Existing customer** — likes being recognized for what they do with the product; affinity space at an event or dinner where they can talk with prospects turns them into evangelists.
- **Key pundits and influencers (analysts)** — have regular cadences pushing key reports; the product-GTM plan must incorporate those timelines.

*"People always look for help in technology-buying decisions. In the absence of finding something about your product from a 'third party' the moment they search, a competitor fills the void."*

## Communities as an evangelism vector
Ch 6 names community as *"another common way to scale advocacy through others."* Three forms:
- **Starting a group** — Customer Council, Advisor Network.
- **Participating in groups that already exist.**
- **Joining third-party oversight organizations.**

*"Important in any community is that it feels like genuine dialogue that's helpful for the people in it."* Community as promotional-channel-in-disguise fails.

## Evangelism Is a Team Sport
Ch 6's closing framing: *"This work, more than any of the product marketer, is done together with product and go-to-market teams. Nearly everything is ultimately executed by others. Product marketing is the catalyst and director on behalf of the product."*

**Foundation dependency:** *"Product marketers use all they've learned and created in the first three fundamentals as a foundation on which this fourth gets built. But you need all four for a product's adoption."*

This is the load-bearing sequence:
1. [[pmm-ambassador|Ambassador]] — know your customer and market.
2. [[pmm-strategist|Strategist]] — direct the product's GTM plan with a clear why.
3. [[pmm-storyteller|Storyteller]] — position and message it so it lands.
4. **Evangelist** — enable everyone else (sales, marketing specialists, press, analysts, communities, customers) to tell the story so the product scales.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Product-first content only.** Cranking out feature-focused collateral without making it *worth others' effort* to talk about.
- **Sales enablement as feature training.** Reps who sound like they're reciting features — not like *"genuine advocates."*
- **Ignoring internal champions.** The rep pitching isn't always the one selling internally — arm the champion with a self-serve internal-approval deck too.
- **Community as promotional channel.** Fails the "genuine dialogue" test; costs trust.
- **Ignoring analyst cadences.** Pundit reports come out on schedules; missing them costs a full cycle of coverage.
- **Assuming "official" marketing does the positioning work.** *"In the absence of finding something about your product from a 'third party' the moment they search, a competitor fills the void."*

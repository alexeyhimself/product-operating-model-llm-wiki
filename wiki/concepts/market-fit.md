# Market Fit

> **"Discovering market pull."** [[loved|LOVED]] Ch 11's crisp definition: *"what makes a customer need or want your product enough that they take action to learn more, try, or buy. And what makes this pattern repeatable."* The **market side** of product-market fit — probed in parallel with product discovery, not after it.

## Why it matters
Ch 11 opens with the **Word for lawyers** case. In Lauchengco's last year on the Word team, WordPerfect still owned the legal market — despite Word being the de facto standard elsewhere. A national tour of non-Word law firms + hundreds of documents + dozens of in-depth interviews revealed formatting needs Word couldn't handle without major workarounds. Fixing them required a rewrite of Word's layout engine — the *"legal will love this"* version was a major release cycle away.

The PMM dedicated to legal came up with the interim fit: **focus marketing on law firms who were more technology-forward** (using PowerPoint for trial presentations, Excel for charts). Word eventually became the legal-market standard — but the case illustrates a key point: *"even for mature products, product/market fit is not a one-time thing. As products and markets evolve, the product needs to respond to the customers it wants to grow into. The go-to-market approach must respond to customer and market realities as they are. Each shapes the other."*

## The market side of product-market fit
Ch 11's most-quoted framing:
> *"I see so many companies find an initial beachhead of customers, think they have product/market fit, and then find their growth stalling. It's never about just one thing. But it's often because not enough attention was paid to market fit."*

Lauchengco's clarifications:
- Market fit and product fit are **discovered in parallel**, not sequentially. *"Discovering the product and its market aren't independent activities."*
- **Value discovery** (of the four risks) is where market fit lives. *"Beyond a product's usability, feasibility, and viability, product discovery work around value is intended to find this answer. Although it is the most difficult and important risk to probe, it tends to be underdeveloped."*
- **People say ≠ people do.** *"People say they will use or buy a lot of things, which is easy to confuse with product desirability. Not all tests and techniques are equal in placing decisions or actions into a realistic enough market context."*

**Market fit = what people are likely to *truly do* in real-life conditions.** *"How will they act when confronted with a crowded market, competing priorities, limited budgets, and a status quo that works well enough?"*

Value-discovery work must probe **beyond** *"would you buy this, would you buy it from us, and how much would you expect to pay?"* — assessing market fit requires probing more deeply on market conditions that motivate action or create urgency. Sometimes it's about distribution.

## Where market fit sits in the wiki's frame
- **Inside viability** ([[the-four-big-risks]]). The wiki uses *"market side of product-market fit"* as one of the questions viability answers — see [[pmm-ambassador]] and the [[the-four-big-risks]] sources note grounding LOVED Ch 3.
- **Complements [[product-discovery]].** Discovery answers whether the *product* is valuable/usable/feasible/viable; market fit answers whether the *market* will pull — will find, understand, try, buy, and recommend.
- **Owned by no single function.** *"Doing the work is not the exclusive domain of product marketing, but understanding and applying it to a product's go-to-market is."*

## Probe Early, Probe Often — the discovery discipline
Ch 11 groups market-fit probing questions into two sets:

### Value questions
- Who is most likely to use this? Who buys it? Who influences decisions?
- Do they prioritize the problem?
- What else are they considering that is similar?
- What have I said/shown you that is most compelling (**aha moment**)?
- **Relative-urgency probe:** *If you had 10 points in your budget to allocate to solve your most urgent problems, how many points would each get?*
- **Pricing probe:** How much would you expect to pay? Maximum? What else have you recently bought at this price?
- What new product have you most recently bought and why?

### Growth / Connection questions
- What created curiosity?
- **How would they describe this to a colleague?** (crucial for messaging insight)
- Where would they expect to see this product talked about?
- How would they expect to assess it?
- What would make them a raving fan?
- **Who are the next most likely market segments to adopt?** Do they feel the same sense of urgency? What market situations are necessary for them to act?

*"The questions don't need to be asked all the time — nor are they the only ones — but the collective discovery work should answer these more market-oriented questions."*

## Discovery techniques adapted for market fit (Ch 11)
Six adaptations of discovery techniques for market-fit-specific signal:

- **Exit surveys.** For immediate-exit website users — a one-question pop-up: *"Is there anything we could have done differently to make you stay?"*
- **A/B messaging test.** *"When you do A/B testing of messaging, don't just pay attention to what performs better. Ask what the relative performance differences tell you about market perception."* Are you positioning in the right category / to the right customers?
- **Demand-test variation.** On a landing-page demand test, ask only for a buy or trial commitment — separates the curious from those willing to take meaningful action. Be transparent on product state. Ask *"Why did you decide to buy?"* on the page.
- **Ad testing.** Test *vastly different directions* — aspirational, product-oriented, problem-oriented. *"You're not trying to optimize which is best; rather, you're trying to learn what direction gets people to act and what their relative performance tells you about the market."*
- **Sentiment probe.** Take a baseline sentiment measure (7- or 10-point scale) on interest for your product/problem/space. Show a video (your product · competitive product · ad · brief explainer). Measure sentiment again. Ask shifters *why*.
- **Usability test variation.** Include key competitors' websites in the flow; watch users navigate and ask what they'd do next. Or watch people search for your product / one like it — reveals the full customer journey, not just your product in isolation.

Lauchengco: *"I encourage people to be creative and get out of the building to really understand market fit. That's where a lot of big 'aha' insights happen that might otherwise go unnoticed."*

## Creative market tests (Ch 11 UC Berkeley examples)
Ch 11 shares three student-run examples from Lauchengco's UC Berkeley course (three market tests in a week, assess implications):

1. **Developer application-security tool** — in-person messaging test at a transit stop. The message with the most glances got zero walk-ups; the message with fewer glances got walk-ups asking questions. **Implication:** the more specific message was less interesting to most but more interesting to the right target.
2. **Wearable sleep-tracker wristband** — Walgreens rush-hour survey. Day 2 added an actor in a doctor's lab coat, silent, nodding behind the surveyor. **Implication:** implied medical endorsement noticeably shifts perception of value + interest.
3. **Tech-career bootcamps** — value-matching game with 5 dollar amounts across 5 competing brands, then compared to how respondents rated brand importance in decision-making. **Implication:** people think they're not affected by brand, but *"when forced to make choices compared to similar products, they are willing to pay more for the brand they know, even relative to what they feel that brand is worth in a different context."*

## Rediscovering market fit for existing products
Ch 11's four techniques for when market fit may have been lost:

- **Win/loss analysis.** *"It's not just who you're losing to (although that matters), it's the why."* Product? Process? Perception? Brand? PMM's cross-functional relationships make this uniquely their work to drive.
- **Sales-call shadowing.** In person or via recordings from sales-intelligence platforms. *"There is body language and intonation that can't be communicated in text and speaks volumes on sounding 'pretty good' versus a clear 'wow' but isn't said directly."* Critical for messaging + target-segmentation.
- **Intent data.** Access to customer data from account-based or predictive-marketing tools reveals what actions historically translate into sales. PMMs leverage for market segmentation, messaging, campaign development.
- **Social / customer sentiment.** Reviews, social postings, Glassdoor employee posts. Both positive and negative sentiment inform. *"Take these social signals for what they are — directional feedback that shows how people feel, not necessarily how things are. But know that belief equals truth for many people."*

## Timeboxing
Ch 11's guard against analysis paralysis: *"Perfection can quickly become the enemy of great."*

Timeboxing = *"allocating a fixed time period for a planned activity, then assessing whether or not you've reached a reasonable outcome at the end of it. It establishes a time boundary at the start of the process, not as a result of the process, and keeps you accountable to moving at the speed markets require."*

**A week to a month is usually enough** to create a starting point on customer-discovery work.

## Active Listening
Ch 11's discipline: *"There is one particular quality that distinguishes those who are great at product and market discovery work: active listening. They don't listen to respond or have their assumptions validated. There is open, attentive listening; their mission is to learn."*

Three practical mechanics:
- **Lead with open-ended questions** that reveal market perceptions *before* diving into specific product-discovery ideas. If you lead with your ideas, you've primed the response.
- **Put multiple different ideas in front of customers** so you can gauge relative strength of response.
- **Always use calibration** — ratings, rankings, or *"would you hit the like button?"*.

Lauchengco's closing: *"If you're a product manager or a product marketer thinking 'we need to do more to probe market fit,' you're right. Both roles don't tend to do enough and need the insights to do their jobs well."*

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Assuming initial beachhead = product-market fit.** Growth stalls because market fit was under-invested.
- **Confusing product desirability with market fit.** People say a lot of things they won't do.
- **Skipping value-discovery depth.** *"Would you buy this?"* and *"how much would you pay?"* don't capture urgency, competing priorities, or the status-quo alternative.
- **One-and-done PMF check.** Word for lawyers proves even mature products need rediscovery as they grow into new customers.
- **Listen to respond, not to learn.** Active listening is the distinguishing quality of great discovery work; without it, you hear confirmation of your assumptions.
- **Wait for perfect data.** Timebox the learning; iterate on directional signals.
- **Delegate discovery to a research team.** PMM (and PM) need the primary experience of talking to customers.

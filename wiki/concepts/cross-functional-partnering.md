# Cross-Functional Partnering

> The "product model in action" — how empowered product teams interact with everyone else in the company. Named in *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* Part VI ("The Product Model in Action"), one dedicated chapter per constituency: **Customers · Sales · Product Marketing · Finance · Stakeholders · Executives**. The transformation politics work ([[transformation-politics]]) is *about* getting to healthy partnerships; this page is *what healthy* looks like.

## Why it matters
Part VI answers the practical question the rest of the book raises: *if empowered product teams are no longer subservient to stakeholders, what is the new relationship?* Cagan is explicit that these chapters describe the **ideal case** — *"even in the very best companies, with the very best people and intentions, things won't always go as you might like"*. The wiki uses this concept as (a) the coaching target — what to aim for, (b) the counter to [[overcoming-objections|Part X objections]] — most objections dissolve when the partnership pattern is in place, and (c) the standard against which [[stakeholder-relationship-health]] and related diagnostics measure the current state.

## The six partnerships

### 1. Partnering with Customers (Ch 21)
The change most customers notice first — the product team, not just the salesperson, engages with users and customers directly.

- **Promises come only from the team that will deliver them.** *"The only people who can make a promise for a product deliverable are the product team that will be responsible for delivering on this promise. Not the company's executives, not sales, not marketing, not customer success, not program or delivery managers, and not product managers."* Promises are made only after enough discovery to know the solution will work — see [[high-integrity-commitments]].
- **Direct access is a prerequisite.** *"These proxies — whether from your company or the customer — are not sufficient. Direct access between the product team and actual users is essential, and a prerequisite to any commitments."*
- **Commercial product vs custom solution.** Where a customer's requested solution won't work for other customers, the team either finds a better solution that does, or (rarely) directs the customer to a custom development firm.
- **Delivery obligations.** Two tests: the new capability works as expected, and it doesn't introduce regressions. Modern design + deployment techniques smooth adoption cost. When mistakes happen: correct quickly, competently, analyze the recurrence.
- **The highest-order goal:** *"happy, referenceable customers"* — customers using your products every day for important work, loving them enough to share with others.

*"Customer"* here is broad — users, buyers, approvers, influencers, internal colleagues serving end customers.

### 2. Partnering with Sales (Ch 22)
Not universal — applies where the company has a direct or channel sales force. When it does apply, *"the relationship between the product organization and the sales organization can literally make or break the success of the company."*

- **The interdependence.** *"There are perhaps no two more interdependent roles in a company than product and sales."* Product depends on sales for reach; sales depends on product for solutions that meet customer needs.
- **The old dysfunction pattern.** Sales asks for features that don't solve the customer's real problem; product ships things that don't sell; both become unhappy. Salespeople live on commissions and are wired to respond directly to customer requests, so *"unless there's a genuine belief that the product team exists to support similar goals, the sales team believes they must battle for their very survival."*
- **The fix — trust built by shared time in front of customers.** Starts at the head-of-product / head-of-sales level, spreads to individual PMs and salespeople.
- **What each sees.** Salesperson sees the team discover a winning solution the customer didn't know was possible; the team sees the salesperson navigate the buyer/user/influencer/approver dynamics.
- **The load-bearing pattern: reference customers.** Working with prospective customers together, the team learns what is consistent across customers and what is not — enabling a *single* solution deployed successfully across many. *"When product has not provided these happy, referenceable customers, the sales job is dramatically more difficult, in addition to fueling sales's distrust of product. It shouldn't be a surprise that this situation leads directly to requests for custom ('special') solutions in order for sales to be able to sell anything."*
- **Sales gets a real partner.** *"In most legacy models, the sales organization is the sole customer-centric group that is outcome based… The product model gives sales a true partner that is also customer centered and outcome based."*

### 3. Partnering with Product Marketing (Ch 23)
The closest cross-functional partner for most product teams. See also the [[product-marketing]] competency page. Rare orgs pair a PMM to a single product team; more common that PMMs are shared across teams (because product marketing doesn't organize on the same team topology). Regardless, the PM reaches out to establish the relationships.

The **eight collaboration areas** the book names:
1. **Market Understanding and Competitive Analysis** — PMM has access to industry analysts, market insights, competitive research. Runs both ways: new competitors + technologies flow between team and PMM. Canonical joint work: evaluate a new competitive offering and put together a response for sales.
2. **Product Go-to-Market** — *"arguably the most important area of collaboration."* Coming up with an effective product is only half the problem; getting it into customers' hands is the other half. PMM is the resident expert on GTM alternatives. Established GTM, then feeds into every team so they understand its power and nuance.
3. **Key Product Decisions** — for important decisions in discovery, include the PMM's perspective. GTM is a first-class strategy input.
4. **Customer Discovery Program** — the specific technique from *[[inspired|INSPIRED]]* (called *Charter User Program* in the 1st ed, Ch 15) for creating new products or taking existing products to new markets. Identify, select, work with a set of prospective customers from the same target market together with product marketing and sales; discover + deliver a solution meeting their needs; the result is the initial set of reference customers. PM applies learnings to product; PMM applies learnings to GTM, sales tools, sales process. See [[reference-customer-program]] for the full mechanics + non-negotiables (no prepayment; target-market not early adopters; not a custom build; partners not customers-yet).
5. **Messaging and Positioning** — day-to-day changes have implications; PMM consulted for tactical and broad-implication changes.
6. **Customer Impact Assessment** — with continuous deployment, some changes have implications for customers, sales, or CS. Coordinate with PMM to prevent surprises; if PMM isn't prepared, hold the change or use feature-flagging to release-dark. (See [[2012-11-01-cagan-assessing-customer-impact]] for Cagan's original article on this technique.)
7. **Pricing and Packaging** — in most product-model companies, product teams *defer* to PMM (expertise + pricing-firm relationships + PMM typically operates at higher granularity than the team topology). Product provides input; other factors compute price.
8. **Sales Enablement** — PMM owns product collateral and sales tools; product teams contribute expertise. *"Experienced product managers learn that spending time explaining nuance to the product marketing manager is often a very high-leverage use of their time."*

Longer reference: [[loved|LOVED]] by [[martina-lauchengco|Martina Lauchengco]].

### 4. Partnering with Finance (Ch 24)
Critical partnership — *"there is simply no viable alternative to working together effectively."*

- **Start with the diagnostic conversation.** Head of product with head of finance: look together at the data — the current model isn't very predictive in funding decisions or in estimating incremental revenue from product. Compare past business-case claims to actual results — usually "painfully clear" the old model wastes money and time. *"The only real question is whether we can do better."*
- **Test the model.** Frame it as a test — conservative (a small number of teams) or aggressive depending on frustration + urgency levels.
- **Additional benefit to name.** If the transformation succeeds, the company may be valued by public markets *at least in part as a tech-powered company* — often an investor motivation.

**How product changes its side of the collaboration:**
- Stops hiding behind features and roadmaps and making up business cases.
- Signs up to be measured by business results — new product = business success; new capability = value and business impact.
- Admits what it doesn't know and what it can't know; runs tests to get data.
- Incremental resource requests come with data + transparent analysis; large asks preceded by small, cheap tests.
- Where a date is really needed → [[high-integrity-commitments]] (defensible, accountable).
- When innovation lands, share the evidence with finance and investor relations.

**What product asks of finance in return:**
- **Fund product teams, not projects** — focus areas + measures of business results per quarter+.
- **New competencies** — mostly coach and train existing employees; where replacement is needed, product leadership brings a proposal. Note: *moving to the product model usually saves money* even when insourcing critical competencies.
- **Judge on business impact, not shipping** — leave feature/project granularity to teams.
- **Minimize HICs** — nontrivial work just to produce a date; disruptive.

### 5. Partnering with Stakeholders (Ch 25)
Book definition: *"someone not explicitly a member of a product team, yet who represents a key constituency, area of the business, or special expertise."*

- **The big change stakeholders feel.** In the old model, tech resources were there to serve the stakeholder's needs. In the new model those resources serve customers directly. *"Yes, they are no longer subservient to the stakeholders, but they do still depend on them."*
- **Range of reactions.** Some stakeholders are frustrated enough to try anything new; some feel accountable to deliver without the resource-control they had; some take a wait-and-see approach. All *do* recognize the change is real.
- **The mechanic — trust between PM and stakeholder.** Empowered team solves problems for customers *in ways that work for the business*. Customer-love is usually solvable if the team has direct access; *"solving for the many, often-competing needs of the different parts of the business can be very challenging."*
- **Committees don't innovate.** *"Bringing all the relevant stakeholders together for every decision is not only an excessive use of time and money; we also know committees very rarely innovate."*
- **Product leadership owes stakeholders competent PMs.** *"Expecting stakeholders to trust a product manager who does not have this foundation is not only unrealistic, but also unwise."* Very few PMs know all the relevant business dimensions in depth.
- **The PM's commitment.** Engage directly with each relevant stakeholder; learn constraints; when a proposed solution might impact a stakeholder, *show the prototype* before building. Ideally the stakeholder needs only a few minutes to give a thumbs-up. If there's a problem, iterate on the prototype until it works. Sometimes finding a solution that works for customers *plus* multiple stakeholders takes many iterations — and *"the solution that solves for all parties may end up being something that none of the stakeholders had imagined was possible."*
- **The promise:** never build (and certainly never ship) a solution that doesn't meet critical business needs. If it happens, correct quickly + investigate recurrence.
- **Transparency.** Stakeholders welcome to participate in user testing, view prototypes, see product data (usage + live-data test results).

See also: [[stakeholder-relationship-health]] (the diagnostic).

### 6. Partnering with Executives (Ch 26)
The chapter organized around **six named elements** — the wiki names this pattern as its own framework page: [[partnering-with-executives|the six-element executive trust model]] (Decisions · Outcomes · Disagreements · Promises · Surprises · Trust).

- **What executives actually need.** *"'Please just back off and give the product teams space to do their work'… ignores the reality that executives have very real needs when it comes to running the company responsibly and effectively."*
- **Frequent, high-quality engagement.** In the product model, teams *need* frequent executive interaction — teams depend on strategic context, and executives depend on team information. It is the **nature**, not the frequency, of the interactions that changes.

## Cross-cutting patterns across Part VI

Reading all six chapters together reveals shared moves the wiki collects here:

- **Prototypes are the shared artifact of trust.** Ch 21 (customers testing prototypes) · Ch 25 (PM shows stakeholders prototypes before building) · Ch 26 (executive prototype preview → no post-build surprises). The prototype is Cagan's cross-cutting *evidence-of-thinking-that-invites-response* — much cheaper to iterate than a built product.
- **Direct access to users is a nonnegotiable.** Ch 21 explicit; Ch 22 (product-team access to customers) explicit; Ch 25 assumed.
- **Reference customers are the single most-cited answer.** Ch 21 (customers get reference-customer confidence) · Ch 22 (reference-customers-not-specials) · Ch 23 (customer discovery program → reference customers) · Ch 24 (in the funding conversation).
- **Business cases → data + tests.** Ch 22 · Ch 24 · Ch 26. The move is from *"predict returns"* to *"run a test to learn."*
- **Product leadership owes the trio to the org.** Ch 25 (stakeholders should get competent PMs); Ch 26 (executives should get product leaders who can connect the dots).
- **Trust is bidirectional.** Ch 22 · Ch 25 · Ch 26. Product-side trust to build (with head-of-sales, stakeholders, executives); executive-side trust to extend (empowerment).

## Anti-patterns
- **Backing off entirely.** "Empowerment = executives back off" is Cagan's explicit anti-pattern in Ch 26. Empowered teams need *more* strategic context, not less.
- **Design-by-committee.** Ch 25 warns against bringing all stakeholders together for every decision. Reflected on [[stakeholder-relationship-health]].
- **Sales collects requirements → product builds them.** Ch 22 explicit — *"not part of the product model."*
- **Product delegates customer contact to a proxy** (customer success manager, account manager, salesperson). Ch 21 explicit — proxies "are not sufficient."
- **Business-case funding cycle.** Ch 24 explicit — invites over-promising then excuse-making. Replace with fund-teams + measure-outcomes.
- **PMM as afterthought.** Ch 23 (implicit) — the eight collaboration areas signal that pairing/relationship is load-bearing, not optional.
- **HICs as the default.** Ch 21, Ch 26 explicit — HICs are the exception path; making them the norm corrupts empowerment and burns capacity.

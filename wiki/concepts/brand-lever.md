# The Brand Lever

> **Brand isn't a logo — it's a *promise between a customer and a company* delivered consistently across every touchpoint.** [[loved|LOVED]] Ch 16's core reframe. *"Brand isn't done well by most tech companies because it is largely misunderstood."*

## Why it matters
Ch 16 opens with **Netflix's trial-notification decision.** Netflix's homepage-conversion team noticed that notifying customers *before* their card was charged at trial-end led more people to cancel (who might have otherwise just forgotten). The team had to choose: proactive notice that maintains brand vs tens of millions in extra revenue each month.

*"They decided Netflix's brand reputation was the right choice for future growth. And they were right; that decision was tens of millions of global subscribers ago."*

Lauchengco's reframe: *"Brand isn't done well by most tech companies because it is largely misunderstood. It is presumed to be a company's name, logo, colors, design, and tone of voice — all things Netflix also does very well. Brand includes all this, but it is more about a consistent experience across every aspect of how a company acts — it's a promise between a customer and a company."*

## Ch 16's second worked example — WebFilings/Workiva
When **WebFilings** (2008 startup) launched, *"no one would call their logo, brand colors, or company name particularly inspired."* But customers loved them. Reason:
- **First-name access to a dedicated customer success manager** with weekly check-ins.
- **Suggestions on how to use the product better.**
- **Made customers feel they couldn't fail.**

Result: *"unmatched loyalty for the brand propelling them to category leadership."* They rebranded to Workiva and IPO'd.

**Coaching payoff:** *"They had a great product, of course, but the loyalty people had to the brand came from an early and deep investment in customer success."*

## What brand actually is in tech
Ch 16's operational definition:
- **For most people, the brand of a tech company is driven primarily by their product experience.**
- **But a customer's brand experience includes:**
  - Product support · sales process · pricing
  - Website · social media · advertising
  - *"Even something as perfunctory as notifying of a charge about to hit an account"* (Netflix)

**In crowded markets, brand experience can make a product feel different.** *"It's a way to shift the playing field beyond features and punch above your weight for earlier stage companies. Attention to brand signals maturity."*

**Products change; brand promises don't.** *"When a company invests well in its corporate brand, customer loyalty lives across products or despite them. That's the end goal of brand for growing, multiproduct companies: long-term loyalty."*

## Where PMM's brand work sits
Ch 16 pins down the boundary: *"Much of brand management and execution is beyond the domain of product marketing."* But four situations put brand at the *product* level — PMM's turf:

1. **Product or feature naming.**
2. **Moving toward a multiproduct suite.**
3. **Moving to a line-of-business brand.**
4. **Diagnosing when there is a company brand issue, not a product one.**

## The three brand levels (Ch 16)
Any multi-product tech company has three levels to manage:

- **Company** (e.g. Apple).
- **Line of business or key brand** (e.g. Music · TV · Watch · iPhone · Mac).
- **Products** (e.g. MacBook Air · MacBook Pro · iMac).

*"These different levels exist to simplify how customers understand a company. It creates a categorization system for customers, but it does not mean each level gets marketed."*

- **Apple** — markets at the **product level** (Apple TV+, newest MacBook or iPhone). But the website organizes by line of business.
- **Microsoft** — markets at the **line of business / suite level** (Office · Xbox · Azure).

*"Larger company brand strategy often determines how an individual product gets marketed."* Lauchengco's Word example: when she was on the Office team, Word's marketing was reduced to a small number of features that supported marketing Office as an integrated productivity suite.

## Four brand situations PMM faces (Ch 16)
The four common PMM-facing brand situations Lauchengco walks through:

### 1. Product scope expands; market perception is narrow
**Pocket** (originally Read It Later) — as they added videos, images, shopping links, the "Read It Later" name no longer encompassed the product. **Pocket** worked for all of it and for **Pocket Hits** (their weekly email, seen by more people each week than used their mobile app). The rebrand worked so well it became the company's name.

### 2. Leverage existing product-brand loyalty to acquire new audiences
- **Intuit** — QuickBooks + TurboTax are better known than the company brand. Product brands are used as shortcuts: QuickBooks Payroll, QuickBooks Time.
- **Atlassian** — Jira Software is dominant among developers. Atlassian uses the Jira brand in Jira Service Management and Jira Align to lend Jira's developer credibility even to non-developer audiences.

*"Naming decisions at this level are often driven by product marketing. It's crucial to pay attention to where brand loyalty lives and use it with intention."*

### 3. Improve ability to penetrate a market with a new brand
**Microsoft → Xbox.** Microsoft is firmly associated with enterprise/productivity — *not* edgy gaming. Xbox was created as the brand for the gamer audience. *"Sometimes, companies need to create a different line of business or key brand because the existing brand has too much baggage that doesn't connect with a target market."*

Another version: **unifying a group of integrated or connected products under a product suite name** — especially when multiple products target the same audience.

### 4. Product naming is brand strategy
**Salesforce Marketing Cloud** — Email Studio, Audience Studio, Mobile Studio, Social Studio, Data Studio. *"Even if you don't know the products, you do know if Studio is in the name, it is part of the Marketing Cloud."* Consistent prefixes/suffixes connect products in a broader family.

*"Product marketers drive this naming thinking because they are expected to have visibility into product roadmaps. Naming taxonomies affect future product naming. They also play a vital role in identifying if a bigger aspect of brand strategy affects product go-to-market."*

## Sometimes there is a bigger battlefront — the Netscape cautionary tale
Ch 16 closes with a Lauchengco personal case: at **Netscape Communications**, everyone called their dominant browser *"Netscape"* even though the product was Netscape Navigator. As long as the browser was winning, the company brand and the browser brand were fused.

When **Microsoft's Internet Explorer attack** hit, the perception became *"as goes the browser, so goes Netscape."* *"It didn't matter that our growth businesses were going strong or that we were innovating new Internet protocols and services."*

*"We had a company brand problem, but we kept fighting it with product wars. It was the product marketing team going through a product naming exercise that flagged the bigger company brand issue."* Eventually Netscape was acquired by AOL — largely because Netscape's brand was associated with a market segment AOL couldn't capture.

**Lauchengco's lesson:** *"It seared into me the importance of constant examination of the relationship between products and their larger company brands."*

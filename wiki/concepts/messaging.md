# Messaging

> *"The key things you say to reinforce your positioning, making you credible so people want to learn more."* [[loved|LOVED]] Chs 22 + 23. **Messaging is short game; [[positioning]] is long game.** Both grounded in what customers *need to hear*, not what the team wants to say.

## Why it matters
Ch 22 opens with Lauchengco's workshop exercise: pick from a list of messaging examples the one that best connects with you *and* the one that makes you most curious to learn more. For over a decade, the overwhelming winner on both:

> *"Expense reports that don't suck! Hassle-free expense reporting built for employees and loved by admins."* — early Expensify

Why it wins (Ch 22):
1. **It feels emotional and true.** *"Expense reports do suck!"* Willing to say how we feel, not just talk about the product.
2. **It efficiently communicates benefits in simple and compelling ways.** *"Hassle-free"* doesn't say *"automation"* or *"efficiency"* — it conveys ease.
3. **It covers multiple interested audiences.** In four words — *"and loved by admins"* — it conveys a sophisticated understanding that expense reports are also a lot of work for admins.

*"Every word in their messaging means something concrete and packs a thoughtful and emotional punch. It was so successful in its day, it had the ultimate compliment: lots of copycats, like Zoom that went with 'Video conferencing that doesn't suck.'"*

## Bad messaging — the anti-example
Ch 22's contrast: *"[Our product] improves the efficiency and effectiveness of field service organizations and mobile workforces. Meaningful, measurable value that optimizes your business, improves efficiency, and delights customers."*

*"How many products could you substitute into this statement? Hundreds. Do you have any ideas on what position they're trying to own? I don't."*

**The load-bearing test:** *"Good messaging doesn't just say what a product does or its hoped-for benefits. It conveys a deeper understanding of the people the product is built for, which requires great listening skills. You're looking for insights that make customers feel known."*

## Listen and Learn — Ch 22's discovery
Ch 22's discipline: the best way to know what customers need to hear is direct conversation. **Open-ended questions Ch 22 recommends:**

1. Tell me what your average day looks like.
2. What really frustrates you in how you do your job? What keeps you up at night?
3. What is the straw that broke the camel's back? What made you seek a new way of solving the problem?
4. If you had a magic wand to create something that could do anything, what would it do?
5. What's the last problem at work you spent money to fix?

*"Listen for insights and language that makes them feel known, like you really get how they're experiencing a problem."*

## Choose credibility and clarity — Expensify vs SAP Concur (Ch 22 Table 22.1)

**Expensify (recent):**
> *"Sit back and let Expensify do the work. Whether you're a road warrior with pockets full of receipts or a busy accountant buried in paperwork, Expensify automates the entire receipt and expense management process. · One-click receipt scanning · Next-day reimbursement · Automatic approval workflows · Automatic accounting sync"*

**SAP Concur (recent):**
> *"Better travel and expense management. Always. Helping businesses with their top travel and expense challenges. Concur Expense. Submit and approve expenses from anywhere. Concur Travel. Capture travel no matter where it is booked. Concur Invoice. Automate and integrate your AP process."*

Lauchengco's X-ray (Ch 22):

- **Concur** — *"They conclude they're better but don't say why. They also treat the employee and admin as experiencing the same benefits… The list is led by individual product names, not the customer's point-of-view."*
- **Expensify** — *"'Sit back and let Expensify do the work' sets up the idea that the product is going to do our work for us."* The road-warrior + accountant framing lands via *"specific details that capture real peoples' frustration."* *"One-click"* and *"next-day"* are *"notably better than the five-clicks and 30-day norm many older systems require."*

**Small language details matter:** *"'accounting sync' versus 'automate and integrate your AP process.' They mean the same thing, but the former is more efficient and modern, the other is precisely correct but feels tired."*

*"The workforce is now more than 50% millennial with Gen Z right behind. Messaging needs to match the temperament of the generation it's trying to reach. Whenever possible use evocative, energizing language that also helps you connect with your target market."*

## CAST — the messaging gut check
Ch 22's practical guide (introduced Ch 6, deepened Ch 22). **Grade yourself on each; push for better:**

| Letter | Question |
|---|---|
| **C**lear | Is what you do clear and is there a reason to be curious? Is being comprehensive getting in the way of clarity? |
| **A**uthentic | Is the language evocative and meaningful to your customer? Is it said in a way that makes them feel known? |
| **S**imple | Is it easy to understand what's compelling or different? Will customers know what's better? |
| **T**ested | Was it tested and iterated in the context customers will experience it? |

*"It's worth mentioning again how important testing in the context in which a customer will experience it is. It immediately makes flabby language easier to spot. You also get a sense of what you can use in design or visuals to emphasize points, so not everything has to go into product messaging."*

## Netflix — messaging that tracked the business (Ch 23)
Ch 23 walks through **Netflix's messaging evolution** as a masterclass in matching message to stage:

### 2009 — DVD days
Homepage messaging:
- *"Rent as many movies as you want! For only $8.99 a month."*
- *"Classics to New Releases to TV episodes."*
- *"Free DVD shipping — both ways."*
- **Third bullet was not product-benefit but trust:** *"Cancel anytime."* For a newish Internet company, mitigating perceived risk. Plus a highly visible 24/7 support number.

Visual: *"the promise of snuggling up at home with the family for a fun movie night."* Emotional punch.

### 2014 — streaming era
- *"Watch TV shows & movies…"* (TV leads because it drives "bingier" behavior.)
- *"…anytime, anywhere"* (advantage over DVDs).
- *"Only $7.99 a month"* (price up front — same clarity as DVD era).
- *"Start Your Free Month"* — clear CTA.

### 2016 — brand-leads era
- *"See what's next."* Reason to be curious + differentiation from growing competition.
- *"Watch Anywhere."*
- *"Cancel Anytime."* — trust-building consistency across 7+ years.
- *"Join"* replaces *"Start Your Free Trial"* — *"You belong to a tribe."*

Lauchengco's takeaway: *"Each of these word choices might seem small, but in aggregate, they pack enormous punch. It positioned what was different and their overall value in remarkably few words. It took the better part of two decades to get to the place where their messaging could coast off their brand. It's the technology adoption curve at work."*

## Zendesk — anticipating what customers want to know (Ch 23)
Ch 23's second worked example.

### 2010–2011 — early
Home page anticipated the *"What is Zendesk?"* question with the answer directly: *"Web-based help desk software with an elegant support ticket system and a self-service customer support platform."*

**But that wasn't the header.** The header was *"Customer Support Made Easy."* — what best positioned them.

Ch 23's observation: *"Explaining what they did was still necessary three years after starting and 10,000 customers in. This is how long it took the industry to know what Zendesk did. It's yet another reminder of just how long it takes even successful businesses to move through the technology adoption curve."*

**Ch 23 notes** what Zendesk *didn't* do: *"Notice the absence of words like modern or revolutionary, even though they were all these things. Instead, they kept language more authentic to what their audience wanted to hear. That made their differences clear."*

The homepage also worked hard on credibility:
- *"10,000+ companies use Zendesk"* (validation).
- Customer Support activity map (global reach).
- Product visuals in the hero image (multi-platform).
- *"No credit card needed"* (easy).

### Pre-IPO 2014 — customer outcomes lead
- *"Customer satisfaction has never been easier"* — reason to be curious.
- Product positioning simpler: *"Beautifully simple customer service software."*
- ROI calculator instead of "we have good ROI" claim.
- 30,000+ customers social proof.

### Post-IPO
- *"Beautifully simple… software for better customer service."*
- **Pricing up front** — no clicks needed. Reduces friction to a trial.

Ch 23's coaching payoff: *"In this era, if you compared Zendesk's website analytics — average page views, time on site per visitor, etc. — to any other major B2B SaaS company, they outperformed them all. They took the website's role in the customer journey very seriously."*

## Accuracy for the engineering-trained (Ch 21 addendum)
Ch 21 addresses the technical-precision impulse: *"For people who come from engineering backgrounds, the need for precision is real. It's what makes statements feel believable."*

Lauchengco's guardrail: *"Talking about something in a way that simplifies — and leaves some details out — is not being inaccurate. It's guiding toward shared understanding. When gravity is introduced as a concept, most start with Newton's story of an apple falling from a tree before introducing the equation."*

*"Messaging's job is to connect first before diving into all the details. It's hard to process depth without some table-setting context. If everything said about a product is true, it is still accurate. It might not be the precise way you would talk about it with someone with deep knowledge and full context, but that is the precise difference between the job of messaging versus that of product details."*

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Feature-list messaging.** *"Improves the efficiency and effectiveness of…"* — hundreds of products could substitute in.
- **Formulaic messaging** (see [[positioning]] and [[pmm-storyteller]] for the positioning-statement-generator trap).
- **Skipping the emotional register.** *"Expense reports don't suck"* works because it says how we *feel*.
- **Ignoring generational language shifts.** Millennial + Gen Z workforce → evocative + energetic language.
- **"Precise" over "clear."** Engineering-trained impulse. Messaging connects first; details follow.
- **Announcing new-category words when the market doesn't have the mental map yet** (see [[positioning]] Loudcloud).
- **Trendy buzzwords over concrete verbs** (see [[pmm-storyteller]] Looker vs *"collaborative data platform"* rewrite).
- **Testing only in documents.** CAST's *T* — test in the context customers will experience it.

# Technology Adoption Life Cycle

> How new technology products flow through distinct **adopter groups** — innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards. [[loved|LOVED]] Ch 15's key claim: *"one of the biggest misses I consistently see in how products, especially for startups, go to market."*

## Why it matters
Ch 15 opens with an **online backup company** whose original target customers were *"Anyone whose money was green."* Radio host endorsements worked to capture customers — but paid acquisition kept getting more expensive. A deeper look at the customer base was shocking: it skewed **senior-citizen old**. In retrospect, obvious given the radio-heavy channel mix.

The problem: those customers *"were easy to retain but did nothing to help the business grow. There was no word of mouth — people didn't tend to ask their parents for online backup advice — and they weren't the sort that wrote reviews."*

The company overhauled its GTM to focus on channels that brought customers who *talked about* products with others. The real target turned out to be *"people in their early 30s who listened to NPR and felt they had more to lose — family photos or an archive of work."*

Lauchengco's takeaway: *"It was an expensive and time-consuming lesson in the importance of paying attention to the technology adoption curve."*

## The curve — Ch 15's basics
The bell curve moves through five adopter segments:
- **Innovators** — first customers; often adopt for the technology itself.
- **Early adopters** — the "aha-moment" customers; disproportionate influence on subsequent adopters.
- **Early majority** — the fat middle-left of the curve; adopt once evidence + credibility have accumulated.
- **Late majority** — the fat middle-right; adopt once the product is mainstream.
- **Laggards** — slow to adopt any new technology.

**Adoption cycles typically take 7–10+ years** to move through. *"People can only adopt technology so fast. Marketing awareness and activities must build and layer over time."*

## The failure modes Ch 15 names
- **Believing they're moving through segments faster than they really are.**
- **Not realizing initial market segments don't set them up for the next stage of growth.** (The online-backup senior-citizen trap.)

**Movement isn't evenly distributed.** *"A product can spend years at the earlier stages of the adoption curve and move more rapidly through the 'majority' stage — as often happens for enterprise companies — or vice versa, as is the case for many 'hot' consumer tech companies."*

## Segments are more than demographics
Ch 15's important nuance: customer segments now include multiple dimensions:
- **Demographics** — age, gender, location.
- **Psychographics** — attitudes, values, lifestyle.
- **Verticals** — industries, use cases.
- **Technographics** — technologies already in use.
- **Firmographics** — size, geography, industry.
- **Behaviors** — likelihood to buy.
- **Intent** — competitive-site visits, content reads.
- **Product usage** — features most used by renewers.
- **Browsing history.**
- **Account-based actions** — inside a company.

## PMM's role — bridging the PM/marketing gap
Ch 15's insight for coaching a PMM or PM through this: **PMs and PMMs think about customer segments differently.**

- **PMs** are *"always looking for the next customers with new needs to solve."* They move quickly through customer definitions using **jobs-to-be-done** or **problems-to-solve** — not "addressable customers in market who are likely to buy."
- **PMMs** bridge this gap. As **ambassador to product**, adoption-curve thinking informs product decisions. As **ambassador to marketing**, PMMs prioritize customer segments so marketing focuses on the *right* customers. As **storyteller + evangelist**, PMMs shape categories so a product is seen as valuable even when ahead of the market.

*"After teams identify which customer segments are most meaningful in shaping market adoption, the plan on how to target them through marketing strategies and actions is what goes into a product's go-to-market plan."*

## Startup-specific application
Ch 15 uses **Superhuman** (Rahul Vohra) as the exemplar for defining early adopters with intent:
- Defined the ideal early customer as *"the one[s] who would enjoy the product for its greatest benefit and help spread the word."*
- Extremely clear positioning: *"the fastest email experience ever made."*
- **Refer-a-friend to jump the beta line** (tens of thousands long).
- **Qualification before beta acceptance** — you couldn't be a user if they didn't think you'd experience benefits.
- **Monetization at start of onboarding, no free trial.**
- **Required onboarding call** to ensure success.

*"They said 'sorry, not yet' to any early customer they weren't sure would help them succeed."*

The general lesson: **know your ideal early adopters precisely, and be willing to say no** to customers who won't help you succeed.

## The over-generalized segment trap
Ch 15's most-quoted cautionary framing: *"Watch the tendency to overgeneralize customer segments when doing product go-to-market planning. One I see all the time: SMB, mid-market, enterprise. When it comes to designing a go-to-market, a mid-market company is not the customer. A 'mid-level supply-chain manager who is willing to adopt new technology and directs purchases across at least 50 vendors' could be. Likewise, for consumer-facing companies, a marketable customer segment is not 'everyone who uses the Internet'; it's 'a word-lover who is curious about new words and language.'"*

## Same decision-maker, different GTM
Ch 15's cybersecurity example: **Palo Alto Networks vs. a startup**, both selling to CISOs.
- **Palo Alto Networks** — leverages brand awareness, existing products, CISO programs, and a large sales team.
- **A startup** — focuses on who experiences the daily pain the product solves. GTM activities focus on activating users to *influence* the CISO.

Same end decision-maker; different companies have different strengths; different specific segments + marketing activities.

## The iPhone worked example (Ch 15)
Lauchengco walks the iPhone through the curve — a decade+ of considered GTM action:

- **Innovators** — *"My Apple fanatic friend Mike"* stood in line day 1. **$599** price kept the beachhead exclusive.
- **Early adopters** — months later, price drops to **$499** with the OS 2.0 upgrade. *"With Mike's endorsement, I felt comfortable getting one."*
- **Early majority** — year 1, the **iPhone 3G** + AT&T multi-year exclusive deal → **$199**. **App Store launches** — 10M downloads first weekend. Lauchengco's *"siblings in finance, the military, and commercial production"* joined. Year 2, **iPhone 4 on Verizon** with Retina Display + front-facing camera + FaceTime → her mother-in-law joined.
- **Late majority + laggards** — iPhone 5/6/7 timeframe, ~year 7. Lauchengco's mother — *"a German immigrant and the most resistant technology adopter I know"* — joined via a foisted Mother's Day gift, adopting for the camera and FaceTime.

**The strategic takeaway:** *"Even if Apple's long-term goal was adoption by people like my mother, they didn't start there. They started with early adopters like Mike. Getting to my mom took the better part of a decade and a steady evolution in product, distribution, pricing, advertising, and promotions."*

By year 10, the **iPhone X** (killing the home button) + the Apple Watch signaled Apple was on to its next adoption curve.

**The generalizable question:** *"If the iPhone is one of the most successful products of all time — with a marketing budget to match — think about how long it will take your product to get through its adoption life cycle."*

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Believing you're further along the curve than you are** — the #1 mistake.
- **Assuming initial customer segments set you up for the next stage.** They usually don't — the online-backup case.
- **Overgeneralized segmentation.** "SMB / mid-market / enterprise" is not a target; a specific role with specific behaviors is.
- **Ignoring word-of-mouth capacity.** Early customers who won't tell others cost you the next stage.
- **Trying to skip stages.** Product/distribution/pricing must evolve step-by-step; leapfrogging fails.
- **Treating the same decision-maker as the same GTM.** Palo Alto vs startup selling to CISOs — very different playbooks.

# Product Strategy

> **How you plan to accomplish the [[product-vision|vision]] while meeting business needs** — and above all, **deciding which problems are most important to solve.**

## Why it matters
Strategy is the bridge from vision to teams. "Product strategy is all about which problems are the most important to solve" (*TRANSFORMED* Ch. 16); its **output is a set of business or customer problems to solve ([[team-objectives|team objectives]])** (Ch. 13). Get it wrong and empowered teams pour effort into the wrong problems.

## How it works
There is **one** product strategy — Cagan prefers the term **holistic** (it must look across all related teams) — and defining it is the ultimate responsibility of [[product-leadership|product leaders]]. The four [[principles-of-product-strategy|strategy principles]] govern how it's made: [[focus]], [[powered-by-insights]], [[transparency]], [[placing-bets]].

From Cagan's Q&A with Huryn:

- **Two facets:** (1) choosing the most important problems (the core); (2) [[placing-bets|placing bets]] — playing the odds because solving isn't guaranteed.
- **Value proposition is orthogonal** to strategy — it applies to vision, strategy, *and especially discovery*; you test it at every level.
- **Business strategy is the input** to product strategy (problems to solve are the output); insights from [[product-discovery|discovery]] commonly drive the *next iteration* of strategy (top-down + bottom-up).

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- Strategy as a **prioritized feature list** rather than a choice of problems.
- Many competing "strategies" instead of one holistic one.
- Set once in an offsite and never revisited — strategy is *discovered* and iterated as insights accumulate.

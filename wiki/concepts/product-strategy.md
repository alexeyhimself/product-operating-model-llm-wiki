# Product Strategy

> **How you plan to accomplish the [[product-vision|vision]] while meeting business needs** — and above all, **deciding which problems are most important to solve.**

## Why it matters
Strategy is the bridge from vision to teams. "Product strategy is all about which problems are the most important to solve" (*TRANSFORMED* Ch. 16); its **output is a set of business or customer problems to solve ([[team-objectives|team objectives]])** (Ch. 13). Get it wrong and empowered teams pour effort into the wrong problems.

## How it works
There is **one** product strategy — Cagan prefers the term **holistic** (it must look across all related teams) — and defining it is the ultimate responsibility of [[product-leadership|product leaders]]. The four [[principles-of-product-strategy|strategy principles]] govern how it's made: [[focus]], [[powered-by-insights]], [[transparency]], [[placing-bets]].

Cagan's foundational essay [[2020-02-17-cagan-product-strategy-overview|Product Strategy – Overview]] decomposes the work into four things, which became the four principles:

1. **Focus** — willingness to make tough choices on what's really important (and therefore everything you won't do).
2. **Insights** — generating and identifying insights from study and thought, across data, customers, enabling technology, the industry.
3. **Action** — converting insights into [[team-objectives|team objectives]] for specific teams.
4. **Management** — active stewardship without micromanagement; product strategy is never static.

From Cagan's Q&A with Huryn:

- **Two facets:** (1) choosing the most important problems (the core); (2) [[placing-bets|placing bets]] — playing the odds because solving isn't guaranteed.
- **Value proposition is orthogonal** to strategy — it applies to vision, strategy, *and especially discovery*; you test it at every level.
- **Business strategy is the input** to product strategy (problems to solve are the output); insights from [[product-discovery|discovery]] commonly drive the *next iteration* of strategy (top-down + bottom-up).

> "Product strategy is distinct from both business strategy and go-to-market strategy. Many companies have strong skills in business strategy and/or go-to-market strategy, but product strategy is often missing completely." ([[2022-09-23-moore-changing-how-you-decide-which-problems-to-solve|Moore, "Changing How You Decide Which Problems to Solve"]])

### The four elements, focus, and OKRs (the "Missing Link" talk)
The spoken origin of this framework is [[2020-02-22-cagan-product-strategy-the-missing-link|"Product Strategy: The Missing Link"]] — strategy is the missing middle between a big objective and "a roadmap of 100 features" (the "underpants gnomes" gap). Two sharp points:
- **[[focus|Focus]] ≠ prioritization.** The Pandora anti-pattern — giving stakeholders "funny money" to buy features — produced 60+ "must-dos," an IPO with only 40 engineers, and a salvage sale to SiriusXM. Focus means the *few* things that can actually move the needle, not a politically balanced buffet.
- **OKRs are downstream, not a substitute.** Cagan "does not recommend OKRs anymore" unless you're *already* an empowered-team org — OKRs came *from* that model (Intel → Google); bolting them onto feature teams yields "**OKR theater**." Three requisites: empowered teams; team-level objectives only (not per-manager, not individual); and active leaders where **objectives come from leaders, key results from teams**. Strategic context is the empowered-team analogue of military "command intent." The two strategy books Cagan endorses (most are "paint-by-numbers"): *[[good-strategy-bad-strategy]]* (Rumelt) and *[[the-art-of-action]]* (Bungay).

### Strategy in depth: flight levels, levers, and bets (the "Hardest Challenges" interview)
[[2024-03-28-cagan-hardest-challenges-product-strategy-product-direction|"The Hardest Challenges of Product Strategy"]] adds operational depth:
- **Why most companies have no strategy — the same reason they don't do [[product-discovery|discovery]].** In the project/stakeholder model, top-level allocation plus stakeholder roadmaps replace strategy; the nickname is the **"peanut-butter" strategy** — spreading engineers thinly across everything, working hard quarter after quarter yet rarely moving the needle.
- **Three flight levels.** *Business strategy* (CEO/C-suite: sell internationally? which sales channel?) is an **input / strategic context**; *product strategy* makes it real by choosing the few important problems; *[[product-discovery|discovery]]* solves them. Keep them distinct — and don't frame the bets as "business cases."
- **Two elements of the work.** (1) Senior leaders pick the most important **outcomes** (e.g. growth, churn) — product defers to them here; (2) product leaders find the **levers/insights** (from data, customers, enabling tech) and **[[placing-bets|place bets]]** on the problems most likely to move those levers.
- **Assign the same problem to multiple teams** — an underused technique. Not a competition (different people, data, technology) but a way to raise the odds that at least one team cracks a hard problem; in the ideal case you get the cumulative win.
- **Set aggressiveness explicitly.** When you hand a team a problem, state the **confidence level** you want — ~50% (a real stretch / moonshot) vs ~90% (near-certain / roofshot).
- **Strategy outranks discovery in importance.** "Even if you're terrible at discovery, as long as the money doesn't run out you might get there; if the strategy is bad, it almost never works out."

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- Strategy as a **prioritized feature list** rather than a choice of problems.
- **Peanut-butter allocation** — spreading engineers thinly across every stakeholder's asks; lots of work, no needle-movement ([[2024-03-28-cagan-hardest-challenges-product-strategy-product-direction|canon]]).
- Many competing "strategies" instead of one holistic one.
- Set once in an offsite and never revisited — strategy is *discovered* and iterated as insights accumulate.
- **Avoidance of the hard choice.** Rumelt (quoted by Cagan): "Bad strategy is the active avoidance of the hard work of crafting a good strategy. One common reason for choosing avoidance is the pain or difficulty of choice."
- **A goal + a roadmap, no strategy.** Many orgs have a goal (double revenue) and a roadmap (the tactics) but no strategy connecting them.
- **50-objective wall of initiatives** — every team committed to >100% of its capacity to a dozen "priorities." No real focus, no real progress on the levers.

# Product Marketing by Stage

> Product marketing's **core responsibilities stay stable across company stages** — the range, focus, and complexity of how they're applied changes. [[loved|LOVED]] Chs 29 + 30. **The wrong expectation for stage is a common source of PMM disappointment.**

## Why it matters
Ch 29 opens with **Michelle Denogean's Roadster hiring surprise.** A veteran automotive CMO commuting from LA to San Francisco for a startup that landed the country's largest car dealerships. The product team kept passing on senior PMM candidates citing *"not deep enough on product."*

By the third rejection, Michelle pushed the product team on *why*. She learned they *"was looking for something totally different: a person to help Roadster's customers use the product more in their day-to-day work."*

*"It was a complete surprise to Michelle, who felt such work was more suited to either a product manager focused on product engagement or customer success manager, not a product marketer."*

Lauchengco's takeaway: *"This misalignment in expectations is unfortunately not rare. Because the scope of product marketing isn't well understood, each team assumes it is the salvation for whatever gaps they feel most."*

The Roadster case: product team wanted a gap around product adoption filled; marketing team wanted a gap in "better leverage product in GTM" filled. Neither was what a PMM actually does.

## Early Stage — Ignition
Ch 29's framing: *"A time of massive learning. It's when go-to-market fit gets discovered and frequent iterations are the norm. You're trying to find what ignites growth."*

**What PMM does here:**
- **Learn what consistently converts people into valuable customers.** A lot of testing of theories; some can only be answered with time (like best customers to retain).
- **Everything feels urgent and important.** *"Early go-to-market feels like hand-to-hand combat."*
- **PMM at the forefront of interpreting market signal + translating into GTM actions.**
- **Frequent, regular back-and-forth** between anyone talking with customers — design, product, sales, CS, customers themselves.
- **B2B specific:** the sales playbook is taking shape; test if you have repeatable steps.
- **Messaging refinement** based on marketing activities that get strong responses.
- **Evangelism discovery:** who are the best influencers, what communities spread the word.

**Key stance:** *"lead with a point of view as much as what's in your product. The world must understand why your approach matters, why now, and why it's better than what the world already does. The ability to craft story using elements beyond just technology and product is crucial to get to the next stage."*

**Skill level required:** *"This diverse range of skills requires a product marketer with more experience, typically at the director level. Once the product marketing foundation is in place, additional marketing resources can be added to amplify messages and drive campaigns."*

**Graduating out of early stage:** GTM feels repeatable for **2–3 quarters** and the company has more confidence in the customer segments where it succeeds.

## Growth Stage — Rapid Rise
Ch 29's framing: *"This is the fuel-on-a-fire stage because you have a baseline of what works. Success feels (mostly) repeatable. The challenge becomes, what got you here isn't necessarily what will keep you growing."*

**What PMM does here:**
- **Enable business strategy**, especially newer growth opportunities.
- **Competitive forces drive a lot** — bigger companies add functionality that competes; upstarts do something different or cheaper.
- **Own a category position** — elevated story + steady cadence of activity; expand people's notions of your category and your product's position in it.
- **Actively cultivate evangelism drivers** — influencers, analysts, pundits, raving fans — and activate them with what they need to tell your story.
- **Product also gets more expansive** — new functionality + new products. PMM ensures additions matter to the market; the *why* for everything integrates into a bigger story.
- **Warning:** stories can get too complex or too far ahead of what the market can bear. PMM brings market realities back to product + spearheads how to package messages and releases.
- **New GTM models introduced** (direct sales → PLG or vice versa) — PMM's scope shifts.
- **Process in place to keep product + GTM connected.**

**Growth is of outsized importance at this stage.**

**Graduating out of growth stage:** *"the business has a pattern of many quarters in a row of strong, continued growth and revenue matching expectations of a company of your stage/size."*

## Mature Stage — Peak Burn
Ch 29's framing: *"The line between growth and mature companies is fuzzy. It can be revenue, size of company, or sometimes simply how long a company has been around. The challenge of this stage is to maintain sustained growth."*

Lauchengco's warning: *"Ironically, a mature technology company can have immature product marketing. This is evident if everyone is doing their own variation on a product marketing theme."*

**PMM's impact is amplified at maturity** — game-changing when done well, debilitating when it isn't.

### Ch 29's mature-company competitive-response case
Sales-marketing-product meeting on the biggest competitor. Sales identified urgent issues:
- Competitor exec relationships with key stakeholders were established before sales was in an account.
- Competitor invited a product bake-off on a part of the product where they'd win.
- Competitor's product approach *felt* newer → credibility.
- The mature company's people had preconceived ideas of what the product could + couldn't do.

**PMM's coordinated response:**
- **Sales qualification improvement** — PMM worked with sales on customer segments + qualifying criteria.
- **Positioning frame before features** — PMM created a stronger story that positioned the product; controlled the conversation of why the approach is different + better.
- **Advantages weren't memorable** — PMM created more customer success stories + data supporting the benefits; presented in more memorable graphics + collateral.
- **Competitive repositioning tools** — PMM gave sales competitive battle cards with *"kill points"* on how to respond and win. *"Let sales sound smart at a moment's notice."* Worked with product to make sure future versions matched or beat the competition in key areas.

*"This is typical product marketing work at a mature company. They stitch together responses across product and go-to-market that are critical in highly competitive markets."*

### Longer-term work at mature stage
- If the company wants to change revenue mix over 18 months, PMM sets in motion the partnerships, positioning, packaging, and sales incentives.
- Foundation for strong evangelism by others — sales enablement + the larger influencer ecosystem.
- Working with marketing to understand market sentiment (there's more invisible influence at maturity).

### Uneven marketing across products
*"For mature companies, not all products are equal."* Older products may need technical upgrading but little marketing. A new product might require a lot of marketing investment. A strategic product suite might require a lot of extra PMM work.

**Rule:** *"Product marketing should be intentionally applied to the areas of greatest market need."*

## Ch 29's operational discipline
> *"The gap between the aspirations of product marketing and reality often leads to needless disappointment. Take the time to keep the organization's expectations of the role aligned among all teams, not just the ones product marketing works with frequently."*

> *"Throughout all the stages of a company, product marketing's core responsibilities stay relatively stable. It's the range, focus, and complexity of how they apply them that changes as companies mature."*

> *"The key at any stage is to be unafraid to adapt the scope of the role and to communicate it clearly when you do. It's not easy, but it's what makes impactful product marketing possible."*

## Mature-company inflection points (Ch 30)
Ch 30's four scenarios where PMM is a *powerful catalyst* in transformation:

### 1. "Traditional" company becomes tech-first
Ch 30's opener: a 100+ year old construction-equipment company that now employs more software than mechanical engineers — building precision construction with equipment as mobile sensing units streaming to the cloud.

**When the org went tech-forward:** they reorganized to empowered product teams. First "customers" for PMM were **internal**: marketing, process, dealer, field sales.

**PMM's role:** product info had to go beyond comparisons + website details to include context around data + technology in customers' lives. Concrete proof (a time-study using sensor guidance vs without). Tried new GTM optimized for early adopters — Valentine's Day social video loved by tech-forward followers, not as loved by corporate marketing.

*"This tension — both internal and in the market — is natural and necessary as markets and traditional companies get pushed. Product marketers in this situation must remain steadfast in nudging the market and internal groups forward."*

### 2. Single-product → multi-product company
*"This inflection point happens for every growing tech company. Some reach it sooner than they should. It's inevitable if you're succeeding."*

Challenges:
- **Organizational + market muscle memory.** Sales more comfortable selling what they know.
- **Customers don't rethink your notions** unless pushed.
- **"Mo' product, mo' better"** trap — PMM must resist it.

**Ch 30's discipline:** *"Establishing the story framing the why is important before diving into any product specifics."* Focus people on the value of a solution, not which product does what. *"Product dividing lines often reflect a company's organizational structure or maturity, not necessarily how customers want to experience value."*

**Don't assume the same GTM works.** Different products may have different competition, buying centers, market dynamics. Especially true for acquired products.

### 3. Moving from product → solution / service / customer-centric
Ch 30's Microsoft Office → Microsoft 365 example. Old Office: choose Standard or Pro based on which products were included. Microsoft 365: choose based on *segment* (Home vs Business). Products + services move fluidly across offerings.

*"This is a prime example of service, solution, or customer-centric go-to-market versus product-centric. Value is constructed around a market segment and providing ongoing value to those customers."*

**PMM sweet spot:** *"how to frame and package value to improve adoption in desired markets."*

### 4. International expansion
Ch 30's Demandbase example: ABM was established in the US (Demandbase moving to its next big message), but the European market lagged. The head of European sales relied on older ABM messaging because anything different was too far ahead of the market.

**Rule:** *"Markets in different geographies are distinct. Go-to-market tactics and messaging must be tailored to the specific needs of local markets."*

**PMM's role:** translation layer between in-country GTM specialists and HQ. International PMMs are usually overlays across multiple products/services. They:
- Represent + prioritize local market needs to product + GTM teams.
- Communicate non-obvious marketing challenges (feature name doesn't translate; technology accessibility differences; culturally-incorrect product behavior).

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Same PMM role expected at every stage.** Startups need a director-level generalist; mature companies need specialists. Roadster mistake: product team wanted a customer-success role but called it PMM.
- **Mature company, immature PMM.** *"Everyone doing their own variation on a product marketing theme."*
- **Growth-stage story too complex or too far ahead of market.** PMM's job is bringing market realities back to product.
- **"Mo' product, mo' better"** at the multi-product inflection. Story-frame the why before diving into product specifics.
- **Same GTM for a new product** at the multi-product inflection. Especially wrong for acquired products.
- **HQ messaging shipped as-is internationally.** Local market context needed — in-country PMMs as translation layer.

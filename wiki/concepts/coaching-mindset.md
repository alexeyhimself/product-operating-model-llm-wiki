# The Coaching Mindset

> The **mindset** a manager or [[product-coach|coach]] must hold before the mechanics (assessment · plan · 1:1 · written narrative · strategic context) can work at all. From *[[empowered|EMPOWERED]]* Ch 7 (Cagan & [[chris-jones|Jones]], 2020): *"A coaching mindset provides a foundation of intent. It is the framing that directs your application of coaching techniques."* Without the mindset, the tools "actually undermine their intent."

## Why it matters
Coaching mechanics without the underlying mindset produce the opposite of coaching. Ch 7's canonical example: *"you may be committed to having regular one-on-one meetings with each of your team members, but if these meetings consist primarily of you allocating and prioritizing tasks, they are not helpful — and likely even harmful — as a coaching tool."* The wiki grounds the assessment on [[pm-competency]] and the plan on [[coaching-the-pm]]; both assume the mindset described here.

## Ch 7 opener — the [[bill-campbell|Campbell]] frame
Ch 7 opens with the fullest [[bill-campbell|Bill Campbell]] canonical passage in the SVPG corpus — the *mentors-vs-coaches* distinction Cagan uses to define what a coach actually does:

> *"Coaching might be even more essential than mentoring to our careers and our teams. Whereas mentors dole out words of wisdom, coaches roll up their sleeves and get their hands dirty. They don't just believe in our potential; they get in the arena to help us realize our potential. They hold up a mirror so we can see our blind spots and they hold us accountable for working through our sore spots. They take responsibility for making us better without taking credit for our accomplishments."* — [[bill-campbell|Bill Campbell]] (via [[empowered|EMPOWERED]] Ch 7)

## The seven principles (Ch 7)
Cagan & Jones enumerate seven mindset principles. Each is a coaching commitment, not a technique — the technique goes on [[coaching-the-pm]].

### 1. Developing People Is Job #1
*"It's amazing and distressing how few managers actually subscribe to this principle."* Most managers say the right things about the team, but their actions treat the team as a *means to an end* toward aggregate product outcomes. The book's counter-standard:
- *"If you are a manager, you should be spending most of your time and energy on coaching your team."*
- *"You should measure your own job performance on the successes of your team members, even more than the success of your products."*

This is the mindset that grounds the ~50%-of-week coaching guidance on [[product-leadership]] and the "manager not a coach = not a manager" position on [[product-coaching]].

### 2. Empowering People Produces the Best Results
The new-manager anti-pattern: *"driving their team's task list."* Short-term wins, long-term loss of ownership and retention. *"Empowering means creating an environment where your people can own outcomes and not just tasks. This doesn't mean less management — it means better management."* The stance: **step back to create the space; step in to remove impediments, clarify context, provide guidance.** Grounds [[empowered-product-teams]] and [[missionaries-vs-mercenaries]] — the Doerr quote is repeated here as the reminder.

### 3. Beware Your Own Insecurities
*"Insecure managers have a particularly hard time empowering people."* The insecure manager sees the team's success as a threat to their own recognition, and responds by controlling the work or the team's visibility to leadership. *"Truly bad managers may actively undermine their own team."* Cagan's clarification: arrogance is often insecurity's outward form — the antidote is *"a healthy level of humility"* and continual self-improvement.

The corollary for leaders without the coaching experience to develop others: *"immediately find some product leader coaching for yourself. Find someone that has been there and done that at a strong product company and engage them to coach you — and help you in coaching your people."* This is the practical origin of the [[product-coach|leadership-coach]] altitude on [[product-coaching]].

### 4. Cultivate Diverse Points of View
Two mechanisms: (a) **hiring** — treat the team as a *portfolio of strengths and backgrounds*; (b) **environment** — create space where alternative views can flourish, sometimes by empowering a report to work differently than you would. Explicitly **not consensus** — the coach helps the team *"learn how to make good decisions collaboratively — leveraging the skills and expertise of each person."*

### 5. Seek Out Teaching Moments
*"Reaching potential requires working through adversity."* The coach's job is to right-size opportunities that create *some discomfort* — enough to stretch the person, not enough to break them. *"It's through pushing through the discomfort that people overcome their fears and realize what they're truly capable of."* Includes developing strengths, not just closing gaps — *"particularly important for more seasoned product people."*

### 6. Continually Earn the Trust of Your Team
*"None of your coaching efforts will be effective without trust."* Trust is earned through the coach's actions, not demanded. Concrete practices from Ch 7:
- Honest in both praise and criticism; don't sugarcoat.
- *"Praise publicly but criticize privately."*
- Support the team both privately and publicly.
- Share some of your own personal challenges to humanize the relationship.
- Express genuine interest in the person as a person — *"trust grows whenever a working relationship is humanized."*

This is the book-form primary for the trust foundation the wiki uses on [[trust-over-control]], [[stakeholder-relationship-health]], and [[coaching-the-pm]].

### 7. Have the Courage to Correct Mistakes
The hardest principle to follow. When you know a team member isn't going to make it, *"act decisively."* Avoidance harms three people: **you** (time spent on them at the expense of others), **the rest of the team** (signals tolerated mediocrity — *"a reliable path to undermining trust and killing motivation"*), and **the person herself** (denied the chance to find a role where they'll succeed).

Cagan's guardrail: *"I'm not suggesting you be cavalier about firing people or moving people into different jobs. You should always treat these decisions very seriously. I am saying that when you know, don't wait. You're not doing anyone any favors."*

Chris Jones adds the personal note: he learned coaching from a company deeply committed to it, and grew into leadership on that foundation. *"Unfortunately, most companies today are not so committed to coaching and development of their people, and you may have to be the one who models this approach."*

## Alternatives to Manager-as-Coach (Ch 7 addendum)
The default assumption in EMPOWERED is a **functional org** — PMs report to a PM manager, designers to a design manager, engineers to an engineering manager — and each manager coaches their own reports. But some orgs use alternative structures where the manager can't coach the report on the craft (e.g., a BD-background GM leading a cross-functional trio).

Cagan's rule: *"While it is our first choice to have the manager be the coach, when this is not possible, we can still provide effective coaching. The key is that someone in the organization needs to be tasked with providing the necessary coaching."* Example: a design manager elsewhere in the org is asked to coach the designer on that non-functional team. Same for PM and engineers.

The non-negotiable: *"every person on a product team knows who is specifically assigned to help her develop and reach her full potential."* Feeds the [[product-coach|product-coach]] page's in-house-vs-external framing.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **1:1s as status reviews.** Ch 7's opening example — the mechanic is present, the mindset is absent, the tool becomes harmful.
- **"Empowering" = backing off.** Empowerment without impediment-removal, context-clarification, or guidance is abandonment, not empowerment. Ch 7 is explicit: step back *and* step in.
- **Praising a struggling report to avoid the hard conversation.** Signals tolerated mediocrity; costs the team's trust and the person's chance to find a better fit.
- **Insecurity dressed as arrogance.** Bosses who suppress diverse views, control visibility to leadership, or take credit — usually a compensation for feeling threatened.
- **Substituting mentoring for coaching.** Ch 7's Campbell frame: mentors dispense wisdom; coaches "get in the arena." Both have value; only coaching develops.

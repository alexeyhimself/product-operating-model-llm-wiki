# Outcome-Based Roadmap

> A **transitional artifact** between the old feature-roadmap and the empowered-team operating model: a roadmap whose entries are *outcomes to achieve* (or problems to solve) rather than *features to ship*. Useful when stakeholders need the visual continuity of a "roadmap" but the team is no longer in the business of pre-committing features. Canon framing: [[2015-09-07-cagan-the-alternative-to-roadmaps|*The Alternative to Roadmaps*]].

## Why it matters
Two truths force this concept into existence:
- **Roadmaps don't go away just because the team adopted OKRs.** Executives, sales, and partners still want to see "where the product is going." Refusing to communicate at all isn't workable.
- **Feature-roadmaps cause the dysfunctions in [[2015-06-05-cagan-product-fail|*Product Fail*]]** — wasted ROI, mercenary culture, orphaned features.

The outcome-based roadmap is the bridge: it preserves the *communication surface* of a roadmap while replacing the *content* with problems and outcomes. Pairs with [[high-integrity-commitments]] for the small number of date-based commitments the business actually needs.

## How it works
- Entries are **problems and outcomes**, not features. "Reduce new-customer onboarding time from 30 days to 3 hours" — not "ship onboarding redesign."
- Timeframes are **honest about uncertainty** — problem entries say "we're solving this in Q3"; date-based items only appear when there's a [[high-integrity-commitments|high-integrity commitment]] behind them.
- The roadmap is **owned by leadership, not stakeholders** — derived from [[product-strategy|product strategy]] and [[team-objectives|team objectives]], not negotiated quarterly with business owners.
- **Public versions** (for partners, investors, sales) are deliberately even higher-level — vision + key bets, not a feature schedule ([[2009-01-07-cagan-product-roadmaps|canon]]).

## Why it's transitional
Cagan and Moore are explicit: this is a **bridge state**, not the destination ([[2022-09-23-moore-changing-how-you-decide-which-problems-to-solve|Moore]]). The destination is a directly-communicated **product strategy + team objectives + occasional high-integrity commitments** — the "roadmap" frame eventually dissolves as the organization gets comfortable working in problems and outcomes. But for orgs in transition, the outcome-based roadmap is genuinely useful: it lets the team operate the new way while the rest of the company adjusts.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Outcome-shaped roadmap, feature-shaped reality.** The roadmap entries say "improve activation," but in the team's planning the items are features. The roadmap is theater.
- **Treating the outcome-based roadmap as the strategy.** A list of outcomes is not a strategy — strategy is *which* problems and *why now* ([[product-strategy-quality|canon]]).
- **Hiding feature commitments inside outcomes.** "Ship onboarding redesign" relabeled as "improve onboarding" is the same feature-team OKR pattern dressed differently.
- **Forgetting commitments still need to be honored.** When something on the roadmap *is* a date-based commitment, treat it as a [[high-integrity-commitments|high-integrity commitment]] — don't pretend uncertainty exists where it doesn't.
- **Skipping the bridge.** Some orgs can move directly from feature-roadmaps to OKR-only communication. Many can't without losing alignment with sales/marketing/finance/partners.

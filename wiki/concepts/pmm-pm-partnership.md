# PMM ↔ PM Partnership

> The **constant** across every version of product marketing practiced ([[loved|LOVED]] Ch 8). *"Product marketing is the go-to-market yin to product management's product yang. Their end goal is the same — a product people love and buy — and they get there with a collaboration that lifts both functions up to their full potential."* The PMM-side view; the PM-side view is [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Ch 23.

## Why it matters
Ch 8 opens with Lauchengco's own experience on Word for the Mac: her PM counterpart, Jeff Vierling, and her split customer + market work — *"One of us translated them into the product getting built; the other translated it into go-to-market. Because each of us had the other's expertise to rely on, we felt more confident in the decisions we made."*

Ch 8 makes the load-bearing structural claim: *"The product manager–product marketer pairing is the constant in any version of product marketing practiced. This is in contrast to the relationships with marketing and sales, which evolve quite a bit as companies mature and depend a lot on how those teams are organized."*

Ch 8 also grounds the practical structural framing: PMM works best **embedded with the product team**, with a designated PM partner — *"Some refer to it as the triad expanding into a quad, but the bottom line is the product marketer feels like the product squad's designated marketing strategist."* This is the wiki's canonical *"trio + PMM = quad"* framing.

## Beyond the core product team — what PMM adds
*"Market strategizing and product building are different skills. It's why both jobs exist and both roles are needed."* Lauchengco's distinction:
- **PMs use everything they build to achieve a product vision.**
- **PMMs use everything product builds as a portfolio of possibilities to achieve go-to-market ends.**

**Translating discovery work into GTM-implication is PMM's wheelhouse.** *"For product marketers, they take market learning and examine how it might impact marketing channels, distribution partners, pricing, packaging, timing, positioning, or out-marketing competitors. This is the market-fit side of product / market fit."*

Backs the wiki's use of "market side of PMF" inside [[the-four-big-risks|viability]].

### Tactical pre-work PMM leads
When direct sales is the primary distribution channel, PMM thinks through:
- Sales' capacity for new functionality or a new product.
- Whether incentive structure, pricing, or packaging is in place so sales can sell.
- Whether features are *marketable* — not just buildable.

Ch 8's diagnostic warning: *"It's easy for product teams to get myopic in managing backlog, focusing on what can be built and not on how it's going to market. Product marketing is the team's partner in understanding if features or functions are marketable."*

## Indicators the partnership is working well (Ch 8)
The seven signals Lauchengco lists — coach against these when auditing a PM ↔ PMM relationship:

1. **The why behind product go-to-market is understood.** PMs feel it's a thoughtful approach that matches the product being built.
2. **Product squads want PMM involved in major product decisions** to understand market implications.
3. **PMs and PMMs collaborate heavily on positioning + messaging.** PMs are comfortable it's technically accurate and aligns with the product vision.
4. **PMs are highly engaged in analyst relations.**
5. **Competitive response is rapid, collaborative, and coordinated.**
6. **Pricing + packaging ownership is clear**, and packaging serves customer segments and business goals well.
7. **PMs feel they can offload most collateral, content creation, and sales enablement** with minimum consultation.

## Set It Up for Success — the ratio question
Ch 8's practical structural answer to the *"how many PMs per PMM?"* question:

- **No typical ratio.** Ranges from **1:1 to 1:5** in practice; **average is 1:2.5 PMs per PMM**.
- **Depends on GTM model + org support.** More PMMs where product complexity is high or where dedicated sales-enablement / program-management teams *don't* exist.

**Alignment isn't headcount-driven — it's driven by how customers experience the product and where the business wants to grow.**

### The five typical PMM alignment patterns (Ch 8)
Lauchengco enumerates five configurations by company/product maturity — useful when structuring a PMM team:

1. **Startup.** Product + company GTM are synonymous. PMM embedded with both product and GTM teams; rapidly adapts to ill-defined markets + customers.
2. **Individual product forward.** Market/category forged by products defining them; credibility from product position + adoption. PMM ↔ PM 1:1 designated relationship.
3. **Product suites forward.** Multi-product companies; PMM shifts to product-suite level or customer segments (incl. verticals). *"In these scenarios, there are product marketers without product manager designates."* They rely on embedded PMMs.
4. **Verticals / New Markets.** Vertical-focused PMM without designated PM counterparts; pull the story together across products for a vertical's needs.
5. **Customer segments.** Segment-focused PMM (may include different GTM models). Market-needs-driven; less product-driven. *"Looking for market wedges that help drive adoption."*

## Anti-patterns — what better looks like (Ch 8)
Three signals the partnership needs work:

| Anti-pattern | Better looks like |
|---|---|
| **What product ships, sales can't sell.** Product created in a tech-first vacuum. | PMM is a partner in product planning + provides input on what's meaningful to the market. Beyond "can we wrap a story around it," PMM should have enough market insight to *challenge* product teams if plans seem out of line with market realities. |
| **Product ships at a terrible time to market it.** Product teams define releases by *when work is done*, not by when customers can best adopt. | PMM always represents the market + customer perspective *before* decisions on major-release timing are made. |
| **PMs are tapped too often for product collateral or sales support.** Evidence that PMM doesn't know the product well enough or that tools created around product aren't strong enough. | The PM takes time to get PMM up to speed on product so this work can be reasonably offloaded. PMM knows enough to say what's most important + represent it well in collateral. |

## Best-practice touchpoints (Ch 8 cadence)
The wiki adopts this cadence as the PMM ↔ PM operating rhythm:

- **Ongoing** — Understanding market fit (Ch 11 detail; steady stream of small tests in market).
- **Weekly** — PMM part of regular product-squad meeting. *"An absolute minimum of weekly."* Which meeting depends on the squad's cadences.
- **Bimonthly or monthly** — Product planning reviews. Regular review of what's been learned in product + market discovery + what's taking shape in product commitments. Sales, CS, and other functions also present; PMM raises market implications + opportunities.
- **Quarterly** — Review product go-to-market and product planning. GTM activities have longer lag-times (a sales-process change takes at least a quarter to show).

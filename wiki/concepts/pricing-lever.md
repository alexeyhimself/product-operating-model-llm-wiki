# The Pricing Lever

> **Pricing is value engineering — not cost recovery.** [[loved|LOVED]] Ch 17. *"Pricing is not about what it costs to produce a service. It's about people's perceived value of a product and their willingness to pay."*

## Why it matters
Ch 17 opens with **Nike sunglasses at a women's marathon focus group.** Lauchengco free-ranged around a room full of sunglasses, stack-ranked her top choices by what mattered personally (sportiness · trendiness · feel · color) — then they revealed the prices.

*"When I saw the pair I'd ranked as number one cost $300, it immediately dropped to my lowest choice. That was more than I was willing to pay for fashion sunglasses from Nike. But I was willing to pay $125 for a performance pair designed for racing. Those formerly-ranked-number-four sunglasses were now my number one pick. The perceived value matched my desired use case."*

The universal calculus: *"if I like this product, is it worth it to me at this price from this company?"* Brand plays an enormous role. **Apple watches** price $199–$399. **Google Fitbit** watches price $179–$299 despite similar features. Same delta comes from brand.

## Pricing basics — Ch 17 fundamentals
Ch 17's discipline for evaluating price:
- *"It's important to look at pricing from the lens of the customer: how much do they value what you do for them relative to the price they're paying your company."*
- **Price is relative, not absolute.**
- *"Often when people think their product has a pricing problem, the real problem is the perceived value isn't there. Price was set relative to similar products in the same category, not to market sentiment or brand perceptions."*

**Six things factor into a customer's value calculus** (Ch 17):
1. **Context** — how pricing is presented on a page, especially relative to other options.
2. **Brand.**
3. **Competitive alternatives.**
4. **Availability.**
5. **Ease.**
6. **Budget.**

## Who owns pricing?
Ch 17's honest answer: *"It varies wildly based on go-to-market, business models, product complexity, leadership, and company stage. While it increasingly falls to specialists, I've seen just as many product managers own it as I have product marketers."*

Ch 17's three distinct aspects — one may be owned by different functions:

### 1. Monetization strategy — how and when you'll make money
*"Distinct from how much you'll charge."* Often a joint exercise between **product · business operations · PMM**.

### 2. Pricing strategy — determining the actual price
*"Heavily driven or influenced by teams in finance or business operations."* Shaped by:
- Customer funnel dynamics.
- Conversion estimates.
- Scenario forecasting.
- Cost to build + support each customer.
- For anything sold by salesforce, sales provides input.

**PMM's specific role:** *"Framing the perception of value for the customer falls to product marketing."*

### 3. Packaging strategy — bundles that serve segments + business goals
*"Typically driven by product marketing with product a big partner in enabling it."* *"Distinct from pricing; they do different things."*

**Wherever pricing + packaging live**, PMM's role is to *"ground it in real value for customers and make sure it serves different customer segments and business goals well."*

## The four essential concepts for pricing metric design (Ch 17)
*"When selling tech products, you want the hard thinking to be around whether or not to buy your product, not understanding how it's priced."*

1. **Use a metric that reflects value + grows as your product provides more value.** Example: Dropbox costs more with each additional terabyte. *"Want more? Pay more. Keep it simple."*
2. **Simple enough for customers to do the math in their heads.** *"People like to know what they're in for so they can assess if the value is worth the cost. Per user pricing works because it's easy to calculate what the cost might be depending on how many people are on a particular team."*
3. **Easily measurable.** IT + compliance have to know whether they're complying with contract terms. *"Per seat pricing seems simple on the surface, but if you're trying to grow usage inside a company rapidly, individual licenses can be an impediment."*
4. **Something a CFO or procurement person will understand as they compare your cost to others.** They won't understand the nuances of your differentiators — they're coming at this from total cost of ownership + ROI. *"They're comparing your perceived worth relative to other contracts."*

## What drives your business — cheap vs best
Ch 17's blunt spectrum: *"When it comes to pricing, the psychology can be oversimplified into two types: those who buy because it's cheap and those who buy because it's the best. Decide which end of the pricing spectrum best matches your product and go-to-market."*

Two examples of the extremes:
- **Cheap / free version** → capture customers who could be evangelists.
- **iPhone $599 initial pricing** → keep beachhead customers small in number, feeling exclusive (see [[technology-adoption-life-cycle]]).

**Whether you *can* be a premium product is about perception + brand.** *"Is this product worth the price from your company?"*

**If you're a premium product and don't hear complaints, you're leaving money on the table:** Ch 17's very-early-stage sales-leader anecdote: *"'I basically double the price at every meeting and am waiting for someone to blink. So far, that hasn't happened. We still don't know its price.'"*

**Find breaking points via real customers.** *"You can't be sure about your product's value until you're out there in the market, pricing with actual customers."*
- **B2B early-stage** — you might not know until you hit 20–30 customers. Customized pricing pre-threshold is okay. Post-threshold: establish repeatable guardrails.
- **B2C** — revisit pricing frequently; price testing is easy online.

## Packaging strategy — the three canonical strategies (Table 17.1)
*"The job of packaging is to move people toward and not away from a purchase decision. It's counterintuitive, but lots of options attract gawkers and can prevent purchases. Decision fatigue is a real thing."*

| Strategy | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **All Inclusive** | Early product | Simple for customers · Simple for sales |
| **Platform + Add-Ons** | Expanding product lines + feature sets | Flexible for customers but brings complexity · Lets sales customize approach |
| **Good / Better / Best (editions)** | Well-defined use cases + market segments | Drive highest possible ASP · Less decision fatigue · Repeatable sales execution |

### The editions guideline (Ch 17)
**Entry-level edition:** should include the **must-have** product(s). *"You want the vast majority of potential customers to think 'look at all I get for this price.'"*

**Next-level edition:** contains must-haves for **specific market or customer segments**. Not for everyone; for those who need it, high-value and clearly differentiated.

**Add-ons / additional editions:** niche; highly specific use cases.

## Trends shift expectations of value (Ch 17)
Purchasing behavior has shifted toward **cloud + subscription** in both D2C (Netflix · Spotify · Apple Music/TV+) and B2B (Google · Salesforce). Lauchengco's Word example: Microsoft Office Home & Student used to cost $104.99 (used for years). The renamed **Microsoft 365** gives more and costs $99.99/year — a subscription.

*"Smart pricing and packaging can remove unnecessary friction and enhance the perception of value. But most importantly, it is one of your most powerful business levers."*

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Pricing based on cost, not value.** Ch 17's opening reframe.
- **Setting price relative to category competitors** without checking market sentiment or brand perception.
- **Metric complexity.** Customers can't do the math in their heads → friction.
- **Too many packaging options.** Decision fatigue prevents purchase.
- **Premium positioning without brand to support it.** *"My reaction to $300 fashion sunglasses from Nike was very different than it might have been if they were from Gucci."*
- **No premium-market complaints.** Leaving money on the table.
- **Waiting to know pricing until it's "right."** Only actual customers reveal breaking points.

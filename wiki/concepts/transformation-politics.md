# Transformation Politics

> Every transformation is a people change at scale, and every people change is political. Cagan (channeling Christian Idiodi): **"every problem is a people problem."** In every successful transformation, leaders were aware of and intentional about the political challenges; they did not avoid them ([[2024-11-21-cagan-transformation-politics|canon]]).

## Why it matters
A transformation to the product model **redistributes decision rights** — from stakeholders (who used to fill the roadmap) to product teams (who now choose solutions). It changes who is consulted, who is accountable, and who is judged for results. Without explicit political work, transformations stall at [[model-maturity|Level 2 — checkbox transformation]] because the new structures don't have the trust they need to operate.

## Eight political dimensions (Cagan's framing)
Each comes from [[2024-11-21-cagan-transformation-politics|Transformation Politics]] directly:

### 1. Product Leaders
The product leader is the key. Where the leader hasn't worked in the model, a [[types-of-product-coaching|product leadership coach]] is usually essential to fill the gap. The leader must establish working relationships with executives and stakeholders — starting with **humility and a genuine desire to learn** their concerns and constraints. The product leader is "the face of the broader product organization" — be careful which PMs you expose to stakeholders, and when. **"The product leader will be judged by her weakest product manager."**

### 2. Product Managers
"Few things will undermine confidence and trust faster than people with a product manager title that are viewed by stakeholders or executives as clueless about how the company actually works." Hence the need for **effective PM onboarding** on business viability — marketing, sales, finance, monetization, legal, compliance, security, privacy. **Protect the integrity of the "product manager" title** — and any title with the word "product" in it. Resist title proliferation (assistant/associate PM, multiple flavors of PO).

### 3. Change Management
Gradualism rarely works. Once a transformation starts, the **clock starts ticking** — orgs run out of patience before small changes can show impact. The more reliable path is **demonstrating the impact of a much larger set of changes in a contained scope** (= [[pilot-teams|pilot teams]]). Show what good really looks like, on real work.

### 4. Pilot Teams
The **political mechanism** of transformation. See [[pilot-teams|pilot teams framework]] for the design checklist. Strong product leaders hand-pick members, pick problems carefully, ensure training and coaching, and aim for **business outcomes** stakeholders will respect.

### 5. Product Discovery
**Trust the stakeholders on the problem.** Stakeholders have lived with the problems for years and have very good understanding of what needs solving. Pilot teams that spend weeks or months in problem discovery while stakeholders watch are politically corrosive. **Race to a successful solution.** If the team's problem-framing turns out to be wrong, solution testing will reveal it within days.

The general rule: if a product team works the same problem for **more than two weeks**, they need immediate coaching to decide whether to continue ([[discovery-health|canon]]).

### 6. Product Strategy
This is the **politically hardest** of the three transformation dimensions — moving control over what to work on from stakeholders to product leaders. The wiki's operational rule: **hold this dimension for last**, after Solve and Build have produced trust. Cagan: "you will likely have better results holding off on changing how you decide which problems to solve until after you have largely won the hearts and minds of the stakeholders and executives." (This refines [[2022-05-31-moore-transformation-defined|Moore's earlier 'pursue in parallel' guidance]] — both are canon, see [[transformation|Transformation page]].)

### 7. Keeping the Lights On
Every product team also has a responsibility to handle the never-ending list of minor items stakeholders need to run the business. If a team — pilot or not — appears to ignore this, stakeholders will insist on resources for it one way or another. **Budget for it in pilot teams**, or the political support evaporates.

### 8. Internal Evangelism
Large-company transformation takes **1–3 years**. Very few orgs are prepared to wait that long for results. Maintain a **drumbeat of real business outcomes**, monthly minimum:
- **Real business results**, not activity. ("Don't confuse business results with activities.")
- **Both successes and failures.** Sugar-coating destroys credibility.
- **Frequency matters.** Monthly is the floor; more is better.

## What this is *not*
- **Not "playing politics" in the bad sense.** Cagan is explicit about this. Politics here means *being intentional about the people-change* a transformation requires.
- **Not flattering stakeholders.** Trusting their problem framing is not flattery; it's respect for their domain expertise and a faster path to results.
- **Not waterfall.** Holding the *Decide* change for last doesn't mean staging Solve+Build in advance. It means sequencing **what you reset trust on** when.

## Anti-patterns
- **Avoiding the politics.** "We just need to do the work; the politics will sort itself out." It won't.
- **Picking the politically loudest stakeholder as the pilot's customer.** The point is *visible business outcome*, not appeasing the loudest voice. Pick the problem that, if solved, demonstrates the model.
- **Long problem-discovery against stakeholder will.** Politically toxic; usually unnecessary; rarely worth the trust cost.
- **Sweeping organizational change before pilot proof.** New titles, new orgs, new processes adopted enterprise-wide before a pilot has produced a single business outcome.
- **Quarterly transformation reports of *activity*.** "We trained X people, ran Y workshops, established Z forums." None of these are business outcomes.

# PMM ↔ Marketing Partnership

> The relationship between **product marketing** and the **rest of marketing** (brand, PR, events, social, demand gen, content, community). *"An energizing and crucial partnership in how go-to-market scales"* ([[loved|LOVED]] Ch 9). PMM is the product's ambassador to the marketing team; marketing brings much of the product's go-to-market to life.

## Why it matters
Ch 9 opens with **Workiva** — annual event for SEC financial professionals with a big-budget, multiday, in-person format. Continuing-education sessions, Spin-to-Win prizes, make-a-sundae stations, aquarium socials, theme-park access, army of PMs and PMMs answering questions. *"Attendees left the conference raving fans, engendering loyal word-of-mouth in a community not really known for that. It even swayed skeptical analysts."* The conference paid for itself every year in customer conversion.

The lesson: *"marketing brings much of go-to-market to life, which means they play an enormous role in shaping a customer's experience of a company — from things as small as an email signature to major events like Workiva's."*

## Where PMM ends and marketing begins
Ch 9 pins down the division of labor:

- **Marketing specialties** (brand, PR, events, social, demand gen) live at the **company level** — they serve all products + overall goals.
- **Product marketing** makes sure the right work is happening in the marketing machinery **for a product's go-to-market**. PMM *"defines the strategic palette from which a product's go-to-market activities get drawn, then guides their execution."*
- **The marketing organization relies on PMM to get the product part right.**

Two things PMM provides that marketing consumes:
1. **The messaging foundation** (led by PMM, often tested by marketing) — how the marketing team knows *what to say* about a product and in which circumstances.
2. **The strategy foundation** (led by PMM in the product-GTM plan) — how marketing teams know the *why* and *when*. Teams then collaborate on the *what* and *how*.

## The four marketing goals → activities map (Ch 9)
Ch 9 connects marketing goals to typical activities — useful when a PM is trying to understand *what* marketing is doing and *why*:

| Marketing goal | Typical activities |
|---|---|
| **Make customers aware** of the problem, solution, and company | Advertising (TV/radio/print/outdoor · mobile/search/display) · website · SEO · press articles · analyst reports |
| **Encourage consideration** of a solution | White papers · videos · customer stories · events · email · account-based marketing · direct mail · partnerships · PR · press articles |
| **Encourage purchase or renewal** | Pricing · packaging · customer events · reviews. Plus customer success (not marketing but part of the mix). |
| **Enhance awareness and loyalty around the brand** | Customer community · social media · content · newsletters · influencers · third-party events · press articles |

Lauchengco's key line: *"A broad, well-coordinated mix — not any one thing — is the winning formula for good product go-to-markets. Often, it takes deliberate experimentation to find the mix that works best. And that mix changes over time as products move through the adoption curve."*

## Indicators the partnership is working well (Ch 9)
Seven signals — coach against these:

1. **Marketing understands the nuances of the market and how to best segment customers.** Marketers feel they know key insights that help them do great work.
2. **Marketing understands the context** for why a product is valued, not just what features matter, and has a strong messaging framework.
3. **Marketing understands the why** behind recommended activities, and *"a variety of new ideas are explored to reach new or existing markets. Marketing teams do more than just double down on what has worked in the past."*
4. **Decisions around product naming shifts** or moves toward a line-of-business brand are done collaboratively and with the larger company brand in mind.
5. **The cost to acquire customers is sustainable** for the business.
6. **Teams collaborate on how to adapt product-related marketing** in response to shifts in markets.
7. **PMMs feel their product's go-to-market goals are being well served** by marketing actions.

## Set It Up for Success
- **Reporting structure ≠ collaboration.** *"In most organizations, product marketing reports into marketing, which creates a natural presumed alignment between teams… But shared reporting structure does not guarantee good collaboration."* (Ch 26 explores PMM reporting into product as the alternative — future page.)
- **PMM directs, both sides learn faster.** *"The product marketer is constantly trying to find new ways to expand product go-to-market, while the marketer is constantly trying to use product to improve marketing outcomes. By working well together, everybody learns faster."*
- **Agile marketing is one systematic way.** Led by PMM, weekly meetings with marketing specialists to prioritize workload + learn from recent activities. Ch 12 detail — see [[agile-product-marketing]].

## Anti-patterns — what better looks like (Ch 9)
Three signals + fixes:

| Anti-pattern | Better looks like |
|---|---|
| **Campaigns perform but product isn't getting positioned.** Easy to maximize campaign-level performance metrics without aggregating to whether the business + positioning benefit. | PMM is part of the planning process for campaigns to make sure — *in aggregate* — they help position the product, say the right messages, and stay mapped to the company's goals. |
| **"Future state" is too far ahead of current reality.** Marketing tries to solve slow velocity or lack of differentiation by pushing hard on an aspirational state so far ahead of product realities it detracts from credibility. | PMM is the ideal partner in finding the line between aspirational-future-that-inspires and where current product reality should play a bigger role. |
| **Creativity that pushes the edge but doesn't connect with customers.** Marketing falls in love with an idea that gets attention but doesn't feel true to the target audience. | *"Clever always has a place, but the most clever also connect to the product too."* PMMs are the eyes of the audience. Test bold ideas in front of customers before committing. |

## Best-practice touchpoints (Ch 9 cadence)
- **Weekly — Alignment meetings.** PMM + marketing align on which campaigns/assets are being prioritized. Shifts in product; urgent items (like competitive response) can jump the queue. Fine-tune campaigns. See [[agile-product-marketing]] (Ch 12).
- **Monthly — Activity reviews.** What's been learned should be applied into next month's plan. Good cadence for funnel metrics (which take time to reflect changes). Are activities adding up to the intended business outcome?
- **Quarterly — Revisit product go-to-market and funnel metrics.** Marketing gets updates on product priority shifts. Tie sales results into where to focus next quarter. The Ch 19 one-sheet PGTM canvas is the anchor artifact.

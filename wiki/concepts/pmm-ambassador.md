# PMM Fundamental 1 — Ambassador

> **Connect Customer and Market Insights.** The first of Martina Lauchengco's four fundamentals of [[product-marketing|product marketing]] ([[loved|LOVED]] Ch 3). The **foundation on which all other product-marketing work gets built** — Lauchengco: *"a deep understanding of customers and markets is Fundamental 1 of product marketing. It is simply the foundation on which everything in a product's go-to-market gets built."*

## Why it matters
Everything else PMM does — [[pmm-strategist|strategy]], [[pmm-storyteller|positioning + messaging]], [[pmm-evangelist|evangelism]] — is built on what PMM knows about the customer and the market. Without it, PMM defaults to *"cranking out content that talks about the product"* rather than *"making it compelling, credible, or desirable for others to talk about"* (Ch 6).

Ch 3's Dropbox opener grounds why the fundamental has to be *lived*, not delegated. Julie Herendeen (then VP Global Marketing at Dropbox) had *"users in the tens of millions and all the data that came with them"* but pulled her whole marketing team out of the building to visit customers. The team returned with insights *"I couldn't see any of this in the data"*: Dropbox customers were smallish businesses who needed to collaborate on *big* jobs (sharing video-shoot dailies with clients); they valued *"the freedom to work with whomever they wanted, however they wanted"*. Marketing shifted messaging, channels, and campaigns as a result. Data alone missed the nuance.

Lauchengco's stance-check: *"Most underestimate how nuanced and layered both customers and markets are today, and just how much time and work it takes to truly understand them."*

## Market Sensing — the baseline practices
Four minimum practices PMM should run to stay connected to customer + market realities:
1. **Have direct customer interaction — ideally weekly.**
2. **Develop a standard set of open-ended questions to ask customers or prospects.**
3. **Reflect insights into product and go-to-market team discussions.**
4. **Write the most important insights down so they can be easily shared and used.**

**Market fit ≠ product fit.** *"Think of it as the market side of product market fit. It must be probed right from the start just as product is being explored."* This is the LOVED framing that grounds the "market side of product-market fit" phrase the wiki uses on [[the-four-big-risks]] (viability) and [[product-marketing]].

**Distinguish insight from noise — PMM's judgment call.** *"Not all customer insights are equal in unlocking markets. Product marketing is responsible for deciding which key learnings help go-to-market and product teams do their jobs better. Will an insight help a team make a decision or tradeoff on what to say or do next? If the answer is yes, it's additive. If the answer is no, archive it."*

### The eleven market-sensing questions (Ch 3)
PMM tries to answer these across the entire buyer's journey, capturing both rational and emotional motivations:

1. What are they trying to do?
2. Do they recognize and prioritize this problem?
3. What is motivating them to solve the problem?
4. What compels them to take action?
5. What in this product delivers the most value?
6. Who is most likely to value and buy this product?
7. What starts the journey toward acquiring the product?
8. How might a product get discovered and become more desired over the entire journey?
9. How might we reduce friction in acquiring the product?
10. What do people need to see or hear to become customers?
11. How can we delight customers so much that they want to talk about the product with others?

*"Rarely are they clear or complete at the start. As with products, learning the market side of product fit is a dynamic process. Start with a reasonable hypothesis and use everything in market — websites, emails, sales conversations — to iterate toward answers."*

## Third-Party Insights
The market is shaped by more than direct customer feedback — *"it is influenced heavily by the ecosystem surrounding it."* PMM runs a regular practice of gathering signal from **third-party data, research, reports, articles, websites, reviews, press, and social media**. Two specific tools Ch 3 names:

- **Google search trends** — how one set of words compares to another for discovery.
- **Content services** showing top-read content on a subject — for engagement patterns.

At mature companies with dedicated customer-insights / customer-research / data-analytics teams — *"they are a meaningful shortcut in gaining market and customer learning. Use them!"*

PMM's role is to **intersect direct feedback with third-party insight** and use the combination to direct internal conversations. The Pocket case ([[loved|LOVED]] Ch 1) is the worked example: despite more users and platforms than Instapaper, Pocket wasn't perceived as category leader. PMM's job was *"to move the ecosystem's perception toward their realities."*

## The Competition
Ch 3 makes an explicit distinction: **the competitive landscape shapes market perception even when your product doesn't change.** Three real examples Lauchengco cites:
1. A competitor changed only their *sales process* and started winning head-to-head deals.
2. A competitor **hadn't shipped a product yet** but wrote well and often about their POV; searches for the category surfaced them first — *"perceived as the market leader before they even shipped a product."*
3. A company in an adjacent category held an event that got press coverage promoting features the category leader lacked — the leader looked like it was playing catch-up.

**The dual PMM discipline for competition:**
- **Don't ignore competitors** — *"you can't ignore how much they can shape perceptions."*
- **Don't over-rotate to them** — *"companies can lose their own way if overly responsive to competitors' agendas rather than staying focused on what's best for their customers or market."*

**The chess frame:** *"Meet challenge with challenge. Outplay the competition whenever you can. But think of this as chess; move ahead of your competition, don't just respond."*

**PMM = decides what merits response.** *"Product marketing helps the company stay the course for what is most important and exercise judgment on what merits response."* Compare this to a competitive-analysis-only role, which reacts to every move.

## Ambassador of Insights
PMM is *"the ambassador for all these customer and market insights, so they must be a part of the right internal conversations."* Concrete moves:
- Accelerate a product feature.
- Direct an engineering blog to diminish a competitor's approach.
- Route the insight to whichever internal team can act — product, marketing, or sales.

### Insights ≠ requirements
Ch 3's most-quoted discipline for the PM–PMM boundary: *"Customer insights can sometimes be heard as customer requirements. These are two distinct things. When product marketers bring customer or market insights to a product team, it's important to see them for what they are — a way for the team to make smarter decisions based on market realities, not just technical ones. How any insight impacts product priorities falls to the product manager to decide."*

This grounds the Ch 8 PM ↔ PMM partnership: PMM feeds insight in; PM owns [[the-four-big-risks|value + viability]] decisions. Related on [[product-manager]] and [[cross-functional-partnering]].

### Different insight artifacts for different functions
Customer insights get translated into artifacts with function-specific purposes:
- **Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)** — often used by **product**.
- **Personas** — often used by **design** or **product**.
- **Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)** — often used by **sales**; account fit + likelihood to buy (technologies in use, org size, budget, internal champion).
- **Customer segmentations** — often used by **marketing**.

*"Each has a purpose that is specific to the function."* Lauchengco's example: an ICP might overlap with problems in a JTBD story — but the ICP is used to determine buy likelihood, not to describe the customer's problem. Don't collapse them.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Data-only.** *"'This is amazing. I'm learning so much' and 'I couldn't see any of this in the data.'"* The Dropbox insight came from customer visits, not the tens-of-millions of user data.
- **Broad customer categories treated as monoliths.** "Small businesses" is a category; a *marketing-video-production shop sharing dailies with a client* is a customer.
- **Insight hoarding.** PMM collects insight without deciding what's decision-useful. If it doesn't help the team choose, archive it.
- **Reacting to every competitor move.** Over-rotation to competitors' agendas costs focus on what customers actually value.
- **Treating insights as requirements** (Ch 3 boundary) — PM owns priority decisions; PMM feeds the insight.
- **Delegating to a research team without doing the direct customer work yourself.** Even at Dropbox scale, the *"get out of the building"* discipline matters.

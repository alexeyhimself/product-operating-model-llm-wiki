# Time to Money

> The right metric for a product team. **Time-to-market** is when the thing shipped; **time-to-money** is when it achieved the necessary **outcome**. Product teams optimize for the latter; project teams can only meaningfully optimize for the former. Canon: [[2023-08-17-cagan-from-projects-to-products|Cagan, From Projects to Products]].

## Why it matters
- A feature that shipped on time but produced no value is a *worse* outcome than a feature that shipped two months later and moved the metric. Time-to-market measures the wrong thing for most product work.
- Time-to-money makes the [[product-discovery|discovery]] investment economically obvious — discovery shrinks iterations from quarters to weeks.
- It reframes "speed" away from project velocity (number of features ÷ time) toward outcome velocity (time until the metric moves).

## The math
([[2022-09-20-moore-changing-how-you-solve-problems|Moore]]) A typical feature requires 3–5 iterations to produce the necessary business result.

- **Feature team:** each iteration goes through a quarterly planning cycle. 3–5 iterations × 1–2 quarters each = **1–2 years** before the feature produces the desired return — assuming stakeholders are willing to re-list the iterations on the roadmap (they often aren't).
- **Empowered product team:** 3–5 iterations happen **in discovery, in 1–2 weeks**, mostly with prototypes (cheaper by an order of magnitude or two). The validated build then ships in a few more weeks.

This is the structural reason small empowered teams routinely out-produce much larger feature-team orgs.

## What it doesn't mean
- **Dates never matter.** Sometimes a date *is* the priority — see Cagan's *Managing Commitments in an Agile Team* (referenced in [[2023-08-17-cagan-from-projects-to-products]]) for high-integrity commitments. Time-to-money is the **default**, not the only metric.
- **You can hit time-to-money with a feature team.** You can't, structurally — the org isn't held accountable for the outcome.

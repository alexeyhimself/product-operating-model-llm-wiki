# Corporate Courage

> The load-bearing leadership disposition that separates the small subset of companies that successfully transform from the many more that don't. Named as **Key #10** in [[keys-to-successful-transformation|Cagan's ten keys]] (Ch 48 of *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]*). Also present in older SVPG canon as **the ninth reason for weak product** ([[2010-08-16-cagan-top-10-reasons-for-weak-product|"Lack of Corporate Courage"]]). The wiki gives it its own concept page because it names a specific thing the other keys and reasons circle around: the *willingness of executives and senior leaders to actually commit to the change* — beyond understanding it, beyond nominally sponsoring it, beyond announcing it.

## What it is
Cagan's Ch 48 line: *"every case of successful transformation we know of required true courage from executives and other senior leaders."* Concretely: the willingness to **make a leap** *"to a fundamentally different model — even when the current model is broken."*

- **A leap of faith.** The word "faith" is Cagan's — it acknowledges that the leap has to be made before the evidence is fully in. Waiting for certainty is what keeps most companies stuck.
- **A leadership disposition, not a company property.** Companies don't have courage; specific people do. The observable question is whether *these particular executives* are willing to do what it takes.
- **Not bluster.** Cagan explicitly notes that the leaders who *do* transform *"don't get the recognition for this courage they deserve"* — courage in this sense is quiet and specific to the decisions that get made, not a stated stance.

## Why it matters
Courage sits alongside [[keys-to-successful-transformation|Key #1 (CEO active support)]] as the pair of leadership-disposition keys — but they are not the same thing. **CEO active support** is a *commitment* (the top of the org has decided to transform). **Corporate courage** is the *willingness to actually pay the costs of that commitment* — political capital, incumbent stakeholder relationships, quarterly forecast risk, personal reputation, and the reality that transformation *"takes anywhere from six months to two years"* (Ch 48 intro to Part VIII).

Cagan's counter-observation is stark: *"We know many more senior leaders who don't have the courage and aren't willing to do what it takes to help their company not just survive, but thrive."*

## How it shows up (and how it doesn't)

**Present:**
- **Divestments and shutdowns to enable focus.** [[datasite-transformation|Datasite/Merrill's transformation was as much what was cut as what was built]] — a courage decision at CEO Rusty Wiley's level.
- **Bet-the-company decisions on the strategic bet.** [[adobe-creative-cloud-transformation|Adobe's Creative Suite → Creative Cloud]] required CEO Shantanu Narayen and CFO Mark Garrett to make a *"potentially a bet-the-company bet."* [[lea-hickman|Hickman]] names it that way.
- **Naming and correcting the anti-patterns publicly.** A CEO who cancels an in-flight big-bang project once it's clear it's the [[transformation-as-a-project|named anti-pattern]] — visible loss of face, visible correction.
- **Killing low-ROI initiatives.** Cagan's older framing on [[2010-08-16-cagan-top-10-reasons-for-weak-product|weak product reason #9]] and [[model-maturity|Level-3 → Level-4 sustaining work]] — celebrating teams that *killed* low-value work rather than pushing it to launch.
- **Sequencing patiently.** Accepting that pilot teams take a quarter or two to produce visible outcomes, and defending that time budget to the board.
- **Trusting stakeholders on the problem** (per [[transformation-politics]]) rather than defensively re-doing their problem discovery.

**Absent (or its opposite — corporate cowardice):**
- Naming a "digital transformation" leader while continuing business-as-usual (the [[keys-to-successful-transformation|Key #1 anti-pattern]]).
- Announcing the transformation and then measuring it by activity ("we trained X, ran Y workshops, established Z forums") rather than outcomes.
- Waiting for competitive existential pressure to move — [[the-need-for-speed|Cagan's ten reasons speed matters]] is at least partly about the courage to move before you're forced.
- Sacrificing the pilot when a stakeholder complains — the [[transformation-as-a-project|checkbox transformation]] retreat.
- Blaming individuals ("we needed a better CPO") when the leadership disposition was the deficit.

## Relationship to the other keys
Corporate courage is the **enabling disposition** for six of the other nine keys:
- **Key #1 (CEO)** — a supportive CEO without courage becomes a nominal sponsor.
- **Key #2 (Role of Technology)** — moving from cost-center to profit-center funding requires courage from the CFO's side too.
- **Key #7 (Insights-Based Strategy)** — *"and this meant all the things we were not going to do"* ([[datasite-transformation|Idiodi]]) is a courage decision every time.
- **Key #8 (Stakeholder Collaboration)** — asking incumbent stakeholders to give up feature-request authority takes courage from the leaders who have to defend the new model to them.
- **Key #9 (Continuous Evangelization of Outcomes)** — Cagan is explicit: share the learnings *"honestly and openly"*, including failures. Shared honestly = courage.
- **Key #4 (True Product Managers)** — coaching a candidate for 3 months and *"if the person still is not able to do the job that is required, then you need to find that person a different role and find someone capable to replace them"* ([[overcoming-objections|Ch 43]]) is a courage decision.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Confusing courage with bravado.** Loud pronouncements and reorg announcements are not courage. Courage is the specific decisions made when it costs something to make them.
- **"Corporate courage is a personality trait."** It's a *disposition* — situationally-observable, coachable, and lost when the political cost of maintaining it exceeds the leader's tolerance. Track it as a state, not a trait.
- **Assuming board pressure will produce courage.** Boards can accelerate or blunt courage; they rarely create it in leaders who don't have it.
- **"Only the CEO needs it."** Cagan explicitly says *"executives and other senior leaders."* Product leaders' courage matters when they push [[transformation-politics|Dimension 8 (Internal Evangelism)]] — telling the truth about outcomes and failures.

## In your context
_When was the last time a senior leader in your org made a decision that visibly cost them political capital — a divestment, an in-flight project cancellation, a public acknowledgment of a failed transformation attempt? If you can't name one, courage is the missing key, and coaching or replacing the leaders in question is where the transformation should focus first._

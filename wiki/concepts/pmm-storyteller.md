# PMM Fundamental 3 — Storyteller

> **Shape How the World Thinks About Your Product.** The third of Martina Lauchengco's four fundamentals of [[product-marketing|product marketing]] ([[loved|LOVED]] Ch 5). Positioning is the long game; messaging is the short game. Both are hard, and neither comes from a formula.

## Why it matters
Ch 5 opens with the reminder: *"Building a great product isn't enough to succeed if you don't also take the time to position it in the market. Don't make the mistake of assuming the world knows how to think about your product and why it's valuable. You must frame its value. If you don't do it, other market forces will."*

Lauchengco's warning: *"positioning a product well is much harder to do than it looks. It's more than just data, stories, claims, or a positioning statement. It's the collective outcome of everything you do to bring your product to market over time."*

## Positioning vs Messaging — the distinction
Ch 5 pins the two terms down (they get conflated in practice):

- **Positioning** — *"the place your product holds in the minds of customers."* How customers know what you do and how you differ.
- **Messaging** — *"the key things you say to reinforce your positioning, making you credible so people want to learn more."*

**Positioning is the long game. Messaging is the short game.** You hold a market position much longer than any specific message.

## Use formulas as *input*, not *output*
The classic *positioning statement* formula (For [target] who [need], our [product] is a [category] that [benefit], unlike [competitor], we [differentiator]) has *"become the straight jacket of positioning."* Ch 5's problem statement:
- Teams take formula output and *"applied it — as is — all over product materials. They assumed because it's called a positioning statement, they'd checked the box on positioning."*
- Formula-generated messaging is *"derivative, dense, and jargon filled."*
- More importantly: formulas focus teams on *what they want to say* — not on *what customers most need to hear*.

**The formula's real job is to push teams to think through audience, unique value, and reasons to believe.** As *input* to the thinking, useful. As *output* customers see, harmful.

### Worked example — Looker vs RJMetrics (Ch 5)
Ch 5 puts two competitors' messaging side-by-side. Same audience (data analysts), same value proposition, same head-to-head competition:

- **Company A:** *"[Company A] is the best tool for running a data-driven online business. Data-driven decisions lead to better results."*
- **Company B:** *"[Company B] re-invents business intelligence. Our modern data discovery platform takes a markedly different approach to analytics. Because it operates in-database, all your data is inherently drillable and explorable."*

Company A says something everyone knows to be true — *"could be talking about Microsoft Excel."* It doesn't help a data analyst understand *how* better results are achieved. Company B is longer + more concrete: names what's different (*"markedly different approach"*), what the technique is (*"operates in-database"*), and what an analyst can do differently (*"data is drillable and explorable"*). *"Even if you don't know what this means from a technical perspective, you do know what they're claiming is unique."*

Outcome: Company B was **Looker** — acquired by Google for $2.6B after 7 years. Company A was **RJMetrics** — *"average"* outcome despite a 4-year head start.

Lauchengco's takeaway: *"Messaging isn't what makes a product good. But a good product can't succeed in the market without good messaging. It won't come from a formula; it comes from knowing what your audience needs to hear."*

## A Better Process — CAST
Ch 5 offers **CAST** as a check for whether messaging is grounded in what customers need to hear — *not* a formula, but a guide:

| Letter | Question |
|---|---|
| **C**lear | Is what you do clear and is there a reason to be curious? Is being comprehensive getting in the way of clarity? |
| **A**uthentic | Is the language evocative and meaningful to your customer? Is it said in a way that makes them feel known? |
| **S**imple | Is it easy to understand what's compelling or different? Will customers know what's better? |
| **T**ested | Has it been tested and iterated in the context customers will experience it? |

**Test messaging in context, not documents.** *"Teams often iterate messaging in documents, assuming it's honed because product, sales, and marketing all got input. That is only a starting point. When messaging is tested in a web page or email, not only do you get better customer input, it's easier to see unnecessary or confusing phrasing."*

**Simple ≠ jargony-and-promotional.** Ch 5's warning even for experienced PMMs: *"Imagine if Looker's 'Because it operates in-database, all your data is inherently drillable and explorable' was 'Because it's a collaborative data platform, there are no limits to what you can explore!' The original doesn't have trendy buzzwords, but it is much clearer for a data analyst."* Trendy buzzwords ≠ compelling.

## The Tendency to Be Overly Precise
Especially in **technical or developer** products: *"one of the biggest challenges in technical product marketing is being technical enough at the right times versus technically precise all the time."* The Ambassador judgment kicks in — *what does the target market most need to hear?*

Messaging should be concrete enough to be credible to the technically savvy — but the job of messaging is to *create a connection*, not to say everything with technical precision from the start. *"This may mean messaging works best when accompanied by a product trial, videos, or customer testimonials. Don't expect messaging to do all the heavy lifting."*

## SEO — an input, not the driver
Ch 5's SEO discipline:
- ~70% of buying decisions happen online in some form — the keyword-phrase ecosystem around you and competitors matters.
- SEO can anchor much of digital strategy (content, ad buys, email subject lines).
- **Keyword audits** show what words others associate with your product.
- **Journey tests** watching users search for you show *how they think about your product, the language they use, and which competitors they associate with you.*

**Beware the trap:** *"Beware the temptation to chase keyword phrases that get clicks. It can be at odds with your longer-term positioning."* SEO is input to positioning; not the driver of it.

## Positioning = Your Actions + Others'
Ch 5's most important structural insight: messaging is only the most visible artifact of positioning. **Every activity in a product go-to-market plan reinforces positioning in some way** — proof-of-concept criteria, demo emphasis, press-release triggers, analyst-review evidence. That's the positioning work *within* your control.

But *"an equal if not more powerful force influencing a product's perception is the 70% of decision-making that happens beyond a company's control."* Ch 5 names this the **dark funnel / dark social**: engagement, buying processes, sharing of content and POVs that impact adoption but can't be seen or tracked.

*"Comparison sites, reviews, ratings, social postings, shares, online forums, other people's content, and even employee buzz are just a search away. They collectively form a digital footprint that has enormous impact and quietly positions a product and the brand reputation of a company. Pay attention to the power of word-of-mouth and what people are saying. It can become your de facto positioning, even if your messaging and 'official' marketing channels say something else."*

## The Long Game
*"Positioning happens slowly over time through all marketing actions. Persistence and consistency are key. You hold a market position much longer than any messaging. Its likewise much more challenging to change, so make sure to do this with intention from the start."*

This is why Fundamental 4 — [[pmm-evangelist|Evangelism]] — is essential. *"Intentional and accidental evangelists can create either lift or drag on all of this work, and they are the best way to scale go-to-market efforts."*

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **The positioning-statement straitjacket.** Formula generators become the finished messaging; teams check the box and stop. Fix: use formulas as input, iterate to real messaging via CAST + testing.
- **Formula messaging.** Derivative, dense, jargon-filled. Focus on *what customers need to hear*, not what the team wants to say.
- **"Data-driven decisions lead to better results"** (or its category equivalent). Says nothing — could be describing any product.
- **Trendy buzzwords substituting for clarity.** Ch 5's Looker warning: *"collaborative data platform, there are no limits to what you can explore"* sounds modern; the concrete original is much clearer.
- **Testing messaging only in documents.** Real signal comes from context (web pages, emails, sales conversations).
- **Chasing keyword clicks over positioning.** Short-term SEO wins can undermine long-term positioning.
- **Ignoring the dark funnel.** Believing "official" marketing is what positions the product. It isn't — 70% of decision-making happens beyond the company's control.
- **Positioning as one-shot.** *"Persistence and consistency are key."* Positioning is earned across every marketing action, over time.

## In your context
_Agent prompt: does your messaging pass CAST — Clear · Authentic · Simple · Tested — for its intended audience? Would you rate it Looker or RJMetrics? What does your dark-funnel search / reviews / forums say your product actually is, versus what your official messaging says?_

# Insights

> What you learn about customers, data, technology, and the industry that tells you **where the leverage is** — the raw material [[product-strategy|product strategy]] is built from. Distinct from the principle [[powered-by-insights]] (the *belief* that strategy should be insight-driven): this page is about what insights *are* and where they come from.

## Why it matters
You can't choose the most important problems to solve without understanding where the leverage actually lies — and most organizations default to opinion, precedent, or the loudest voice in the room instead. Naming insights as a first-class input, with named sources, turns "we should be data-driven" from a slogan into something a coach can actually ask about.

## How it works — the four sources
[[2020-02-19-cagan-product-strategy-insights|Cagan]] (Pillar 2 of the strategy four-pillar series) names four sources of insight, deliberately broader than "just data":

1. **Quantitative** — analytics, experiment results, funnel data. Tells you *what* and *how much*.
2. **Qualitative** — customer interviews, support tickets, sales conversations. Tells you *why*. Split further into two kinds that get conflated:
   - **Evaluative** — reacting to something already built or prototyped (most discovery insight is this kind).
   - **Generative** — surfacing something nobody had framed yet; needs explicit room to emerge, since teams under delivery pressure default to evaluative-only work.
3. **Technology** — new enabling technology that makes a previously-impossible solution possible (the same lens behind [[empowered-engineers|empowered engineers]] and [[2020-05-20-cagan-customer-inspired-technology-enabled|customer-inspired, technology-enabled]]).
4. **Industry** — competitive landscape, market trends, regulatory shifts.

"Insights can come from anywhere," but a leader who can't name which of the four sources backs a given strategic bet is very likely working from opinion instead.

## Insights must be shared, not just discovered
[[2020-02-19-cagan-product-strategy-insights|Cagan's]] sharper point: an insight that lives inside one team's head doesn't power *strategy* — strategy is a leadership-level act, and insights only become strategic when they're **actively circulated** (a head of product sharing the quarter's top learnings in an all-hands, not waiting for someone to ask). This is the discipline [[product-leadership|product leadership]] owns, distinct from any one team's discovery work.

## Where insights come from in practice
- **[[product-discovery|Discovery]] is the main generator.** Weekly customer contact, experiments, and prototypes surface qualitative and quantitative insight as a side effect of testing ideas — see [[continuous-discovery]].
- **Teams feed strategy, not just the reverse.** [[2022-09-23-moore-changing-how-you-decide-which-problems-to-solve|Moore]]: many of the best insights come *up* from teams doing the work, not down from a strategy offsite — a bottom-up channel leaders must deliberately keep open.
- **Case-study examples** the wiki already grounds: [[amazon-prime|Amazon's]] Charlie Ward connecting an enabling capability (subscription + shipping) to a business opportunity is a technology insight; [[spotify-discover-weekly|Spotify's]] ML engineers spotting the Discover Weekly opportunity is the same pattern.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **"Insights" reduced to "the dashboard."** Quantitative data is one of four sources, not the whole thing — and even quantitative work splits into "what happened" (analytics) vs. "would this work" (experiments).
- **Evaluative-only qualitative work.** Only ever testing what's already been built starves generative insight — new opportunities stop surfacing.
- **Insight hoarding.** A team learns something important and it never reaches the people setting strategy; the org keeps re-litigating decisions the data already answered.
- **"No paint-by-numbers" trap.** Cagan explicitly warns against expecting a fill-in-the-blanks insights template — gathering and synthesizing insight is a craft, not a form.
- **Consultancies as an insight shortcut.** Cagan's caveat: outside consultancies are usually weak at product strategy specifically because they lack the day-to-day depth of context that generates real product insight — they're better at business strategy.

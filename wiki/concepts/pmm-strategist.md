# PMM Fundamental 2 — Strategist

> **Direct Your Product's Go-to-Market.** The second of Martina Lauchengco's four fundamentals of [[product-marketing|product marketing]] ([[loved|LOVED]] Ch 4). Marketing strategies are the **why** behind every market-facing activity; without them, PMM defaults to *"a list of to-dos"*.

## Why it matters
Ch 4 opens with Pocket's follow-up to the 4.0 relaunch — CEO Nate whiteboarding v5.0 features while the CTO asks, *"How do we make all this matter?"*. The team wrote three marketing strategies on the whiteboard — **grow a loyal user base · define and lead the category · leverage partnerships for growth** — and used them to judge each launch idea. A generic online launch would have been fine execution; it wouldn't *define* the category. The strategy-directed answer was **Pocket Matters**, an in-person v5.0 launch event with press, partners, and 10 users at a San Francisco wine bar. Within hours: a flurry of press, downloads, and partner discussions. Within a month: Nate named a Time *30 Under 30 Changing the World*.

Lauchengco's punch line: *"While the event helped with all of their strategies, its purpose was to define and lead the category. This clarity helped them make smart go-to-market decisions with better results."*

## Key terms — Ch 4's disambiguation
The go-to-market vocabulary is used loosely; LOVED Ch 4 pins each term down. The wiki uses this vocabulary going forward:

- **GTM Engine (Lauchengco's term)** — the sum of all marketing + sales machinery. Cross-functional; not associated with any one product.
- **Marketing Strategy** — orchestration of marketing elements in the GTM engine (brand, corp comms, demand gen, promotional). Owned by marketing at the company level; at the individual-product level, driven by PMM to align a product go-to-market plan.
- **Product Go-to-Market** — the path a *particular* product takes to market. PMM's unique purview.
- **Distribution Strategy (also GTM Strategy / GTM Model / Business Model / Adoption Model)** — *"the most confused term."* The chosen *model* to get products into customers' hands. Ch 4's six models — **Direct sales · Inside sales · Channel partners · Direct to professional/customer · Trial or freemium · Product-led growth**. A product's go-to-market can use one or several; companies often layer multiple as they mature. Lauchengco calls these **GTM models**.
- **Channel Strategy (aka Partner Strategy, Marketing Mix)** — either channel-*partners* (a distribution form) or the **marketing channel mix** (PR, events, social, digital paid, content).
- **[[product-strategy|Product Strategy]]** — Cagan's canon: connects business objectives + product vision to team work. In PMM, key elements of the product strategy *drive* the product go-to-market plan, especially timing of tactics.
- **Business Goals / Objectives** — the specific, measurable achievements over a time period; marketing strategies in a product-GTM plan align tightly with these.

## The role of marketing strategies in product go-to-market
*"Much as product marketers can't do their job without customer and market insights, no marketing activities should happen before putting in place the why."*

### Why-then-when-then-what-and-how
Ch 4's discipline: **the why and the when make the what and how worth doing.**
- **Why** = marketing strategies (the guardrails).
- **When** = timing driven by product milestones, customer realities, and market dynamics (e.g. Back-to-School if your target is students).
- **What/how** = the tactics themselves.

*"I see many companies begin their product go-to-market journey thinking of it as a list of to-dos, then asking how they should do them. Take the time to put the strategic why in place first."*

Right-size activities to the company's resources and stage. Pocket's launch event invited **10** customers and partners with deep integrations — not 100 — because that was what a small team could execute well.

### The starter questions
Ch 4's eight questions PMM uses to think through marketing strategies:
1. Is third-party validation important for credibility?
2. What kind of customers are you trying to acquire and how fast?
3. Where do those customers spend time in their professional or personal lives?
4. Are you trying to educate the space?
5. What are the product's strengths?
6. Are there particular trends that present opportunities in your category?
7. Does someone else already have established relationships with the customers you're trying to reach?
8. What is the preferred way to adopt new products or technology for your customers?

### The six strategy themes
*"Remarkably, the strategy building blocks for a product's go-to-market inevitably fall into some variation of these themes"* (Ch 4):

1. **Enable growth to hit a revenue or business goal.**
2. **Improve conversion of specific customers.**
3. **Generate awareness, improve discovery, or build a brand.**
4. **Define, reshape, or lead a category, ecosystem, or platform.**
5. **Engender customer validation, loyalty, or evangelism.**
6. **Find and develop new customer segments, partners, and programs.**

A PMM articulates strategies specific to their product's situation (Lauchengco's examples: *grow healthcare vertical adoption · define product suite for DevOps category*). The tactics — partnerships, channels, branding, pricing, communities — flow from the strategy, not the reverse.

### Strategy as accelerant, not brake
*"Some worry taking the time to be strategic in a product's go-to-market slows things down or makes marketing less dynamic. Done well, it's an accelerant."*

## How company maturity evolves product go-to-market
Ch 4 draws the arc across company stage:
- **Startup, one product** — everything in market *is* the go-to-market puzzle. Rapid experimentation on market dynamics, good customers, product, and best channels — all at once. *"It's why the product go-to-market is your go-to-market strategy at the earliest stages of a company. It's also why product marketing plays such an important role at startups and why I advocate making a product marketer your first marketing hire."*
- **Mature company** — GTM machinery is established and complex. Work is as much internal coordination as external acceleration. Each function (demand gen, brand, sales, CS) has its own strategies + agendas; PMM's role becomes aligning them behind a product's strategy.

This is why an early-stage PMM often *is* the marketing team; a mature-stage PMM directs the GTM engine.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **List-of-to-dos GTM.** *"I see many companies begin their product go-to-market journey thinking of it as a list of to-dos, then asking how they should do them."* Fix: put the strategic why in place first.
- **GTM terms used loosely.** GTM strategy / GTM model / distribution strategy / marketing strategy / channel strategy all get confused. Use Ch 4's vocabulary explicitly.
- **Marketing before strategy.** Cranking out content, campaigns, events without the strategy behind them. The Pocket contrast: a generic online launch vs. the strategy-directed event that defined the category.
- **Right-sizing failures.** Pocket didn't invite 100 partners; they invited 10 with deep integrations. Stage + resources gate what strategies can execute well.
- **Confusing strategy with tactics.** Ch 4 uses examples in Part 3 to show *"how a strategy for one company can be a tactic for another. It all depends on stage and goals."*

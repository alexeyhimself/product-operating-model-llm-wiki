# Empowered Product Teams

> A durable, cross-functional team **given problems to solve (not features to build)** and held accountable for outcomes — "the most fundamental of all product concepts" (Cagan).

## Why it matters
Everything else in the model rests on this unit. Without empowered teams you have a feature factory: teams that receive solutions to build, are measured on output, and own neither the problem nor the result. The four [[principles-of-product-teams|product-team principles]] each describe one facet of an empowered team.

## How it works
A cross-functional team (product manager, product designer, engineers — the [[the-product-team-trio|trio]], plus the full eng team) is assigned a [[team-objectives|team objective]] (a significant problem + measures of success) — via [[okrs|OKRs]], which Cagan calls "first and foremost an *empowerment* technique" ([[2020-02-24-cagan-team-objectives-overview|canon]]). The team owns both [[product-discovery|discovery]] (finding a solution worth building, against the [[the-four-big-risks|four risks]]) and [[product-delivery|delivery]] (building and shipping it), and is judged on the [[outcomes-over-output|outcome]]. It runs on [[collaboration]], not hand-offs, and depends on a culture of [[trust-over-control]].

### The trust-gap diagnosis ([[empowered|EMPOWERED]] Ch 4)
The book's founding question: *why*, given how many books argue for empowered teams, do most companies still not? The CEO answer boils down to **trust** — leaders don't believe they have the level of people needed to truly empower them. Cagan's response is the book's thesis: *"having worked with many people at each of these companies, I point out how ordinary the vast majority of the people on these teams actually are. Maybe the important difference lies elsewhere?"* The subtitle — *Ordinary People, Extraordinary Products* — is that hypothesis: strong product companies **leverage ordinary talent differently**, they don't hire different humans. Backs the "empowerment is not backing off" note further down and connects to [[product-leadership]] (better leadership, not less).

### Two axes (per [[2021-04-27-cagan-product-vs-project-teams|Cagan]])
- **Empowerment** (product vs. feature) — given problems, not features ([[feature-teams-vs-product-teams]]).
- **Ownership / durability** (product vs. project) — durable team (1–2 years minimum), not a pool-allocated project team.

Both must be true. Either deficit produces a feature factory.

### Why this works (per [[2022-09-20-moore-changing-how-you-solve-problems|Moore]])
- Engineers are **the single most important source of innovation** — empowered teams are designed to tap that ([[empowered-engineers]]).
- Discovery shrinks the cycle dramatically: 3–5 iterations in weeks (empowered) vs. 1–2 years (feature) — see [[time-to-money]].
- Only **10–20%** of features on typical roadmaps generate positive ROI; empowered teams pursue outcomes, not feature counts.

### In practice (case study)
The [[spotify-discover-weekly|Spotify Discover Weekly]] case is the canonical illustration of what empowerment actually requires of leaders: co-founder CEO **Daniel Ek was openly skeptical** of the idea and said so — but he **gave the team a problem, not a solution**, and let the work proceed. Empowerment = leaders own the strategy and strategic context (and the hard calls); the team owns discovering the solution, "even if the ideas weren't [the CEO's] own." A concrete marker of the real thing: any empowered Spotify team can roll an experiment to **up to 5% of users without asking permission**.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **Feature team / delivery team:** receives a roadmap of features, measured on shipping them. The opposite of empowered.
- **Project team / "pool model":** engineers reassigned per project; team disbands after launch; no ownership of the outcome ([[2021-04-27-cagan-product-vs-project-teams]]).
- "Empowered" misread as *unaccountable* — empowerment is inseparable from accountability for results.
- Splitting discovery and delivery across different groups, breaking [[sense-of-ownership]].
- **Titles without competencies.** Cagan ([[2023-03-15-cagan-product-model-competencies]]): many adopt the new titles without learning the new competencies — "much more severe than you might imagine."

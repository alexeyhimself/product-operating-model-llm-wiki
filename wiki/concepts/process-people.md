# Process People

> A **third contributor type** — alongside **makers** and **managers** — whose primary job is *the process itself*, not the product and not the makers. Cagan's name (2021) for a rising category that includes Product Owners, Scrum Masters, Black Belts, and (the current example) one common definition of [[product-ops|product ops]]. Process people are not inherently bad — but the *reasons orgs hire them* usually are.

## Why it matters
Cagan's headline observation from [[2021-10-28-cagan-process-people|"Process People"]]: for years the field used Paul Graham's two-category model — **makers** (designers, engineers, and PMs on empowered teams) and **managers** (of makers). Over the last decade a **third category** has grown large enough to name. It matters because the answer to *"how do we scale?"* determines how large it becomes:

- Good companies scale with **leaders/managers** who coach — see [[principles-over-process]].
- Struggling companies scale with **process** — SAFe, stage-gates, "Agile at Scale" — and hire process people to run it.

Every process person is a **choice not to fund another engineer, designer, or PM**. That trade-off is the point of the concept.

## How it works
### The three types
| Type | Responsible for | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Makers** | Designing and building the product | Designers, engineers, PMs on empowered teams; supporting research/analytics |
| **Managers** | **Staffing and coaching** the makers | People managers of PM/design/eng — see [[product-leadership]] |
| **Process people** | *The process itself* — not the makers, not the product | Product Owner (Scrum), Scrum Master, Six Sigma Black Belt, one common definition of [[product-ops|product ops]] |

### Where the PM sits (and why it's tricky)
On a **feature team**, the PM is much more of a *manager* type — stakeholder manager, project manager, roadmap-tallier — closer to a process role. On an **empowered team**, the PM is a **maker** (a less pure maker than an engineer or designer, but a maker). This is why the CSPO pathology hurts: a process role (Product Owner) is asked to cover a product job (PM). See [[product-manager]] and [[feature-teams-vs-product-teams]].

### Why hiring process people usually causes harm
Cagan's four reasons (from [[2021-10-28-cagan-process-people|the source]]):

1. **Never performed the maker or manager role in a strong product company.** Enthusiasm without experience → the wrong people control how teams work → **prime targets for the consultant/vendor industry** that has figured out process people are the best path *into* your company.
2. **Hired as a substitute for weak managers.** If managers won't coach, hiring a process person to "handle training" delegates away the manager's #1 job. *A process person is a very poor substitute for a strong manager.* Fix the manager.
3. **Process becomes religion.** Fanaticism is hard to reason with. (Cagan's aside: process people, by not being makers or managers, tend to have more free time — much of it spent on social media preaching their preferred methodology.)
4. **There is [no one right way](wiki/sources/2018-07-26-cagan-tools-and-processes.md) of doing product.** Standardizing process across a large org **institutionalizes a poor fit for most teams** — see [[2018-07-26-cagan-tools-and-processes|Tools and Processes]] for the mechanism (every tool/process embeds a belief system).

### Regional patterns Cagan sees
- **Europe** — the **Product Owner** problem (CSPO pathology; process role covering PM job).
- **US** — the **product ops** problem (a whole team providing process, tools, and methods for the product org).

Both stem from the same misguided focus on *process* as the way to scale.

### How process people map to the product-ops six models
The six models Cagan catalogues in [[2021-12-20-cagan-product-ops-overview|Product Ops Overview]] can be read as *"which flavor of process-people problem does your org have?"* — see [[product-ops]] for the full table.

- Models **#1 (Reincarnated PMO)**, **#2 (Two-in-a-Box PM)**, and **#3 (Delegated Product Leader)** — process-people patterns.
- Model **#6 (Force Multiplier)** — the escape hatch: staffed with your *best experienced makers/managers* in a support role. **A force multiplier is not a process person** — they are a maker/manager whose job is to raise the bar.

## Anti-patterns & misunderstandings
- **"Process is bad."** Not the claim. Cagan quotes Bezos: *"Good process serves you so you can serve customers. But if you're not watchful, the process can become the thing."* The concept is about being watchful, not abolitionist.
- **"Every Scrum Master / PO is a process person."** The *role definition* is a process role. Whether it does net harm depends on why the org hired the person and what the manager is (or isn't) doing.
- **"Product ops = process people."** Only in the harmful models — see [[product-ops]]. The [[product-ops#the-force-multiplier-model-the-one-cagan-endorses|force multiplier]] model is *not* a process-people pattern.
- **Confusing this with the "20 first principles."** Process people are a phenomenon Cagan describes; the *response* to them is the first principle [[principles-over-process]] (scale with leaders/coaching, not process). Don't conflate.
- **Treating "process person" as an insult.** Cagan explicitly says the vast majority have **good intentions**. The concept is a diagnostic on **how the org chose to scale** and **which manager isn't doing their job** — not a slur on the individuals filling the role.

## In your context
_Field note placeholder — count your makers, managers, and process people. What is each process person a substitute for? If a manager took ownership of what that person is doing, would the process person still be needed? Where a process person is genuinely a force multiplier ([[product-ops|product-ops model #6]]), name it as such and staff it with your strongest people — otherwise, the money is better spent on another maker._

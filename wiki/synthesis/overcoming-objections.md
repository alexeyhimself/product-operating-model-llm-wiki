# Overcoming Objections to the Product Model

> The single page an agent should reach for when the coaching moment is: "our <CEO / head of sales / CFO / PMO director / CIO / VP customer success / stakeholder> won't accept this." Cagan devotes an entire Part of *[[transformed|TRANSFORMED]]* (Part X, Chs 36–46) to it — because most objections aren't change-resistance in disguise, they're **legitimate concerns from people who don't yet see how the new model addresses them**. This page is the wiki's hub of those objection patterns and the SVPG responses, grouped by constituency.

## The framing (why this page exists)

Cagan's premise ([[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Ch 36 preface): *"With any sizable group of people, there will always be objections… some people resist change just because they don't like change… but the more difficult case is that people often have very legitimate concerns, and they simply don't see how those concerns would be addressed in this new model."* This wiki treats objections as **the transformation politics surface** — where [[transformation-politics|the political work]] actually happens. Dismissing them as "resistance" is the reliable path to a stalled transformation.

## Cross-cutting themes across all 11 chapters

Reading Part X in one sitting surfaces a small set of moves Cagan uses *repeatedly*. If an agent internalizes these, most novel objections can be answered by analogy.

1. **"Concerns are usually legitimate, and often about scope, not about the model."** Almost every chapter opens by validating the objection before answering it. The stance is *"you're right that X matters — here's how the model handles it"*, never *"you're resisting change."*
2. **Recast the *solution* the objector proposes as a *problem* they want solved.** "Give us a roadmap" → "you need to know how our work ties to business outcomes; the vision + strategy delivers that better than a roadmap." "Give us a date" → "you need [[high-integrity-commitments|high-integrity commitments]] for the small set of things that truly need dates."
3. **Escalate from tactics to architecture only when tactics won't hold.** Ch 40's "our tools are terrible" is answered *tactically* (staff those tools as product). Ch 44's "I'm a CIO" is answered *architecturally* (cost-center → profit-center). Match the depth of the fix to the depth of the objection.
4. **Point at the systemic *cause* of the recurring symptom.** Ch 36 releases-that-hurt: hurt *because* they're infrequent. Ch 37 specials that keep arriving: because you lack **reference customers**. Ch 39 duplicate BA/PO orgs: because IT never had product managers. Nine of eleven chapters do this — the pattern is *fix the system, not the symptom*.
5. **Reference customers, insights, and the product vision are the three most-cited answers across the whole Part.** They resolve, respectively, sales objections about "features to close," strategy objections from marketing/CEO/board, and "give us the roadmap" objections from customers, board, LOB, and stakeholders.
6. **Coaching is the fallback when a specific person resists.** Explicit in Chs 37 (Sales — "charm school"), 40 (CS — coaching product leaders to hear signal), 43 (HR — three months to competence), 44 (CIO — expand into CTO), 46 (Inside Product — tech leads, engineers, disagreements). Coaching is the transformation's principal tool for changing people rather than replacing them.
7. **Some objections resolve into "the person can't make the transition."** Chs 43 (HR retitle-only fails), 44 (CIOs who don't want to change), 45 (PMOs who can't shift to delivery management), 46 (engineers who "just want to build" are welcome — but not as tech leads). The model is honest about this rather than optimistic-by-default.

## Per-constituency detail

Each section: the representative objection patterns (paraphrased teaching examples, not reproduced verbatim), then Cagan's response in the wiki's voice, then cross-links.

### 1. Customers — Ch 36
Related partnering canon: [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Ch 21 (Partnering with Customers) informs this chapter directly.

**Patterns.** "Commit to this specific capability or we won't buy." · "When can I count on this feature?" · "Show me your roadmap — we're betting on your company." · "Your releases hurt — do them every 3/6/12 months." · "Every new release needs documentation and training." · "Why should we let you collect usage data?" · "We were part of a customer discovery program — did we do something wrong when it didn't ship?"

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **Commercial product vs custom solutions provider.** Customers often express desire *as a solution*; product's job is to find the underlying problem. A true market-of-one belongs at a custom solutions firm. When the customer *is* in the target market, engage directly to understand what "successful" looks like — commit to solving the problem, not to shipping the requested feature verbatim.
- **When a date really is needed → [[high-integrity-commitments]]**, not the default. The point is that HICs have an **opportunity cost**; keep them for cases with true inherent deadlines.
- **Product roadmap vs [[product-vision|product vision]].** "It is actually the product vision that directly addresses this very real customer need." Prefer sharing the vision; when a roadmap is politically required, keep an internal one and an external customer-facing one.
- **Releases hurt because they're infrequent.** "The root cause of these objections is actually due to releasing too infrequently." Continuous delivery + release-dark + feature-flagging removes the customer disruption. Point to browsers, phones, cars, washing machines already working this way.
- **Instrumentation ≠ surveillance.** Data is anonymized + aggregated, and is used to make sure the product is working *for* the customer. Pilot-and-instrument analogy is Cagan's own.
- **Discovery programs sometimes don't ship.** Rare, but honest: sometimes the range of customers can't be served by a single solution, and the true fit is a custom development firm.

**See also.** [[stakeholder-relationship-health]] (the trust discipline), [[outcome-based-roadmap]] (the transitional artifact), [[product-vision]] (why it's the answer here), [[high-integrity-commitments]] (the exception path).

### 2. Sales — Ch 37
Related partnering canon: [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Ch 21 (Partnering with Customers) informs Ch 37 (per the book's own header note).

**Patterns.** "We're on the front lines — we should tell you what to build." · "The vision is years away — I need to sell now." · "Give me a special to close this deal." · "We lost a deal because we lack feature X." · "How do I compete against this specific competitor?" · "Don't send product people to my customers." · "Coordinate customer visits — don't overwhelm them."

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **The foundational asymmetry.** "The customer and the salesperson do not know what is technically possible. They are often experts in their own domain, but rarely are they experts in the enabling technologies used to build the solutions they buy." Strong product solutions come from combining real customer needs with **what's just now possible** — which requires the product team.
- **Selling the current product now while investing in the vision.** Reference customers who love the current offering are the sales team's true asset in the meantime.
- **Specials are the greatest fear** of product teams. Cagan's process: (1) route special requests through the head of sales first (who blocks most); (2) surviving requests go to the PM, who identifies whether the prospect is in target market and finds the underlying problem; (3) either the solution serves other current/future customers too, or you decline in the name of **[[focus|focus]]**.
- **"We lost a deal for lack of feature X" — treat with skepticism.** Customers often say one thing and do another; a "missing feature" is a convenient excuse. **Reference customers** are the acid test.
- **When sales explicitly asks for competitive help.** PM + [[product-marketing]] co-work: are there segments where we're superior? Can we quickly develop reference customers in that segment?
- **Product-team access to customers is a nonnegotiable.** "One of the few truly nonnegotiables in the product model." When account-control ethos is strong, add "customer interaction training" (aka "charm school") before visits — not access-blocking, coaching. If necessary, the CEO makes this point to sales leadership directly.
- **[[product-ops|Product ops / user research]] coordinates visits** so multiple teams don't over-tap the same customers and so account managers stay in the loop.

**See also.** [[product-marketing]] (the PM's closest cross-functional partner), [[product-ops]] (the visit-coordination function), [[focus]] (why specials get declined), [[missionaries-vs-mercenaries]] (why "salespeople know best" is a symptom of a feature-team world).

### 3. CEO and Board — Ch 38
Related partnering canon: [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Ch 26 (Partnering with Executives) informs Ch 38.

**Patterns.** "As CEO, I meet everyone — why shouldn't I decide?" · "I'm accountable for revenue — I should own the roadmap." · "I need to know what will ship, when." · "The board wants a roadmap." · "I have a product idea — how do I share it?" · "What's a reasonable time frame? How do I know if a team is struggling?" · "Alignment across product/sales/marketing/service is my job." · "Investors want more spend on sales than product."

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **Startup CEO-as-head-of-product doesn't scale.** *In startups, the CEO (or a cofounder) is often also the head of product — for good reason. But innovation at scale depends on empowered teams because customers, investors, board members, analysts, and executives don't know what's just now possible.* Companies that never transform because the CEO won't let go stay small (or stall). Related: [[founder-style-leadership]].
- **What the CEO actually needs is context, not a roadmap.** Product leaders should be able to say *which product work* is expected to move *which business outcomes* in *what time frame* — that's the product strategy in written form. When a date is truly required, use [[high-integrity-commitments]]. When the CEO is on a business trip, the team should have enough **strategic context** to decide well without them ([[product-leadership]]'s core job).
- **The board doesn't want a roadmap.** They want to see how the work being funded ties to the hoped-for results. **Product leaders connect the dots.** Before the board meeting, align the CEO, CFO, and CRO on the storyline. If the org lacks a track record, expect a period of earning trust.
- **CEO ideas are welcome — but be explicit whether they're suggestions or edicts.** *"CEOs are often surprised to learn that what they intend as suggestions are often interpreted as edicts."* Cagan's Steve-Jobs pointer: the idea is the easy part; the real work — 90% of it, Jobs said — is validating and turning it into a product.
- **How to know if a team is struggling.** The **written product strategy** already says what's expected and when. Quarterly business reviews with teams, or deep-dives by product leaders each quarter with a report on what's being done to help.
- **Alignment across product / marketing / sales / service / operations = OKR-tool territory** ([[okrs]]). New product launches especially — everyone must do their part.
- **"Board says invest more in sales, less in product."** Correct if [[product-market-fit|product-market fit]] is truly established; often wrong because companies assume PMF from headcount. KPIs to disambiguate: sales cycle length, cost to sell, trial-to-purchase conversion, churn. Until PMF is real, protect product spend.

**See also.** [[product-leadership]] (their responsibility to connect the dots), [[product-strategy]] (the written form), [[founder-style-leadership]] (Cagan's reframe of "founder mode"), [[okrs]] (the alignment mechanism), [[transformation-politics]] Dimension 8 (the drumbeat of outcomes).

### 4. Line of Business — Ch 39
Book context: LOB managers are stakeholders with P&L responsibility for a whole business unit (category manager, general manager, etc.) — many issues overlap with Ch 38.

**Patterns.** "I have P&L responsibility — why don't I control engineering?" · "Leadership expects me to know everything about my business — I can't be surprised." · "The team wants to repeat problem discovery I've already done." · "We need to move faster — how do I share urgency?" · "The team hides early prototypes from us." · "I want my own product managers reporting to me." · "I run the business — I should set the product strategy."

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **LOB manager = startup CEO of that unit.** Just as a startup CEO leans on product and tech leaders, the LOB manager should. Strategic decisions (vision, strategy, team objectives) are **collaborative**; day-to-day resource control belongs to product leaders.
- **The information-flow contract runs both ways.** LOB → product: share as much strategic context as possible (constraints, business priorities). Product → LOB: share emerging insights, especially newly-emerging learnings from the front lines. PM connects each product effort to the intended business impact.
- **The re-doing-problem-discovery frustration is real — the fix is *inclusion, not exclusion*.** If the LOB had brought PM + designer into customer interactions from the start, they'd have learned the problem space *together*. Going forward: sit with the team, offer everything you can, and remind them that customers buy solutions, so they need time for *solution* discovery.
- **Speed reframe: time-to-market → [[time-to-money]] (= [[outcomes-over-output]]).** Discovery is fast *because* it's cheap: teams should be able to test ideas in ~1/10th the time it would take to build.
- **Prototypes: teach LOB to look past visual limitations.** Teams hide early prototypes when they think LOB will nitpick fonts. When LOB gives thoughtful direction-level feedback and surfaces constraints, teams welcome early input.
- **No duplicate PM orgs.** IT era orgs often stood up "business-side PMs" because IT lacked the role. In the product model, that's a trap — you want a **single capable and accountable PM per team**. PMs can dotted-line to LOB with the right leaders, but should report to product leaders who **know how to coach PMs**. Few LOB managers have the time or experience.
- **LOB writes strategy? Collaborate.** Product strategy looks *holistically across* business units to maximize whole-business value; product leaders work with each LOB leader on the best opportunities across the company, not just within each unit. Requires trust + transparency.

**See also.** [[stakeholder-relationship-health]], [[product-strategy]] (whole-business scope), [[time-to-money]], [[team-topology]] (why LOB carve-ups often break the model), [[product-manager]] (reporting line coaching).

### 5. Customer Success — Ch 40

**Patterns.** "Teams don't care about what we need." · "Our tools are terrible." · "We can't keep track of what's changed — how do we help customers?" · "Products take too long — we built our own solutions."

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **First — if a team truly doesn't care, escalate to product leadership.** Not a coaching moment; a leadership problem.
- **More often, teams *do* care but can't hear the signal.** They're pressured from every direction: customers, sales, marketing, ops, senior leadership. The remedy is a **single, live top-10 list** of customer pains from CS — subjective judgment is fine. The list does a service to teams: *"if it's on this list, it's serious; they don't have to weigh it against everything else."* There will *always* be a top 10; the point is that teams are always working to remove items from it.
- **Sometimes the fix looks different than the requester imagined.** The "obvious fix" often has undesirable consequences; teams take a different approach. That's normal — as long as the problem gets solved.
- **CS-facing tools *are* product.** If the customer experience is impacted, it's product — even if the customer never sees the tool. Non-customer-facing product often gets under-staffed; CS may need to be **vocal** about it.
- **"We can't keep track of changes."** Continuous delivery makes tracking harder. Product teams → [[product-marketing]] → CS/sales/customers — whenever anything becomes customer-visible or requires behavior change. Cagan cites *LOVED* here for the techniques.
- **CS-built solutions "simply never end well."** Duct-taped mixes of supported product + unsupported CS custom code fail when a new release lands. The right fix is to improve the product org.

**See also.** [[product-marketing]] (the change-communication owner), [[product-manager]] (top-10 as a de-noising discipline), [[team-topology]] (why customer-enabling and platform teams get under-invested), [[data-product-management]] (the "is it a product?" litmus test).

### 6. Marketing — Ch 41
Book scope: covers **all** flavors of marketing — product marketing, field, corporate, brand. Ch 41 references Ch 23 (Partnering with Product Marketing) as the deeper reference.

**Patterns.** "We interact with sales, watch competitors, do focus groups, know the analysts — who better to define products?" · "How do we best help teams?" · "We want to pre-announce a future state — when is that okay?"

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **The Steve Jobs pointer.** *"You can conduct 100 focus groups; you'll never get an iPhone."* Marketing, sales, customers, analysts don't know **what's now possible**. Two decades ago, business software was product-managed this way — and innovation was rare. That's why we moved on.
- **Marketing's role: feed insights in.** Product marketing works hard to get any potentially valuable market/competitive/analyst signal into PM and product-leader heads. In some cases a PMM is even **embedded in a product team**.
- **PMM sits at the market side of product-market fit.** Sales enablement, messaging, positioning, evangelism, growth. If the theory is *"fix the product to fix the problem,"* PMM's job is to get the relevant info to the PMs who own the product change.
- **Pre-announcing future state — sometimes valid, always risky.** *"You have to be careful because it's very easy to cannibalize your own sales, or create overwhelming pressure on product teams before your new products are ready, or even truly discovered."* The rule: don't do it before you have evidence you'll be correct. Coordinate head of product + head of product marketing + CMO.

**See also.** [[product-marketing]], [[loved]], [[the-four-big-risks]] (usability/viability), [[product-strategy]] (where the "next big thing" belongs).

### 7. Finance — Ch 42
Related partnering canon: [[transformed|TRANSFORMED]] Ch 24 (Partnering with Finance) informs Ch 42.

**Patterns.** "Outsource for flexibility — spend up/down with the business." · "Outsourced engineers are cheaper per head." · "Fund by project with a business case per year." · "Hold teams accountable to the business case they promised." · "Move resources between efforts as needs change." · "We need consistent quarterly progress to keep funding." · "How do we account for discovery — R&D or design stage?"

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **Per-engineer cost is the wrong denominator.** Look at **per-product-team cost against business outcomes**. A smaller team of employees usually outperforms a significantly larger team of outsourced staff — because of role and working relationship, not people-quality. And *"most outsourcing firms refuse to even sign up for outcomes."*
- **Learning value is destroyed by rotation.** *"There are two sources of value once you build something. There is the thing that you built, and there is the learning that accrues from building that thing."* Constantly-changing order-taker staff destroys the second source. Temporary staff for a specific integration or burst (e.g. test automation) is fine.
- **Team stability compounds.** *"Normally it is better for you to expand the charter of each product team so that you can bring work to the relevant teams rather than move employees to the new work."*
- **The business-case fallacy.** *"You've probably figured out that the business cases you've historically been provided have been far from reliable in predicting returns. In fact, very few finance organizations even try to hold their people accountable to what they promised."* Product teams "learn to know what you can't know" — the same way venture capitalists handle uncertainty: seed funding for discovery, then invest more where there's traction.
- **Fund teams, not projects.** Hold them to **product outcomes**. Each team gets one or more key problems + measures of success + a stance on how conservative/aggressive to be. Accountability is calibrated to the **certainty** requested. Product **leaders** are accountable for the strategy-level result — placing a series of bets that succeed or fail in aggregate. Failed commitments trigger a post-mortem, not blame.
- **Moving resources between efforts is inefficient**, not agile — ramp-up time on tech, customers, and relationships dominates. Broad-chartered stable teams beat resource-shuffling.
- **Consistent quarterly-progress instinct doesn't match the curve.** Experienced product leaders manage teams as **a series of bets on specific tech, people, skills, and data**, adjusted each quarter.
- **Discovery is not R&D.** It's the **definition and design** phase of development — fleshing out what will be built in the coming weeks. True research is done outside discovery.

**See also.** [[time-to-money]], [[outcomes-over-output]], [[team-objectives]] ([[okrs|OKRs the SVPG way]] — targets from the team, ambition levels), [[high-integrity-commitments]], [[transformation]] (fund-teams-for-quarters as the small change with largest leverage).

### 8. HR / People Ops — Ch 43

**Patterns.** "Job classifications are disruptive to change." · "Can't we just retitle current people and send them to training?" · "We already have annual reviews — why add coaching plans?" · "We have onboarding — why add product-specific onboarding?" · "How do we budget raises/promotions if managers push for them?" · "We want individual OKRs for everyone."

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **Retitle-only is the #1 way transformations fail.** *"We have never seen a company succeed when it insists on leveraging the current people and job classifications."* The differences look minor from the outside but aren't.
- **Not everyone will *want* the new PM role.** It's *"very demanding, especially compared to the product owner or business analyst role."* If managers can coach and people want to learn, try first: **a capable manager can coach a reasonable PM candidate to competence in ~3 months**. If not by then, replace the person and re-hire.
- **Assessments + coaching plans ≠ annual review.** They're the **primary responsibility of first-level managers** and constitute the manager's major job. Yes it's expensive — but *"not nearly as expensive as having all this staff who don't know how to succeed in their jobs."* Companies with strong coaching investment have higher retention and can hire directly from universities at lower cost.
- **Product-specific onboarding is different from company onboarding.** Both matter.
- **Manager incentives to promote their people.** In the product model, employees taking on tougher problems generate outsized value — a larger population of promotion-ready people is a good problem to have. Managers get people ready; the company still has to fund role/salary availability.
- **Individual OKRs kill product teams.** *"Members of product teams — especially product managers, product designers, and engineers — need to be incentivized to work together as a product team, collaboratively, on the very same problems with the very same measures of success. One of the main ways you do that is to assign OKRs to the product team rather than to the individual members."* Personal goals belong in coaching plans, not OKRs.

**See also.** [[pm-competency]] (the assessment tool), [[coaching-the-pm]] (the plan tool), [[hiring-and-onboarding]] (first-hire timing; potential over pedigree), [[okrs]] (team-level OKRs), [[product-coach]].

### 9. CIO — Ch 44

**Patterns.** "Tech serves the business — where does this leave me?" · "I'm a CIO who wants to lead the move to the product model — how do I become a CTO?"

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **The mindset shift is *cost center → profit center*.** SVPG has seen many CIOs expand into this broader role (sometimes as a combined CTO/CIO); *"in fact, in several cases, they were ones who drove the move to the product model."*
- **The model isn't only about customer-facing tech.** Substantial engineering work sits behind the scenes — platform services, customer-enabling tools, internal tools.
- **The path for CIOs who *don't* want to change** — stay with "true IT" (vendor management, systems that run the business) while someone else leads product engineering.
- **True IT vs product engineering — real differences.** Product engineering is primarily **build**; true IT is primarily **buy/integrate**. Scalability requirements differ (IT: hundreds–thousands of users; product engineering: 1–3 orders of magnitude larger). Different job descriptions and pay scales follow. And critically: **outsourcing is encouraged in true IT** (not a core competency); **outsourcing is fatal to innovation in product engineering**.
- **Where to learn.** Product + engineering leadership coaches; CTO bootcamps.

**See also.** [[product-leadership]] (technology-as-profit-center is one of Cagan's deepest transformation blockers), [[it-to-product-organization]] (Cagan's 10-step framework), [[transformation]] (the ten keys — Key #2 is "The Role of Technology"), [[engineers]] (why "outsourcing product engineering is fatal to innovation").

### 10. PMO — Ch 45
Book scope: PMO here = **program management office** — project management with senior-level visibility.

**Patterns.** "Many marquee product-model companies still have a program management function — what's their role?" · "I run predictability — what happens to me?" · "If not program management, what are we supposed to do?"

**Cagan's SVPG response.**
- **The role is fine; the *culture* around it is the problem.** Large complex products (especially ecosystem products) genuinely have many moving parts and dependencies — project management matters. *"The issue is not with the role; it is with the culture behind how the company provides that role."*
- **PMO-as-command-and-control is the pattern to break.** *"The problem comes when the PMO, under the guise of reporting on status, exists to instill and govern a command-and-control culture."*
- **Convert to delivery management** — servant-based, impediment-removal, empowering rather than disempowering. Delivery managers are *"very valuable to the organization"* when they truly embrace the difference.
- **This is one of the harder situations in transformation.** In most cases it's a job-doing issue with correctable technique. In the PMO case, it's a **cultural and process view often incompatible with the product model** — some make the shift, some don't, some leave.
- **Watch out for people biding time until the old model returns.** They'll surface first when a project-mode reflex is available.

**See also.** [[delivery-manager]] (the target role Cagan rebranded from "project manager"), [[high-integrity-commitments]] (delivery managers track these), [[transformation-as-a-project]] (the named anti-pattern the PMO instinct produces), [[principles-over-process]].

### 11. Inside Product — Ch 46
The largest chapter in Part X — objections from within product management, product design, engineering, product ops, and product leadership. Treated separately because the *model* has to answer its own team's frictions honestly.

**Patterns and Cagan's responses.**
- **"How can we own outcomes we don't control?"** Focus KRs on product outcomes largely in the team's control, but keep some real responsibility for product success. The whole point of OKRs was *"to encourage product teams to get out of the office and figure out what needs to change"* — is the issue in marketing, sales, sales tools, or the product? *"Who in the entire company has more of an ability to impact the products than the product teams?"*
- **"Quality releases are expensive — how can we go faster?"** *"If it hurts, do it more often."* Monthly hurts; weekly or daily forces investment in test + release automation and removes the pain. Cites *Accelerate* for the theory.
- **"We're in a regulated industry — we can't test with customers / release this often."** *"It is remarkable how many regulated companies think they operate under constraints the actual regulations don't describe."* Read the regulations; consult legal and compliance about what actually applies. Customers routinely sign agreements acknowledging an experimental version. Frequent small releases *are* the responsible way to run mission-critical tech.
- **"We don't have time to test ideas — let stakeholders decide."** *"If this approach worked, you wouldn't need the product model."* Ironically the objection often comes from engineers; in strong product companies engineers are the primary source of innovation and care about *what* they build, not just *how*. Engineering leaders coach tech leads accordingly.
- **"Some engineers just want to be told what to build — room for them?"** Yes — the tech lead must care about *what*, but other engineers who just want to build are fine. Companies operating in the model still weight hiring toward engineers who don't want to be told what to build.
- **"If we test with real users, competitors will find out."** Discovery only ships a fraction of ideas — outside observers can't tell which ones worked. NDAs are available if you're still nervous.
- **"Customer got excited about an idea in discovery that we then didn't ship."** Explain up front that you're learning what the best solution would be. It happens; and the reason you test is precisely to deliver valuable solutions.
- **"We found a bigger opportunity in discovery — can we pivot?"** Raise it to leadership; it may become the next strategy focus. But **empowerment doesn't mean working on whatever you like** — it means finding the best solution to the problem you were asked to solve. Current strategy usually depends on it.
- **"We're being set up to fail — too many stakeholders."** Team objectives (per SVPG) have the team saying **what they think they can accomplish**. Teams normally carry 1–2 problems + KTLO. If it still feels impossible, escalate to the manager.
- **"Everything depends on other teams — this doesn't feel empowered."** Symptom of poor **[[team-topology|team topology]]** and/or tech debt. Fixes: larger teams with broader charters (fewer dependencies), delivery managers to track and resolve, expanded use of platform teams (dependencies become explicit and more manageable — often a step toward re-platforming).
- **"Which decisions are ours to make?"** A weekly 1:1 question. Frame each decision as **risk + consequence** — minimal & reversible ⇒ team decides. Amazon's **one-way vs two-way door** metaphor is the sharp version.
- **"Disagreements inside the team — how to resolve?"** *"First, it's a normal and even healthy sign that the product team has different opinions and feels safe enough to share them."* Defer to expertise where the decision is expertise-specific. If unresolved, **run a quick test — learn the answer together**. Weekly 1:1 with the manager if still stuck.
- **"Without a PMO who owns commitments and dependencies?"** Head of engineering personally approves [[high-integrity-commitments]] because their reputation is on the line. Delivery managers track dependencies and remove impediments.
- **"KTLO is eating a team."** Rules of thumb: **≤30% KTLO on experience teams, ≤50% on platform teams**. Above these, add engineers; if you can't, reduce non-KTLO work knowing morale and impact will fall.
- **"Faster to build in production than prototype."** True sometimes — a win when it is. More often the engineers haven't seen modern prototyping techniques. If the team really is skilled and can throw production code away when the idea doesn't work, that's fine.
- **"PM and designer exclude engineers from discovery."** *"One of the most common problems"* — larger issue than morale: **innovation is lost**. First: engineers speak directly to PM and designer. If that fails, escalate to a product leader.
- **"Team is being asked for dates on everything."** Institute a discipline: dates always follow the [[high-integrity-commitments|HIC]] procedure (tech lead + potentially other engineers, with opportunity cost). Educate the org on when a date is actually needed.
- **"Crushing tech debt."** Senior leaders + executives; recovery plans; specialty firms; typically 1–2 years to get back on track. Painful. Many companies don't survive it.
- **"No time to test risks."** [[time-to-money|Time to money]] beats time to market. Prototypes in hours-to-days beat products in weeks-to-months of build-then-discover-it-was-wrong. Often the first hours of discovery reveal an idea isn't worth building — the biggest savings there is.
- **"Discovery/delivery imbalance."** Temporary a week or two — normal. Sustained — usually a staffing issue: discovery-can't-keep-up → too many engineers; delivery-can't-keep-up (backlog piling up) → too few engineers.
- **"Remote-team struggles."** In-person time ~1×/quarter (duration + frequency matter more than location). Increase 1:1 coaching cadence — twice-weekly 30-min for people struggling.
- **"Engineering leader keeps moving people between teams."** Sometimes a leader who doesn't get durability. More often a **team topology** problem — teams scoped too narrowly. Fix: fewer, larger teams with broader charters; teams themselves route work around, not the leader routing people.
- **"Where do POs and BAs fit?"** *"The short answer is that they don't."* Product owner is a **role, not a job** — covered by the PM. Business analyst responsibilities move partially to PMs and partially to product designers.

## What this page is *not*

- **Not a script to win the argument.** Half of Cagan's answers are "you're right, and here's how the model handles what you're worried about." An agent that treats objections as debate loses the transformation. This is [[transformation-politics]] work, not rhetoric.
- **Not a substitute for [[transformation-readiness]].** If the CEO, product leader, coaching plan, and pilot design aren't in place, "handling objections" won't rescue the transformation. Diagnose readiness first.
- **Not organization-specific.** The wiki is org-agnostic. When an agent is coaching *for* a specific org, it must combine this page with that org's separately-supplied [[strategic-context|strategic-context baseline]] to be useful.
